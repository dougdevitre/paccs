## Platform Strategy

### Facebook

- **Audience:** Parents, advocates, community members, professionals (35-65 age range)
- **Content type:** Longer-form posts, article shares, event promotion, live videos, group discussions
- **Posting frequency:** 4-5 times per week
- **Best practices:** Use images with every post. Longer captions perform well. Engage in comments. Use Facebook Groups for community building.

### Instagram

- **Audience:** Younger parents, advocates, professionals (25-45 age range)
- **Content type:** Infographic carousels, Reels (short video), Stories, quote graphics
- **Posting frequency:** 3-5 feed posts per week; daily Stories
- **Best practices:** Visual-first platform. Use carousels for educational content. Reels for short explainers. Use alt text for accessibility.

### X (Twitter)

- **Audience:** Journalists, policy professionals, researchers, advocates
- **Content type:** Short statements, threads, article shares, data highlights, live commentary on legislative events
- **Posting frequency:** 1-3 times daily
- **Best practices:** Use threads for complex topics. Tag journalists and legislators. Use relevant hashtags sparingly (2-3 per post). Engage with related conversations.

### LinkedIn

- **Audience:** Professionals — attorneys, mental health practitioners, researchers, policymakers
- **Content type:** Professional articles, research summaries, event announcements, thought leadership
- **Posting frequency:** 2-3 times per week
- **Best practices:** Professional tone. Share research findings with commentary. Tag relevant professional organizations. Use document posts for one-pagers.

### TikTok

- **Audience:** Younger adults, general public (18-35 age range)
- **Content type:** 30-60 second educational videos, myth-busting clips, professional commentary
- **Posting frequency:** 3-5 times per week
- **Best practices:** Hook viewers in the first 2 seconds. Use on-screen text. Keep it concise and visually engaging. Trending sounds can increase reach when appropriate.

## 20+ Post Templates with Captions

### Education Posts

**Post 1: Definition**
> Parental alienation is a pattern of behavior where one parent damages a child's relationship with the other parent through denigration, interference, and manipulation. It is not a theory — it is documented in hundreds of peer-reviewed studies.
> #ParentalAlienation #ChildCenteredSafety #PACCS

**Post 2: By the Numbers**
> Research shows that approximately 1 in 100 children experience parental alienation, and more than 1 in 8 parents report experiencing alienating behaviors from a co-parent.
> Sources: Harman et al., 2022; Harman, Leder-Elder, & Biringen, 2019
> #ParentalAlienation #ResearchMatters #ChildProtection

**Post 3: ICD-11 Recognition**
> Did you know? The World Health Organization's ICD-11 includes caregiver-child relationship problems (QE52.0), recognizing the dynamics that underlie parental alienation.
> #ParentalAlienation #WHO #EvidenceBased

**Post 4: Both Parents**
> Parental alienation is not a gendered issue. Research shows that both mothers and fathers can be alienating parents, and both can be targeted parents. This is a child safety issue.
> #ParentalAlienation #ChildSafety #BothParentsMatter

### Impact Posts

**Post 5: Children's Mental Health**
> Children subjected to parental alienation experience elevated rates of anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem. These are documented harms with real consequences for children's lives.
> Source: Verrocchio et al., 2018
> #ParentalAlienation #ChildrensMentalHealth #ProtectChildren

**Post 6: Long-Term Effects**
> The effects of parental alienation do not end in childhood. Adults who experienced alienation as children report higher rates of substance abuse, relationship difficulties, and impaired trust.
> Source: Baker, 2005
> #ParentalAlienation #LongTermHarm #ChildCenteredSafety

**Post 7: Attachment Disruption**
> Every child needs secure attachment to their caregivers. Parental alienation deliberately destroys that attachment — one of the most important protective factors in a child's life.
> #ParentalAlienation #Attachment #EmotionalSafety

**Post 8: Loyalty Conflicts**
> Children caught in alienation are forced into impossible loyalty conflicts. No child should have to choose between parents. No child should be punished for loving both.
> #ParentalAlienation #ChildCenteredSafety #PACCS

### Myth vs. Fact Posts

**Post 9: Myth — "It's been debunked"**
> MYTH: Parental alienation has been debunked.
> FACT: Over 1,000 publications in the scientific literature document parental alienation. It is recognized internationally and supported by peer-reviewed research spanning three decades.
> #MythVsFact #ParentalAlienation #ScienceMatters

**Post 10: Myth — "Children never lie"**
> MYTH: Children never lie about abuse.
> FACT: While children's disclosures should always be taken seriously, research shows that children can be coached, influenced, and confused — especially in high-conflict custody disputes. Proper investigation is essential.
> #MythVsFact #ParentalAlienation #EvidenceBased

**Post 11: Myth — "Just a custody tactic"**
> MYTH: Parental alienation is just a custody tactic used by abusers.
> FACT: Research shows that alienation claims are raised by both mothers and fathers, and false claims exist on both sides. Dismissing all alienation claims harms the children who are genuinely affected.
> #MythVsFact #ParentalAlienation #ChildProtection

**Post 12: Myth — "Forced reunification"**
> MYTH: Reunification programs force children into contact with abusers.
> FACT: Ethical, evidence-based reunification programs include safety screening and are designed to restore children's relationships with safe parents in a structured, professional environment.
> #MythVsFact #ParentalAlienation #Reunification

**Post 13: Pillar 1**

## Engagement Guidelines

### Responding to Comments

- **Supportive comments:** Thank the person. Encourage them to share.
- **Questions:** Provide brief, evidence-based answers. Link to PACCS resources.
- **Personal stories:** Acknowledge with empathy. Do not give advice. Direct to appropriate resources.
- **Skeptical but good-faith comments:** Respond with evidence and respect. Cite sources.
- **Hostile or misinformed comments:** Respond once with facts, then disengage. Do not argue.

### General Rules
- Always maintain a professional, evidence-based tone
- Never disclose private information about any family or child
- Never diagnose, give legal advice, or provide clinical recommendations
- Respond within 24 hours when possible
- Escalate threats or harassment to the PACCS communications lead

---

## Crisis Response for Negative Comments

### Level 1: Misinformed Comment
**Example:** "Parental alienation is debunked junk science."
**Response:** "Thank you for engaging. Parental alienation is documented in over 1,000 peer-reviewed publications and is recognized internationally, including in the WHO's ICD-11. We are happy to share research resources if you are interested."

### Level 2: Accusatory Comment
**Example:** "PACCS helps abusers get custody."
**Response:** "We understand this concern. PACCS advocates for evidence-based assessment by trained professionals, which includes safety screening. We support protecting children from all forms of harm, including abuse and alienation. Our resources are available at [link]."

### Level 3: Harassment or Threats
**Action:** Do not respond publicly. Screenshot and document. Report to platform. Escalate to PACCS communications lead. Block if necessary.

### Level 4: Media Inquiry via Social Media
**Action:** Do not respond in the comments. Direct to the PACCS media contact via direct message. Alert the communications lead.
