# Op-Ed Template: Writing About Parental Alienation

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** Advocates, Professionals, and Writers Submitting to Newspapers and Online Publications
**Format:** Template with Guidance and Sample Op-Eds

---

## Op-Ed Structure

### Target Length
- Print newspapers: 600-800 words
- Online publications: 800-1,200 words
- Check the specific publication's submission guidelines

### Structure Overview

| Section | Purpose | Length |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Hook | Grab attention with a timely, compelling opening | 1-2 sentences |
| Problem | Define the issue and establish its scope | 2-3 paragraphs |
| Evidence | Present research and data supporting your argument | 1-2 paragraphs |
| Solution | Propose specific, actionable solutions | 1-2 paragraphs |
| Call to Action | Tell the reader what to do | 1 paragraph |

---

## Section-by-Section Guidance

### 1. Hook

Open with something timely, surprising, or emotionally resonant. Connect to current events, a new study, legislation, or a commonly held misconception.

**Examples:**
- "A child who is coached to fear a loving parent is a child in psychological danger — yet most family courts are not trained to recognize it."
- "If a child were prevented from seeing a doctor, we would call it medical neglect. When a child is prevented from seeing a fit parent, we often call it a 'custody dispute.'"
- "New research confirms what alienated families have known for years: parental alienation causes lasting psychological harm to children."

### 2. Problem

Define parental alienation in plain language. Establish the scope of the problem with 1-2 key statistics. Explain why the current system fails children.

**Key points to include:**
- A concise definition of parental alienation
- Scope: how many children and families are affected
- The gap between research and practice
- Why the current legal system fails to address alienation

### 3. Evidence

Present 2-3 key research findings. Use plain language and cite the source. Do not overwhelm with data — select the most compelling points.

**Useful statistics:**
- Approximately 1% of children experience parental alienation (Harman et al., 2022)
- 13.4% of parents report experiencing alienating behaviors (Harman, Leder-Elder, & Biringen, 2019)
- Children of alienation show elevated rates of anxiety, depression, and substance abuse (Baker, 2005; Verrocchio et al., 2018)
- The WHO ICD-11 includes caregiver-child relationship problems (QE52.0)

### 4. Solution

Propose specific, actionable solutions. Connect each solution to the PACCS Five Pillars.

**Potential solutions to advocate:**
- Statutory definition of parental alienation
- Mandatory training for family court professionals
- Consequences for custodial interference
- Funded reunification services
- Public awareness and education

### 5. Call to Action

End with a clear, specific ask. Tell the reader exactly what they can do.

**Examples:**
- "Contact your state representative and ask them to support [Bill Number]."
- "Learn the warning signs of parental alienation and share this knowledge with your community."
- "Demand that our family courts protect children's psychological safety with the same urgency they apply to physical safety."

---

## Submission Tips

1. **Research the publication.** Read recent op-eds to understand the tone, length, and style the publication prefers.
2. **Follow submission guidelines exactly.** Most publications list their op-ed submission process online.
3. **Include your credentials.** Editors want to know why you are qualified to write on this topic.
4. **Pitch with a timely hook.** Tie your piece to current events, new research, or pending legislation.
5. **Do not submit the same piece to multiple publications simultaneously** unless they accept simultaneous submissions.
6. **Be patient.** Response times vary from 24 hours to several weeks.
7. **Proofread carefully.** Errors undermine credibility.
8. **Prepare a short bio** (2-3 sentences) to accompany the piece.

## Sample Op-Ed 1: For a General-Interest Newspaper

**Title:** Our Family Courts Are Failing Children — Here Is What the Research Says

**Byline:** [Author Name], [Credentials/Affiliation]

**Word Count:** ~750

---

When a child is physically harmed, our legal system responds with urgency. When a child is psychologically harmed — systematically turned against a loving parent through manipulation, false allegations, and interference — our legal system too often looks the other way.

Parental alienation is a pattern of behavior in which one parent damages a child's relationship with the other parent. It includes denigration, interference with contact, manipulation of the child's emotions, and sometimes false allegations of abuse. It is not a fringe concept: it is documented in hundreds of peer-reviewed studies and recognized by mental health professionals worldwide.

Research published in peer-reviewed journals estimates that parental alienation affects approximately 1% of all children and a substantially higher percentage of children in separated and divorced families. More than 13% of parents report experiencing alienating behaviors from a co-parent. These are not small numbers.

The consequences for children are severe and well-documented. Studies show that children subjected to alienation experience elevated rates of anxiety, depression, low self-esteem, and substance abuse. These effects persist into adulthood, impairing their ability to form and maintain healthy relationships. In the most severe cases, children lose a parent entirely — not through death, but through deliberate psychological manipulation.

Despite this evidence, most family court judges and custody evaluators receive little to no training on alienation dynamics. There is no statutory definition of parental alienation in most states. There are few meaningful consequences for parents who systematically interfere with their children's relationships. And when courts do recognize the problem, they often lack the tools and resources to respond effectively.

This is a child protection issue. A child who is alienated from a fit parent is a child whose emotional safety and psychological health have been compromised. The research is clear. The professional consensus is growing. What is missing is the political will to act.

We need legislators to define parental alienation in statute, mandate training for family court professionals, establish consequences for custodial interference, and fund evidence-based reunification programs. These are not radical proposals. They are the minimum standard of care that our children deserve.

Every child has the right to love and be loved by both parents. It is time for our legal system to protect that right.

## Sample Op-Ed 2: For a Legal or Professional Publication

**Title:** Parental Alienation Is a Child Protection Issue — Courts Must Respond Accordingly

**Byline:** [Author Name], [Credentials/Affiliation]

**Word Count:** ~900

---

Family courts exist to protect children. Yet in cases of parental alienation — where one parent systematically undermines a child's relationship with the other parent — courts routinely fail to identify, understand, or effectively address the harm being done. This failure has consequences measured not in legal outcomes, but in the psychological well-being of children.

Parental alienation is not a new concept in the clinical or legal literature. It has been the subject of peer-reviewed research for more than three decades. The World Health Organization's ICD-11 includes "caregiver-child relationship problem" (QE52.0), and professional organizations in psychology, psychiatry, and family law have published guidance on identification and intervention. A 2020 consensus statement signed by researchers from multiple countries affirmed that parental alienation is a legitimate clinical and legal concern supported by a substantial evidence base.

Yet the gap between the research and courtroom practice remains vast. A survey of family court professionals found that many judges report receiving no specialized training on alienation dynamics. Custody evaluators may be unfamiliar with validated assessment tools. Guardians ad litem may conflate a child's expressed preference — which in alienation cases is often a product of the alienating parent's influence — with the child's genuine best interest.

The consequences of this knowledge gap fall on children. Studies consistently document that children subjected to parental alienation experience clinically significant levels of anxiety, depression, and impaired self-esteem. The harm is not limited to childhood: adult survivors of alienation report persistent difficulties with trust, intimacy, and relationship stability. Research by Baker (2005) found elevated rates of substance abuse and self-destructive behavior among adults who experienced alienation as children.

From a legal perspective, the failure to address alienation also raises due process and equal protection concerns. A parent who is falsely accused and systematically excluded from a child's life — not on the basis of evidence, but on the basis of the other parent's manipulation — has been deprived of a fundamental relationship without adequate legal process.

The Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety (PACCS) has proposed a framework grounded in five pillars: emotional safety, psychological health, truth and evidence, protection from abuse of systems, and family preservation and healing. This framework offers courts a structured, evidence-based approach to identifying and responding to alienation that protects children while preserving due process for all parties.

Specific recommendations include: adopting a statutory definition of parental alienation based on observable behaviors and their impact on the child; mandating specialized training for all family court professionals; establishing graduated consequences for custodial interference; creating rebuttable presumptions favoring custody modification when alienation is established; and funding evidence-based reunification programs.

Courts have both the authority and the obligation to protect children from psychological harm. Parental alienation is a form of psychological harm. It is time for the legal system to treat it accordingly.

*[Author Name] is [credentials]. [He/She/They] serves on the [committee/board] of the Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety (PACCS).*
