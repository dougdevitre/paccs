# Podcast and Interview Talking Points

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** PACCS Spokespersons and Media Representatives
**Format:** Key Messages, Q&A, and Media Guidance

## 5 Core PACCS Messages

Weave these messages into every interview. Return to them when the conversation drifts.

1. **Parental alienation is a child protection issue.** Children who are systematically turned against a loving, fit parent suffer documented psychological harm — including anxiety, depression, and long-term relationship difficulties.

2. **The research is clear and growing.** Parental alienation is supported by three decades of peer-reviewed research, recognized in the WHO's ICD-11, and affirmed by professionals across mental health, legal, and research disciplines worldwide.

3. **Children need both parents.** When there are no substantiated safety concerns, children benefit from maintaining meaningful relationships with both parents. Alienation deprives children of that fundamental right.

4. **Our systems need to catch up with the science.** Most family court judges and evaluators receive little or no training on alienation dynamics. We need statutory definitions, mandated training, and evidence-based interventions.

5. **PACCS is grounded in evidence, not ideology.** Everything we advocate is based on the best available peer-reviewed research. We follow the Five Pillars of Child-Centered Safety: emotional safety, psychological health, truth and evidence, protection from abuse of systems, and family preservation and healing.

| Question about abusers using alienation claims | "That is an important concern, and it is exactly why we advocate for evidence-based assessment. The research shows that..." |

## Personal Story Framing: Safe Storytelling

When sharing stories (yours or others'), follow these guidelines to protect families and maintain credibility.

### Do

- Anonymize all details — no names, locations, or identifying facts
- Focus on the child's experience and well-being
- Use general patterns rather than specific case details
- Frame stories as illustrative of broader research findings
- Say: "In cases like these, research shows that children often experience..."

### Do Not

- Share identifying details about any family or child
- Describe ongoing legal proceedings
- Make allegations against specific individuals
- Use emotional language that undermines professional credibility
- Present a single story as proof of a broader claim

### Sample Framing

> "I have worked with families where a child went from having a warm, loving relationship with a parent to refusing all contact — not because of anything that parent did, but because of a sustained campaign of manipulation by the other parent. The research tells us this causes real, measurable harm to children."

## 10 Common Interview Questions and Answers

### Q1: What is parental alienation?

> "Parental alienation is a pattern of behavior where one parent damages a child's relationship with the other parent through denigration, interference with contact, and psychological manipulation. The result is that the child unjustifiably rejects a parent who was previously loved and trusted. It is documented in hundreds of peer-reviewed studies and recognized by mental health professionals worldwide."

### Q2: Is parental alienation recognized by the WHO or DSM?

> "The World Health Organization's ICD-11, which is used internationally, includes 'caregiver-child relationship problem' under code QE52.0, which encompasses alienation dynamics. While parental alienation is not listed as a standalone diagnosis in the DSM-5, this is not uncommon for relational phenomena. Many well-established clinical concepts — like domestic violence or bullying — are recognized and treated by professionals without having their own DSM category."

### Q3: Is not parental alienation something that was debunked?

> "No. That claim is a form of misinformation. Parental alienation is supported by over 1,000 publications in the scientific literature, including peer-reviewed research, systematic reviews, and an international consensus statement signed by researchers from multiple countries. The concept has evolved significantly since it was first described and is now grounded in attachment theory, developmental psychology, and family systems research."

### Q4: Do abusers use alienation claims to gain custody?

> "This is a concern we take seriously, which is exactly why PACCS advocates for evidence-based assessment by trained professionals. The research shows that alienation claims are raised by both mothers and fathers, and that false claims exist on both sides — both false claims of alienation and false claims of abuse. The solution is not to dismiss alienation, but to ensure that courts have trained professionals who can distinguish between alienation and legitimate estrangement."

### Q5: Why would a child reject a parent if not for a good reason?

> "This is one of the most common misconceptions. Research shows that in alienation cases, children reject a parent not because of their own experience, but because they have been psychologically influenced to do so. Children are developmentally susceptible to a powerful parent's influence. The child's rejection feels real and authentic to the child, but it is driven by the alienating parent's behavior, not by the targeted parent's conduct."

### Q6: What does alienation do to children long-term?

> "The research documents significant long-term effects. Children who experience alienation show elevated rates of anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem during childhood. As adults, they report higher rates of substance abuse, difficulty with trust and intimacy, and challenges in maintaining healthy relationships. Some also report guilt and grief when they later recognize what happened to them."

### Q7: What is PACCS asking for specifically?

> "We are asking for five things: a statutory definition of parental alienation so courts have a consistent framework; mandatory training for family court judges and evaluators; graduated consequences for custodial interference; evidence-based reunification programs for affected children; and rebuttable presumptions that protect children's relationships when alienation is established."

### Q8: How do you distinguish alienation from a child who has a legitimate reason to avoid a parent?

> "This is a critical question, and it is why we insist on evidence-based assessment by trained professionals. Legitimate estrangement — where a child avoids a parent due to that parent's own harmful behavior — looks different from alienation. In estrangement, the child's response is proportional to real experiences. In alienation, the child's rejection is disproportionate, is echoed in the alienating parent's language, and often began after the parents separated rather than before. Trained professionals can distinguish between the two through validated assessment methods."

### Q9: Are reunification programs safe for children?

> "Ethical, evidence-based reunification programs include safety screening as a prerequisite. No legitimate program reunifies a child with a parent who poses a substantiated safety risk. What reunification programs do is help children who have been alienated from a safe parent rebuild that relationship in a structured, professionally guided environment. Research on outcomes shows that the vast majority of children who complete evidence-based reunification programs report positive experiences and restored relationships."

### Q10: What can a listener do right now?

> "Three things: First, educate yourself — learn the warning signs of parental alienation and share that knowledge. Second, support legislation in your state that addresses alienation — contact your legislators. Third, challenge misinformation when you encounter it. Every person who understands this issue is a potential advocate for a child who cannot advocate for themselves."

## Do-Not-Say List

Avoid these phrases in any media appearance. They undermine credibility, invite backlash, or misrepresent PACCS positions.

| Do Not Say | Why | Say Instead |
|------------|-----|-------------|
| "Parental Alienation Syndrome" or "PAS" | The syndrome label is controversial and not formally recognized; it invites dismissal | "Parental alienation" (without "syndrome") |
| "The child is brainwashed" | Inflammatory and reductive | "The child has been psychologically influenced" |
| "The mother/father is the alienator" | Gendered framing invites bias claims | "The alienating parent" or "the parent engaging in alienating behaviors" |
| "Courts are corrupt" | Undermines credibility and alienates potential allies in the judiciary | "Family courts need better training and tools to identify alienation" |
| "This is a fathers'/mothers' rights issue" | Gendered framing narrows the issue | "This is a child safety issue that affects families regardless of gender" |
| "All children who resist a parent are alienated" | Overgeneralization; ignores legitimate estrangement | "When a child resists a parent, it is important to determine whether the resistance is based on the child's own experience or on alienating influence" |
| "Therapists are useless" | Alienates a key professional audience | "Traditional therapy approaches may not be designed to address alienation dynamics" |
| "The other parent is evil/narcissistic" | Diagnostic labels and moral judgments are inappropriate in public-facing content | "The alienating parent is engaging in behaviors that harm the child" |
