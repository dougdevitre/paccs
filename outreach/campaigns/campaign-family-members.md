# Marketing Campaign: Supporting Family Member Advocates

> **Target Advocate Type:** Grandparents, siblings, aunts/uncles, extended family affected by alienation
> **Campaign Goal:** Educate family members on alienation dynamics and equip them with safe ways to support without making things worse
> **Duration:** 6 weeks (ongoing nurture after)
> **Channels:** Facebook, email, community groups, faith-based networks

---

## Campaign Overview

When a child is alienated from a parent, the damage radiates outward — grandparents lose access to grandchildren, siblings watch their brother or sister suffer in silence, and extended families are torn apart by a conflict they didn't create and can't control.

Family members often want desperately to help but don't know how. They may confront the alienating parent (making things worse), share unsolicited legal advice (creating friction), or simply withdraw because the pain is too much. This campaign educates family members on what alienation is, how to support safely, and how to maintain hope for reconnection.

---

## Target Audience Profile

**Who they are:**
- Grandparents who've lost contact with grandchildren (largest segment)
- Siblings watching a brother or sister lose their child
- Aunts, uncles, and cousins cut off from the family
- New partners/spouses of targeted parents
- Ages 35-75, skewing older for grandparents

**Pain points:**
- Grief and helplessness ("I can't see my grandchild and there's nothing I can do")
- Confusion ("I don't understand why this is happening")
- Fear of making it worse ("What if I say the wrong thing?")
- Anger at the alienating parent (which can backfire)
- Estrangement from the child who doesn't know them anymore
- Watching the targeted parent deteriorate
- Legal powerlessness (limited standing in most states)

---

## Key Messages

1. **"Your grief is real. Losing access to a grandchild/niece/nephew is a devastating loss."**
2. **"The best thing you can do is support the targeted parent — calmly, consistently, and without drama."**
3. **"Understanding alienation helps you avoid accidentally making it worse."**
4. **"Your role is to be a steady, loving presence — not to fix the legal situation."**
5. **"The child hasn't stopped loving you. They've been told to stop showing it."**
6. **"Patience is not passivity. Staying present IS advocacy."**
7. **"You matter in this child's story — even if they can't see it right now."**

---

## Campaign Phases

### Phase 1: Awareness (Weeks 1-2)
**Theme:** "Your Grief Is Real"
- Share content validating the family member experience
- Educate on what parental alienation is (basics)
- Post in grandparent support and family communities
- Introduce PACCS as a resource
- Goal: Email sign-ups, awareness

### Phase 2: Engagement (Weeks 3-4)
**Theme:** "How to Help Without Hurting"
- Release guide on safe ways to support the targeted parent
- Share the glossary (understand the language)
- Provide "What to Say / What NOT to Say" guidance
- Address common mistakes family members make
- Goal: Resource downloads, engagement

### Phase 3: Activation (Weeks 5-6)
**Theme:** "Your Presence Matters"
- Provide templates for maintaining connection (cards, letters, gifts)
- Share guidance on grandparent rights by state
- Connect to support groups for extended family
- Address financial and emotional support strategies
- Goal: Community connections, peer support

### Phase 4: Sustained Action (Ongoing)
**Theme:** "Hope and Patience"
- Monthly check-in content
- Stories of families that reconnected
- Ongoing education on alienation dynamics
- Holiday and birthday guidance (maintaining presence)
- Goal: Retention, hope, long-term engagement

## Email Sequences

### Email 1: Welcome

**Subject:** Understanding what's happening to your family

Dear [Name],

If someone in your family is experiencing parental alienation, you're living through a kind of grief that few people understand — the loss of a child or grandchild who is alive but unreachable.

You're not alone in this. And understanding what's happening is the first step toward helping.

**What is parental alienation?** It's when one parent systematically turns a child against the other parent — and often against that parent's entire family. The child may refuse contact, repeat rehearsed reasons for rejection, or act as though you don't exist.

**What you should know:**
- This is not your fault
- The child hasn't stopped loving you — they've been conditioned to hide it
- There are safe ways to support the targeted parent
- Patience is powerful, even when it feels passive

Start with our glossary — it explains every term you'll encounter: [link]

We're here for your family.

— The PACCS Team

### Email 2: Follow-Up (Week 2)

**Subject:** How to help without making things worse

Hi [Name],

Family members often want to jump in and fix things. We understand. But in alienation cases, well-meaning actions can accidentally make the situation worse.

**What HELPS:**
- Listening to the targeted parent without judgment
- Being emotionally available and consistent
- Documenting what you personally witness (dates, words, behaviors)
- Supporting financially if you're able (legal costs are devastating)
- Keeping the door open for the child (cards, letters, gifts — even if returned)

**What HURTS:**
- Confronting the alienating parent (will be used against the family)
- Posting about the situation on social media
- Giving legal advice you're not qualified to give
- Pressuring the targeted parent to "just get over it" or "move on"
- Trying to contact the child against the targeted parent's guidance

The most powerful thing you can say: **"I believe you. I'm here. What do you need?"**

Resources for you:
- Understanding Your Child's Behavior Guide: [link]
- Self-Care Guide (for you too): [link]

— The PACCS Team

### Email 3: Call to Action (Week 4)

**Subject:** Your love matters — here's how to keep it alive

Hi [Name],

Even when you can't see the child, your love is not wasted.

Here's what we know from research: Many alienated children eventually reconnect with their rejected family. When they do, they look for evidence that people never stopped caring.

**Keep the door open:**
- Send birthday cards every year (keep copies for your records)
- Send brief holiday messages
- If you can, set aside small gifts or photos
- Write letters to the child — even if you can't send them yet
- Document your outreach (this matters if court involvement happens later)

**Connect with others who understand:**
- Join a grandparent/family member support group
- Connect with PACCS resources for extended family
- Consider therapy for yourself — this IS grief, and it deserves professional support

Your family's story isn't over. Keep loving. Keep hoping.

— The PACCS Team
