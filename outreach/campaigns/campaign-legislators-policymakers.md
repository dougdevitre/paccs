# Marketing Campaign: Protecting Children Through Informed Policy

> **Target Advocate Type:** Legislators and policymakers (state legislators, policy staff, judiciary committee members)
> **Campaign Goal:** Educate policymakers on parental alienation and secure meetings to present model legislation
> **Duration:** 12 weeks
> **Channels:** Direct mail, email, LinkedIn, in-person meetings

---

## Campaign Overview

Parental alienation is a well-documented form of child psychological harm, yet most state statutes do not address it directly. Legislators and judiciary committee members often lack exposure to the peer-reviewed research, the scope of the problem, or the specific statutory language that could protect children. This campaign bridges that gap by delivering concise, nonpartisan, data-driven materials to policymakers and requesting brief meetings to discuss model legislative provisions.

The approach is strictly solutions-focused. Every communication frames parental alienation as a child protection issue supported by research, not as a partisan cause or a grievance platform. Policymakers respond to evidence, constituent impact, and actionable proposals. This campaign provides all three in a format that respects their time and decision-making process.

---

## Target Audience Profile

**Who they are:**
- State legislators serving on judiciary, family law, or child welfare committees
- Legislative aides and policy staff who draft and review bills
- Judiciary committee chairs and members with oversight of family court statutes
- State bar association legislative liaisons
- Governor's office policy advisors on child welfare

**What motivates them:**
- Constituent concerns about family court outcomes
- Data showing measurable harm to children
- Model legislation that has been introduced or passed in other states
- Nonpartisan framing that avoids political risk
- Clear, concise briefing materials they can share with colleagues

**Pain points:**
- Limited awareness that parental alienation is a recognized, research-supported phenomenon
- Competing legislative priorities and limited bandwidth for new issues
- Uncertainty about what specific statutory changes would be effective
- Concern about unintended consequences or opposition from advocacy groups
- Lack of exposure to affected constituents who can speak to the issue
- Difficulty distinguishing evidence-based proposals from emotionally driven advocacy
- Fear of being perceived as taking sides in custody disputes

---

## Key Messages

1. **"Parental alienation is a child protection issue, not a custody dispute."** Research documents measurable psychological harm to children, including anxiety, depression, and long-term relationship difficulties.

2. **"The evidence base is substantial and growing."** Parental alienation is referenced in the WHO's ICD-11, supported by hundreds of peer-reviewed studies, and recognized in family courts across all 50 states.

3. **"Children have a right to healthy relationships with both fit parents."** When a child's bond with a parent is severed without legitimate cause, the child bears the greatest harm.

4. **"Current statutes leave children unprotected."** Most states lack statutory definitions, screening requirements, or intervention frameworks specific to alienation, leaving judges without clear guidance.

5. **"Model legislation exists and is ready for adaptation."** PACCS provides draft statutory language that has been reviewed by family law practitioners and can be tailored to any jurisdiction's existing framework.

6. **"Both mothers and fathers are affected."** Research does not consistently find a gender-based pattern in alienation. This is not a men's issue or a women's issue; it is a children's issue.

7. **"Effective legislation includes safeguards."** Model provisions incorporate safety screening, due process protections, evidence standards, and differentiation between alienation and legitimate estrangement due to abuse.

## Campaign Phases

### Phase 1: Research and Preparation (Weeks 1-3)
**Theme:** "Build the Foundation"

- Identify target legislators by committee assignment (judiciary, family law, child welfare)
- Research each legislator's policy interests, constituent demographics, and prior family law votes
- Prepare personalized briefing packets for each office
- Assemble a constituent contact list of affected families in each legislative district
- Brief volunteer advocates on messaging discipline and meeting protocols
- Finalize all printed and digital materials for distribution

### Phase 2: Initial Outreach (Weeks 4-6)
**Theme:** "Introduce the Issue"

- Send direct mail briefing packets to target legislators via official office addresses
- Send introductory email to legislative aides and policy staff
- Publish LinkedIn posts tagging relevant committee members and sharing research summaries
- Request 15-minute introductory meetings with legislative offices
- Distribute a one-page fact sheet at relevant committee hearings or capitol events

### Phase 3: Engagement and Meetings (Weeks 7-9)
**Theme:** "Present the Evidence"

- Conduct in-person or virtual meetings with legislative offices
- Present the briefing packet, model statutory language, and constituent stories
- Provide printed copies of all materials for office files
- Send follow-up emails within 48 hours of each meeting with digital copies and next steps
- Connect legislators with constituent advocates from their districts
- Share post-meeting summaries with the broader advocate network (without confidential details)

### Phase 4: Follow-Through and Sustained Engagement (Weeks 10-12)
**Theme:** "From Awareness to Action"

- Send model statutory language with a cover memo to offices that expressed interest
- Offer to connect legislative counsel with family law practitioners for drafting support
- Provide testimony templates for committee hearings if a bill is introduced
- Send a campaign summary and thank-you to all offices contacted
- Document lessons learned and update the outreach process for the next legislative session
- Transition interested offices to ongoing relationship management

## Email Sequences

### Email 1: Introduction with Briefing Packet

**Subject:** Briefing: Parental Alienation and Child Protection — Research Summary for [Office/Committee]

Dear [Legislator/Staff Name],

I am writing on behalf of PACCS (Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety) to share a brief, nonpartisan research summary on parental alienation and its impact on children in [State].

Parental alienation — the deliberate undermining of a child's relationship with a fit parent — is recognized by the World Health Organization, supported by hundreds of peer-reviewed studies, and identified in family courts across all 50 states. Yet most state statutes do not address it directly, leaving children without adequate legal protection and judges without clear guidance.

Attached you will find:

- **Legislative Briefing Packet** — A one-page overview of the issue, research base, and policy implications
- **Alienation as Child Abuse Brief** — A summary of the psychological harm documented in peer-reviewed literature
- **Model Statutory Language Summary** — An overview of provisions other states have considered

We are not asking you to take a position today. We are asking for 15 minutes of your time to share the evidence and answer questions.

This is a child protection issue supported by research, not a partisan advocacy effort. We welcome scrutiny of the data and are prepared to provide full citations for every claim.

May we schedule a brief meeting with you or a member of your policy staff?

Respectfully,
[Advocate Name]
PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety

Dear [Legislator/Staff Name],

I am following up on the briefing materials we sent on [date] regarding parental alienation and child-centered family law policy.

We understand that your schedule is demanding and that many issues compete for your attention. We are requesting only 15 minutes — in person, by phone, or via video — to walk through the key data points and answer any questions.

Here is what we would cover:

1. **The scope of the problem** — prevalence data and impact on children in [State]
2. **What other states are doing** — a brief overview of legislative activity nationwide
3. **Model provisions available** — statutory language ready for your counsel's review

We have materials prepared for your office files and are happy to adjust to whatever format works best for your team.

If a meeting is not feasible at this time, we would welcome the opportunity to leave materials with your office or connect you with constituent families in your district who have been affected.

Thank you for your time and your service.

Respectfully,
[Advocate Name]
