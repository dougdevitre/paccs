# Marketing Campaign: Empowering Support Group Leaders

> **Target Advocate Type:** Support group facilitators and peer support leaders
> **Campaign Goal:** Equip support group leaders with facilitation tools, vetted resources, and community connections to run effective parental alienation support groups
> **Duration:** 6 weeks (ongoing community after)
> **Channels:** Facebook Groups, email, webinars, peer networks

---

## Campaign Overview

Support group leaders are the backbone of the parental alienation community. They create the safe spaces where targeted parents find their first moment of validation — "You are not alone. This is real."

But most support group leaders are themselves survivors of alienation, running groups without formal training, clinical supervision, or curated resources. They face compassion fatigue, group conflict, and the weight of holding others' pain while managing their own.

This campaign recruits, equips, and connects support group leaders with the tools and community they need to sustain their critical work.

---

## Target Audience Profile

**Who they are:**
- Targeted parents who started a support group in their community or online
- Peer support volunteers in existing advocacy organizations
- Mental health professionals who facilitate alienation-specific groups
- Church or community leaders who've taken on this role informally

**What motivates them:**
- Deep personal experience with alienation
- Desire to help others avoid the mistakes they made
- Belief that community and peer support save lives
- Frustration with the lack of institutional support

**Pain points:**
- No formal facilitation training for alienation-specific groups
- Managing intense emotions and crisis situations in group settings
- Preventing group sessions from becoming venting-only spaces (vs. action-oriented)
- Compassion fatigue and burnout
- Finding reliable, evidence-based resources to share
- Handling group members who are in crisis or expressing suicidal ideation
- Balancing peer support with boundaries (not becoming a therapist)
- Isolation from other leaders doing the same work

---

## Key Messages

1. **"You are holding space for healing — and that matters more than you know."**
2. **"Great facilitation is a skill you can learn — you don't need a clinical degree."**
3. **"Your group members need resources, not just a place to vent."**
4. **"Taking care of yourself isn't selfish — it's what lets you keep showing up."**
5. **"You're not alone in this work. There's a community of leaders just like you."**
6. **"Evidence-based tools make your group more effective and your members more empowered."**
7. **"Structure protects everyone — including you."**

---

## Campaign Phases

### Phase 1: Awareness (Weeks 1-2)
**Theme:** "You're Already a Leader — Let Us Help You Lead Better"

- Share stories of support group impact (anonymized)
- Post about the unique challenges of facilitation
- Highlight the gap between need and resources
- Introduce PACCS as a resource partner for leaders

### Phase 2: Engagement (Weeks 3-4)
**Theme:** "Tools That Transform Your Group"

- Release facilitation checklists and group structure guides
- Share the PACCS resource library (templates, scripts, education materials)
- Offer downloadable "Meeting in a Box" starter kit
- Host a webinar on effective facilitation techniques

### Phase 3: Activation (Weeks 5-6)
**Theme:** "Connect, Learn, Lead"

- Launch a private community/network for support group leaders
- Pair experienced leaders with newer ones (peer mentorship)
- Share self-care resources specifically for facilitators
- Encourage leaders to share what's working in their groups

### Phase 4: Sustained Action (Ongoing)
**Theme:** "Stronger Together"

- Monthly leader check-ins (virtual)
- Quarterly new resource releases from PACCS
- Annual leader recognition and impact stories
- Cross-group collaboration opportunities
