# Myth vs. Fact: "Parental Alienation Has Been Debunked"

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** General Public, Media, and Professionals
**Format:** Myth vs. Fact Sheet

---

## The Myth

**Claim:** "Parental alienation has been debunked by the scientific community. It is not real, not recognized, and not supported by evidence."

This claim is frequently repeated in media, advocacy circles, and policy debates. It is used to argue that parental alienation should not be considered in custody proceedings and that professionals who identify it are relying on discredited science.

---

## The Facts

### 1. Over 1,000 publications in the scientific literature address parental alienation.

The concept of parental alienation has been the subject of extensive scientific investigation. A comprehensive bibliographic review identified over 1,000 books, book chapters, and peer-reviewed journal articles on the topic (Bernet & Greenhill, 2022). This body of literature spans clinical psychology, psychiatry, family law, social work, and forensic science.

### 2. Parental alienation is recognized internationally.

The World Health Organization's ICD-11 (International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision) includes "Caregiver-child relationship problem" (QE52.0), which encompasses the dynamics described in parental alienation. Parental alienation has been recognized in court decisions in more than 40 countries.

### 3. An international consensus statement affirms the validity of parental alienation.

In 2020, a consensus statement was published by researchers and clinicians from multiple countries affirming that parental alienation is a legitimate, evidence-based concept with significant implications for child welfare (Bernet et al., 2020). The statement was endorsed by professionals from fields including psychology, psychiatry, law, and social work.

### 4. Research on parental alienation is published in mainstream peer-reviewed journals.

Studies on parental alienation appear in journals such as *Family Court Review*, *Children and Youth Services Review*, *Journal of the American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry*, *Psychological Bulletin*, and *Child Abuse & Neglect*, among others. These are established, peer-reviewed outlets — not fringe publications.

### 5. The concept has evolved significantly since its origin.

Early formulations of parental alienation (including the term "Parental Alienation Syndrome" or PAS) have been refined and updated through decades of research. Modern understanding of parental alienation is grounded in attachment theory, developmental psychology, and family systems research — not in the work of any single author. The evolution of the concept is a sign of scientific progress, not evidence of invalidity.

### 6. Systematic reviews confirm the evidence base.

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses have evaluated the literature on parental alienation and confirmed that it is associated with specific, identifiable behaviors and measurable outcomes for children (Verrocchio et al., 2018; Harman et al., 2022). These rigorous review methods represent the highest standard of evidence synthesis.

### 7. Rejection of alienation by some advocacy groups does not constitute scientific debunking.

The claim that parental alienation is "debunked" typically originates from advocacy positions, not from peer-reviewed research demonstrating that the phenomenon does not exist. Disagreement within advocacy communities is not the same as scientific refutation.

---

## Why This Matters

When parental alienation is dismissed as "debunked," children are the ones who suffer. Specifically:

- **Children in alienation cases lose protection.** If courts and professionals are told that alienation is not real, they cannot identify or address it — and children remain in psychologically harmful situations.
- **Misinformation delays intervention.** The longer alienation goes unaddressed, the more entrenched it becomes and the harder it is to restore the child's relationship with the targeted parent.
- **Professionals are discouraged from training.** If the field is dismissed as illegitimate, professionals are less likely to seek education and training — perpetuating a cycle of ignorance and harm.
- **Policy solutions are blocked.** Legislative efforts to define and address alienation are undermined when the concept itself is dismissed.

## What the Research Shows: Key Studies

| Study | Key Finding |
|-------|-------------|
| Bernet & Greenhill, 2022 | Identified over 1,000 publications on parental alienation in the scientific literature |
| Harman et al., 2022 | Estimated approximately 1% of children experience parental alienation; higher rates in separated families |
| Verrocchio et al., 2018 | Systematic review found significant association between parental alienation and anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem in children |
| Baker, 2005 | Adults who experienced alienation as children reported elevated substance abuse and relationship difficulties |
| Harman, Leder-Elder, & Biringen, 2019 | 13.4% of parents reported experiencing alienating behaviors |
| Bernet et al., 2020 | International consensus statement affirming parental alienation as a legitimate, evidence-based concept |

---

## What Professionals Should Know

- **Parental alienation is not "debunked."** It is supported by a substantial and growing body of peer-reviewed research.
- **The concept has evolved.** Modern understanding is grounded in attachment theory and developmental psychology, not solely in early formulations.
- **Assessment requires training.** Identifying alienation requires knowledge of the research, validated assessment tools, and the ability to distinguish alienation from legitimate estrangement.
- **Dismissing alienation is not a neutral position.** Failing to identify alienation when it is present is as harmful as misidentifying it when it is not. Both errors harm children.
- **Stay current with the literature.** The research base is growing rapidly. Professionals have a responsibility to stay informed.
