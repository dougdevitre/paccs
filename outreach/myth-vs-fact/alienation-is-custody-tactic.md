# Myth vs. Fact: "Parental Alienation Is Just a Custody Tactic Used by Abusers"

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** General Public, Media, and Professionals
**Format:** Myth vs. Fact Sheet

---

## The Myth

**Claim:** "Parental alienation is just a custody tactic used by abusive parents to gain custody and silence abuse allegations. Raising an alienation claim is itself a red flag for abuse."

This claim asserts that parental alienation is not a legitimate phenomenon but rather a strategy used by abusers to deflect from their own conduct and gain control over children through the court system. Under this framing, any parent who raises an alienation claim is presumed to be an abuser.

---

## The Facts

### 1. Research shows that both mothers and fathers raise alienation claims — and both can be alienators or targets.

Parental alienation is not a gendered phenomenon. Studies consistently find that:

- Both mothers and fathers are identified as alienating parents in the clinical and legal literature.
- Both mothers and fathers raise alienation claims in family court proceedings.
- The dynamics of alienation are driven by behavior, not gender.

Framing alienation claims as inherently suspect based on the gender of the claimant is not supported by evidence (Harman, Leder-Elder, & Biringen, 2019; Bala, Hunt, & McCarney, 2010).

### 2. Dismissing all alienation claims harms the children who are genuinely alienated.

When the default assumption is that alienation claims are a "tactic," children who are actually being alienated lose their only avenue of protection. The consequence:

- Alienating behavior continues unchecked because the claim has been preemptively discredited.
- The targeted parent is silenced and may lose custody or contact.
- The child remains in a psychologically harmful environment with no intervention.
- The alienation becomes more severe and entrenched over time.

Blanket dismissal of alienation claims is as dangerous as blanket acceptance. Both errors harm children.

### 3. False claims exist on both sides — both false claims of alienation and false claims of abuse.

Research demonstrates that false or unsubstantiated claims occur in both directions:

- Some parents do raise alienation claims inappropriately or strategically.
- Some parents do make false abuse allegations to gain a custody advantage.
- Both types of false claims are documented in the literature (Bala et al., 2010; Trocmé & Bala, 2005).

The solution is not to dismiss one category of claims while accepting the other. The solution is evidence-based assessment of every claim by trained professionals.

### 4. Research on who raises alienation claims does not support the "abuser tactic" narrative.

Studies examining alienation in court proceedings have found:

- Alienation claims are raised by mothers and fathers at comparable rates (Bala, Hunt, & McCarney, 2010).
- Courts that have found alienation have done so against both male and female parents.
- The claim that alienation is exclusively raised by male abusers to gain custody does not reflect the empirical data.
- Many targeted parents who raise alienation claims are themselves the victims of coercive control, litigation abuse, or false allegations (Harman & Biringen, 2016).

### 5. The "abuser tactic" myth is itself a form of misinformation that harms children.

When this myth circulates unchallenged:

- Professionals may be reluctant to identify alienation for fear of being accused of enabling abuse.
- Courts may decline to investigate alienation claims, leaving children unprotected.
- Targeted parents — many of whom are not abusers — are stigmatized and denied due process.
- Alienating parents are emboldened by the knowledge that their behavior will not be scrutinized.
- Children suffer the documented consequences of unaddressed alienation.

### 6. Proper assessment protects children from both abuse and alienation.

PACCS advocates for evidence-based assessment that:

- Investigates all allegations of abuse thoroughly using validated forensic methods.
- Evaluates allegations of alienation using validated assessment tools and trained professionals.
- Does not assume that either allegation is true or false based on who raises it.
- Prioritizes the child's safety and well-being above all other considerations.
- Recognizes that a child can be at risk from both abuse and alienation — these are not mutually exclusive.

---

## Why This Matters

- **Children need protection from all forms of harm** — including physical abuse, sexual abuse, neglect, and the psychological harm of alienation. A framework that recognizes only some forms of harm leaves children vulnerable.
- **The "abuser tactic" myth silences legitimate claims.** Parents who are genuinely experiencing alienation — and their children — are denied access to justice when their claims are presumptively dismissed.
- **Professionals need freedom to evaluate evidence objectively.** When professionals fear being labeled as "complicit with abusers" for identifying alienation, the quality of professional evaluation declines.
- **The myth perpetuates a false dichotomy.** It frames alienation and abuse as opposing claims in which only one can be real. In fact, some cases involve both alienation and abuse, and professional assessment is needed to untangle the dynamics.

## What the Research Shows: Key Studies

| Study | Key Finding |
|-------|-------------|
| Bala, Hunt, & McCarney, 2010 | Analysis of Canadian court cases found alienation claims raised by mothers and fathers; courts found alienation against both male and female parents |
| Harman, Leder-Elder, & Biringen, 2019 | Both mothers and fathers report experiencing alienating behaviors; 13.4% of parents affected |
| Harman & Biringen, 2016 | Many targeted parents experience coercive control and litigation abuse from the alienating parent |
| Trocmé & Bala, 2005 | Significant percentage of abuse allegations in custody disputes are unsubstantiated |
| Verrocchio et al., 2018 | Children subjected to alienation experience clinically significant psychological harm regardless of who the alienating parent is |
| Bernet et al., 2020 | International consensus statement affirming parental alienation as a legitimate, evidence-based concept |

## What Professionals Should Know

- **Do not dismiss alienation claims based on who raises them.** Evaluate every claim on its merits using evidence-based assessment methods.
- **Do not assume that an alienation claim negates an abuse allegation, or vice versa.** Both claims can coexist and both require investigation.
- **Recognize that the "abuser tactic" narrative can itself be used as a tactic.** An alienating parent may invoke this narrative to discredit the targeted parent's legitimate alienation claim.
- **Seek training in both domestic violence dynamics and alienation dynamics.** Competent evaluation requires understanding of both phenomena and the ability to distinguish between them.
- **Focus on the child.** The question is not "which parent is more believable?" but "what is happening to this child, and what does the evidence show?"
- **Document your assessment process.** When you identify alienation, document the evidence and reasoning that supports your finding. When you do not find alienation, document that as well. Transparent methodology protects both the professional and the family.
