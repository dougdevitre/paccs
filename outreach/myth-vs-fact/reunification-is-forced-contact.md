# Myth vs. Fact: "Reunification Programs Force Children Into Contact With Abusers"

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** General Public, Media, and Professionals
**Format:** Myth vs. Fact Sheet

---

## The Myth

**Claim:** "Reunification programs force children into contact with abusive parents against their will. They are dangerous, traumatic, and ignore children's safety."

This claim portrays all reunification programs as coercive interventions that override children's safety. It is used to argue that reunification should never be ordered when a child resists contact with a parent, regardless of the reason for the resistance.

---

## The Facts

### 1. Ethical, evidence-based reunification programs include mandatory safety screening.

No legitimate reunification program reunifies a child with a parent who poses a substantiated risk to the child's safety. Ethical programs include:

- **Pre-program safety screening** to evaluate whether the targeted parent has substantiated findings of abuse, neglect, or other conduct that would endanger the child.
- **Review of court records, evaluations, and available evidence** before any reunification activity begins.
- **Ongoing safety monitoring** throughout the reunification process.
- **Established criteria for exclusion** — cases involving substantiated abuse are screened out, not accepted into the program.

The premise that reunification programs ignore safety is not accurate for evidence-based, professionally administered programs.

### 2. Reunification addresses the harm of alienation — it does not create harm.

Children who have been alienated from a parent have experienced a rupture in a significant attachment relationship. The harm is already occurring. Reunification programs are designed to:

- Restore the child's relationship with a parent from whom they have been unjustifiably separated.
- Help the child process the distorted beliefs and emotions created by the alienating dynamic.
- Provide a structured, professionally guided environment for rebuilding the relationship.
- Address the child's loyalty conflicts and anxiety in a therapeutic context.

Doing nothing — leaving the child in the alienated state — is not a neutral option. It is a decision to allow ongoing psychological harm to continue.

### 3. Research on reunification outcomes is positive.

Published studies on evidence-based reunification programs report:

- The majority of children who complete structured reunification programs show significant improvement in their relationship with the targeted parent (Warshak, 2010; Reay, 2015).
- Children who initially resisted contact frequently report positive experiences after reunification.
- Follow-up studies indicate that reunification gains are maintained over time in the majority of cases.
- Adverse outcomes are more strongly associated with failure to intervene than with the reunification process itself.

### 4. "Forced contact" mischaracterizes what reunification actually involves.

Ethical reunification programs do not simply place a child in a room with a parent and leave them there. They involve:

- **Psychoeducation** — helping the child understand alienation dynamics in age-appropriate terms.
- **Graduated contact** — rebuilding the relationship incrementally, with professional support at each stage.
- **Therapeutic intervention** — addressing the child's fears, distorted beliefs, and loyalty conflicts.
- **Skills building** — helping both the child and the targeted parent develop communication and relationship skills.
- **Professional oversight** — trained clinicians guide every stage of the process and adjust the plan based on the child's response.

Characterizing this as "forced contact" is akin to characterizing evidence-based therapy as "forced feelings." The process is structured, professional, and child-centered.

### 5. The child's resistance may itself be a product of alienation.

A central challenge in alienation cases is that the child's stated resistance to the targeted parent is often a manifestation of the alienation rather than an independent, informed choice. When courts defer entirely to the child's stated preference in these cases:

- The alienating parent's strategy is rewarded.
- The child loses the relationship with the targeted parent.
- The child's alienation-driven beliefs go unchallenged.
- The long-term harm documented in the research is likely to follow.

Reunification programs recognize that protecting a child sometimes means acting on the child's behalf, even when the child resists — just as a parent would insist a child attend school, take medicine, or leave a harmful situation, even if the child objects.

### 6. Opposition to reunification often comes from those who benefit from alienation.

Research and clinical experience indicate that resistance to reunification programs most frequently comes from:

- The alienating parent, who faces the prospect of their influence being identified and addressed.
- Professionals who are unfamiliar with alienation dynamics and mistakenly equate the child's resistance with a legitimate safety concern.
- Advocacy groups that oppose the concept of parental alienation in principle.

This opposition should be evaluated in context. The question is not whether the alienating parent or their advocates oppose reunification, but whether the evidence supports it and whether the child's well-being is served by it.

---

## Why This Matters

- **Children alienated from a parent are already experiencing harm.** Failing to intervene does not protect them — it abandons them.
- **Misinformation about reunification blocks access to effective treatment.** When legislatures, courts, or the public are told that reunification is "forced contact with abusers," they are less likely to fund, order, or support these programs.
- **Children have a right to relationships with both parents.** When there are no substantiated safety concerns, children's well-being is best served by maintaining meaningful relationships with both parents.
- **Evidence-based programs are the standard of care.** Opposition to reunification is not opposition to a specific program's methods — it is opposition to helping children recover from alienation.

## What the Research Shows: Key Studies

| Study | Key Finding |
|-------|-------------|
| Warshak, 2010 | Family Bridges program: majority of children showed significant improvement in relationship with targeted parent; gains maintained at follow-up |
| Reay, 2015 | Review of reunification program outcomes: positive results for majority of participating families |

| Fidler & Bala, 2010 | Failure to intervene in alienation cases is associated with progressive deterioration of the child-parent relationship |
| Warshak, 2015 | Deferring to children's alienation-driven preferences reinforces the alienation and harms children |
| Verrocchio et al., 2018 | Alienation is associated with significant psychological harm to children, supporting the need for intervention |

## What Professionals Should Know

- **Distinguish between ethical and unethical programs.** Advocate for evidence-based programs with published protocols, safety screening, and qualified practitioners. Oppose programs that lack these elements.
- **Safety screening is non-negotiable.** No child should be reunified with a parent who poses a substantiated risk. Ensure that any recommended program includes thorough safety evaluation.
- **Understand that the child's resistance may not reflect their best interest.** In alienation cases, the child's stated preference is often a product of the alienating dynamic. Professional evaluation is needed to determine the source of the resistance.
- **Failure to intervene is a decision with consequences.** Choosing not to order reunification when alienation is established is not a neutral decision — it is a decision to leave the child in a harmful situation.
- **Educate yourself on program models and outcomes.** Familiarize yourself with the published literature on reunification programs so that your recommendations are evidence-based.
- **Monitor outcomes.** When reunification is ordered, maintain oversight to ensure that the program is operating ethically and that the child is responding appropriately.

---

## What Ethical Reunification Looks Like

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Safety screening** | Thorough evaluation to confirm no substantiated safety concerns with the targeted parent |
| **Qualified professionals** | Clinicians with specialized training in alienation and family reunification |
| **Evidence-based methods** | Protocols grounded in peer-reviewed research and clinical best practices |
| **Graduated approach** | Incremental rebuilding of the relationship, not abrupt placement |
| **Child-centered focus** | Interventions designed around the child's developmental level and psychological needs |
| **Ongoing monitoring** | Regular assessment of progress with adjustments as needed |
| **Court oversight** | Reporting to the court with measurable goals and timelines |
| **Addressing alienating dynamics** | Interventions that address the source of the alienation, not just the child's symptoms |
