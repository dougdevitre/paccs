# Myth vs. Fact: "If a Child Resists a Parent, It Must Be for a Good Reason"

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** General Public, Media, and Professionals
**Format:** Myth vs. Fact Sheet

---

## The Myth

**Claim:** "If a child resists or refuses to see a parent, there must be a good reason. We should listen to the child and respect their wishes."

This claim assumes that a child's resistance to a parent is always a direct, proportional response to that parent's behavior. It is often used to argue that children who refuse contact with a parent should not be required to maintain the relationship. While children's voices should always be heard, this assumption can be dangerously simplistic when alienation is involved.

---

## The Facts

### 1. Children resist parents for many reasons — not all of them legitimate.

A child may resist contact with a parent for reasons that include:

- **Legitimate estrangement:** The child has experienced abuse, neglect, or frightening behavior from the parent, and the resistance is a proportional, self-protective response.
- **Alienation:** The child has been influenced by the other parent to fear, dislike, or reject the targeted parent, and the resistance is disproportionate and not based on the child's own experience.
- **Loyalty conflicts:** The child fears the alienating parent's disapproval or rejection and resists contact to maintain the alienating parent's favor.
- **Transitional anxiety:** The child experiences normal anxiety about transitions between homes but would benefit from support rather than permission to avoid contact.
- **Developmental factors:** Adolescents may resist parental contact for normative developmental reasons (desire for independence, peer focus) unrelated to the parent's fitness.

Determining which category applies requires professional evaluation — not assumptions.

### 2. Alienation-driven resistance looks different from legitimate estrangement.

Research identifies distinguishing features:

| Feature | Alienation-Driven Resistance | Legitimate Estrangement |
|---------|------------------------------|------------------------|
| **Origin** | Began after parental separation or custody conflict | Pre-dates separation; connected to specific experiences |
| **Proportionality** | Disproportionate to any identified conduct by the rejected parent | Proportional to documented behavior or incidents |
| **Ambivalence** | Absent — the child shows no mixed feelings, no positive memories | Present — the child may express both positive and negative feelings |
| **Language** | Uses adult phrases, legal terminology, or the alienating parent's exact words | Uses age-appropriate language reflecting their own experience |
| **Scope** | Extends to the targeted parent's entire family (grandparents, etc.) | Typically limited to the specific person whose behavior caused the concern |
| **"Independent thinker" claim** | The child insists the rejection is entirely their own idea | The child can describe specific experiences that led to their feelings |

(Baker & Darnall, 2006; Warshak, 2015; Bernet, 2010)

### 3. The "independent thinker" phenomenon is a hallmark of alienation.

In alienation cases, children frequently claim that their rejection of the targeted parent is entirely their own decision — that they are thinking for themselves. This assertion is often itself a product of the alienating parent's influence. Research shows that:

- Children who are alienated often parrot the alienating parent's views, language, and justifications while claiming independent thought.
- The child's stated reasons for rejection often mirror the alienating parent's grievances rather than the child's own experiences.
- When asked for specific examples, alienated children may provide trivial, borrowed, or fabricated justifications that do not support the intensity of their rejection.

The "independent thinker" claim should not be accepted at face value. It requires professional evaluation in context.

### 4. Reducing contact in response to alienation-driven resistance rewards the alienation.

When a court or professional reduces a child's contact with the targeted parent because the child "resists," the alienating parent's strategy is reinforced. The predictable outcome:

- The alienating parent learns that the child's resistance is an effective tool for reducing the other parent's access.
- The child learns that refusing contact is supported by the system, reinforcing the alienation.
- The targeted parent loses more time and connection, making future reunification more difficult.
- The alienation escalates because the behavior has been rewarded.

Research consistently warns that reducing contact in the absence of substantiated safety concerns is contraindicated in alienation cases (Warshak, 2015; Fidler & Bala, 2010).

### 5. Listening to children and protecting them from alienation are not contradictory.

Children's perspectives should be heard. But "listening to a child" does not mean uncritically implementing the child's stated preference — particularly when that preference may be the product of an alienating parent's influence.

A child-centered approach involves:

- Hearing the child's perspective in a developmentally appropriate manner
- Evaluating the child's statements in context, including the influence of each parent
- Determining whether the child's expressed preference reflects their authentic feelings or the alienating parent's programming
- Acting in the child's best interest, which may differ from the child's stated wish

---

## Why This Matters

- **Children's authentic voices are obscured by alienation.** When courts accept alienation-driven resistance as the child's genuine preference, the child's real needs go unaddressed.
- **Reducing contact causes harm.** A child who loses a relationship with a loving parent suffers documented psychological consequences, regardless of whether the child currently recognizes the loss.
- **The alienating parent is rewarded.** Systems that reduce contact in response to resistance incentivize alienating behavior.
- **Reunification becomes harder over time.** Every day of no contact makes restoration of the relationship more difficult and less likely.

## What the Research Shows: Key Studies

| Study | Key Finding |
|-------|-------------|
| Baker & Darnall, 2006 | Identified specific behavioral strategies used by alienating parents, including encouraging the child's defiance |
| Warshak, 2015 | Reducing contact in the absence of safety concerns reinforces alienation and harms children |
| Fidler & Bala, 2010 | Children's expressed preferences in alienation cases do not reliably reflect their best interests |
| Bernet, 2010 | The "independent thinker" phenomenon is a documented feature of alienation |
| Kelly & Johnston, 2001 | Proposed a continuum model for understanding child resistance, from normative to alienation-driven |
| Verrocchio et al., 2018 | Children subjected to alienation experience elevated anxiety, depression, and self-esteem difficulties |

## What Professionals Should Know

- **Do not assume resistance equals a legitimate reason.** Evaluate the child's resistance in context, including the influence of each parent and the dynamics of the family system.
- **Learn to distinguish alienation from estrangement.** Use the distinguishing features identified in the research. Seek training if you are not confident in making this distinction.
- **Be cautious about the child's stated preference.** In alienation cases, the child's stated preference may be a product of the alienating parent's influence, not the child's authentic feelings.
- **Do not reduce contact as a default response to resistance.** Unless there are substantiated safety concerns, reducing contact in the face of alienation-driven resistance is harmful to the child.
- **Recommend professional evaluation.** When the source of a child's resistance is unclear, recommend evaluation by a mental health professional with training in parental alienation and child development.
- **Consider the alienating parent's role.** Assess whether the alienating parent is supporting or undermining the child's relationship with the targeted parent.
