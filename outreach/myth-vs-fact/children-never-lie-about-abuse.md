# Myth vs. Fact: "Children Never Lie About Abuse"

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** General Public, Media, and Professionals
**Format:** Myth vs. Fact Sheet

---

## The Myth

**Claim:** "Children never lie about abuse. If a child says they were abused, they were abused."

This claim is often presented as an absolute rule intended to ensure that children's disclosures are taken seriously. While the underlying concern — protecting children — is valid and important, the absolutist framing is not supported by research and can itself lead to harm when applied without nuance.

---

## The Facts

### 1. Children's disclosures should always be taken seriously — and properly investigated.

No credible professional organization advocates ignoring or dismissing a child's report of abuse. Every allegation must be investigated thoroughly by trained professionals using evidence-based forensic interview methods. Taking disclosures seriously and recognizing that not all disclosures are accurate are not contradictory positions — they are both essential to protecting children.

### 2. Research shows that children can be coached, influenced, and confused.

Decades of research on children's suggestibility demonstrate that children — particularly younger children — are susceptible to suggestion, leading questions, and influence from authority figures. This does not mean children routinely fabricate abuse, but it does mean that the manner in which children are questioned can significantly affect what they report.

Key findings include:

- Children can incorporate false information into their accounts when subjected to repeated, suggestive questioning (Ceci & Bruck, 1995).
- Younger children (under age 6) are particularly susceptible to suggestion and may confuse imagined or suggested events with real memories (Bruck, Ceci, & Hembrooke, 2002).
- The source of influence matters: a trusted parent or authority figure can have a significant effect on what a child reports.

### 3. False allegations arise at elevated rates in custody disputes.

Research examining abuse allegations specifically in the context of custody disputes has found that a substantial percentage are not substantiated. Studies have reported unsubstantiated rates ranging from approximately 50% to 70% of allegations made during custody proceedings (Trocmé & Bala, 2005; Bala et al., 2007).

This does not mean that all allegations in custody disputes are false. It means that the adversarial context of custody proceedings creates conditions in which false allegations are more likely to emerge — whether through deliberate fabrication, coaching, or the amplification of misunderstandings.

### 4. Coaching is a documented phenomenon.

Clinical and forensic literature documents cases in which children have been coached to make allegations by a parent or other adult. Coaching can be explicit (telling a child what to say) or implicit (repeated questioning, emotional reactions to certain answers, exposing the child to adult narratives about the other parent).

Indicators that coaching may have occurred include:

- The child's account contains adult language, legal terminology, or concepts beyond their developmental level
- The child's account is unusually consistent and rehearsed across multiple tellings
- The child's account emerged only after prolonged exposure to one parent's narrative
- The child cannot provide contextual details that would be expected in a genuine account

### 5. Proper investigation protects both real victims and falsely accused parents.

The solution to the complexity of children's allegations is not to believe every allegation uncritically or to dismiss every allegation reflexively. The solution is rigorous, evidence-based forensic investigation conducted by trained professionals using validated protocols.

Proper investigation:

- Uses open-ended, non-leading questions (e.g., the NICHD Protocol)
- Is conducted by trained forensic interviewers, not parents or untrained professionals
- Considers the context in which the allegation arose
- Evaluates the child's developmental level and susceptibility to suggestion
- Examines corroborating or contradictory evidence
- Avoids repeated questioning by multiple parties

---

## Why This Matters

### For Children

- A child who has genuinely been abused deserves a proper investigation that substantiates their experience and leads to protection.
- A child who has been coached or influenced deserves a proper investigation that identifies the coaching, protects them from being used as a weapon in an adult dispute, and preserves their relationship with a safe parent.
- In both cases, the child is harmed by inadequate investigation.

### For Families

- A falsely accused parent may lose custody, contact, and reputation based on unsubstantiated allegations — and the child loses a parent.
- A genuinely abusive parent may escape accountability if investigations are poorly conducted.
- Proper investigation protects everyone.

### For the System

- When courts accept allegations uncritically, the system is vulnerable to manipulation.
- When courts dismiss allegations reflexively, the system fails to protect children.
- Evidence-based investigation is the only approach that serves justice and child safety simultaneously.

## What Professionals Should Know

- **Children's disclosures must always be taken seriously.** This is non-negotiable. But "taken seriously" means "properly investigated," not "accepted as fact without evaluation."
- **Suggestibility is a well-documented phenomenon.** Professionals must be aware of how questioning methods, parental influence, and repeated exposure to narratives can affect children's reports.
- **Context matters.** Allegations that arise in the context of custody disputes warrant careful evaluation of the circumstances in which they emerged.
- **Use validated forensic interview protocols.** The NICHD Protocol and similar evidence-based methods are designed to elicit accurate information while minimizing suggestibility.
- **Both errors harm children.** Believing a false allegation harms the child (and the falsely accused parent). Dismissing a true allegation harms the child (and enables the abuser). Proper investigation minimizes both types of error.
- **Avoid absolutes.** "Children never lie" and "children always lie" are both harmful oversimplifications. The truth is nuanced, and professional practice must reflect that nuance.
