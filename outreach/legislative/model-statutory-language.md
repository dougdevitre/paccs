# Model Statutory Language: Parental Alienation and Child-Centered Safety

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** Legislators, Legislative Counsel, and Policy Advocates
**Format:** Model Legislative Provisions

---

## Purpose

This document provides model statutory language that legislators may adapt for use in their jurisdictions. These provisions are designed to protect children from the psychological harm of parental alienation while ensuring due process, safety screening, and evidence-based intervention.

**Important:** These model provisions are templates for adaptation by legislative counsel. They should be reviewed by qualified attorneys and tailored to each jurisdiction's existing statutory framework before introduction.

---

## Model Provision 1: Statutory Definition of Parental Alienation

### Recommended Language

> **Section [___]. Definitions.**
>
> As used in this [chapter/title/article]:
>
> (a) **"Parental alienation"** means a pattern of behavior by a parent, guardian, or other person with custodial or visitation rights that:
>
> > (1) Denigrates, demeans, or vilifies the other parent in the presence of or in communications with the child;
> >
> > (2) Interferes with the child's contact, communication, or relationship with the other parent without legitimate justification;
> >
> > (3) Manipulates the child's perceptions, emotions, or loyalties to create fear, hostility, or rejection toward the other parent;
> >
> > (4) Makes false or misleading allegations against the other parent to the child, to professionals, or to authorities;
> >
> > (5) Uses the child as a messenger, spy, or confidant regarding the parental relationship or litigation; or
> >
> > (6) Engages in other conduct that, individually or in combination, has the effect of damaging or destroying the child's relationship with the other parent.
>
> (b) **"Alienating behaviors"** means specific acts or omissions that constitute or contribute to parental alienation as defined in subsection (a).
>
> (c) A finding of parental alienation shall not require proof of conscious intent by the alienating parent. The relevant inquiry is the effect of the behavior on the child and the child's relationship with the targeted parent.

### Commentary

This definition focuses on observable behaviors and their impact on the child, rather than requiring proof of intent. It is consistent with the clinical literature and avoids pathologizing either parent. The enumerated behaviors are drawn from peer-reviewed research identifying the most common alienating strategies (Baker & Darnall, 2006; Bernet, 2010).

## Model Provision 2: Mandatory Training for Family Court Professionals

### Recommended Language

> **Section [___]. Training Requirements for Family Court Professionals.**
>
> (a) All judges assigned to family court divisions, domestic relations courts, or juvenile courts shall complete no fewer than [8/12/16] hours of initial training and [4/6/8] hours of annual continuing education on the following topics:
>
> > (1) The identification and dynamics of parental alienation, including alienating behaviors and their impact on children;
> >
> > (2) The distinction between parental alienation and legitimate estrangement due to abuse, neglect, or other safety concerns;
> >
> > (3) Evidence-based assessment methods for evaluating allegations of alienation;
> >
> > (4) The psychological impact of alienation on children, including short-term and long-term outcomes documented in peer-reviewed research;
> >
> > (5) Evidence-based intervention and reunification approaches; and
> >
> > (6) The dynamics of false allegations in custody disputes and their impact on children and families.
>
> (b) All custody evaluators, guardians ad litem, parenting coordinators, and family mediators appointed by the court shall complete training equivalent to that required in subsection (a) before accepting such appointments.
>
> (c) Training programs shall be developed or approved by [the state judicial education commission / the Administrative Office of the Courts] in consultation with qualified mental health professionals with expertise in parental alienation and child psychological safety.
>
> (d) The [judicial education commission / Administrative Office of the Courts] shall maintain a list of approved training programs and make it publicly available.

### Commentary

This provision ensures that professionals making decisions about children's lives have adequate education in alienation dynamics. The training requirement includes the critical distinction between alienation and legitimate estrangement, ensuring that safety concerns are properly evaluated. Hour requirements should be adapted based on jurisdictional norms for judicial education.

## Model Provision 3: Consequences for Custodial Interference

### Recommended Language

> **Section [___]. Consequences for Custodial Interference and Alienating Behaviors.**
>
> (a) Upon a finding by the court, based on a preponderance of the evidence, that a parent has engaged in a pattern of alienating behaviors as defined in Section [reference to definition], the court may:
>
> > (1) Order the alienating parent to cease specific alienating behaviors, with detailed identification of the prohibited conduct;
> >
> > (2) Order the alienating parent to participate in a court-approved intervention program designed to address alienating behaviors;
> >
> > (3) Modify the parenting plan or custody order to increase the targeted parent's parenting time;
> >
> > (4) Award sole or primary custody to the targeted parent when the court finds that the alienating behavior constitutes a significant risk to the child's emotional or psychological well-being;
> >
> > (5) Appoint a parenting coordinator to monitor compliance with court orders;
> >
> > (6) Order the alienating parent to pay the targeted parent's reasonable attorney's fees and costs incurred as a result of the alienating behavior;
> >
> > (7) Hold the alienating parent in contempt of court for violations of custody orders or orders to cease alienating behaviors; and
> >
> > (8) Impose any other remedy the court deems necessary to protect the child's best interests.
>
> (b) Consequences shall be graduated and proportional to the severity and duration of the alienating behaviors. The court shall consider:
>
> > (1) The nature and frequency of the alienating behaviors;
> >
> > (2) The impact of the behaviors on the child;
> >
> > (3) Whether the alienating parent has been previously warned or sanctioned;
> >
> > (4) The alienating parent's willingness to comply with court orders; and
> >
> > (5) The best interests of the child.
>
> (c) Nothing in this section shall limit the court's authority to address safety concerns, including credible allegations of abuse or neglect, through existing protective mechanisms.

### Commentary

This provision creates a graduated framework for consequences, ensuring that courts have tools to address alienation at every level of severity. The graduated approach begins with behavioral orders and escalates through custody modification only when warranted by the severity of the alienating behavior and its impact on the child.

## Model Provision 4: Rebuttable Presumptions

### Recommended Language

> **Section [___]. Rebuttable Presumption Upon Finding of Parental Alienation.**
>
> (a) When a court finds, based on the evaluation of a qualified mental health professional and other competent evidence, that a child has been subjected to parental alienation as defined in Section [reference], there shall be a rebuttable presumption that:
>
> > (1) It is in the best interests of the child to have meaningful and consistent contact with the targeted parent;
> >
> > (2) The current custody arrangement should be modified to protect the child's relationship with the targeted parent; and
> >
> > (3) The child should be provided with evidence-based reunification services when the child's relationship with the targeted parent has been substantially impaired.
>
> (b) The presumption established in subsection (a) may be rebutted by clear and convincing evidence that:
>
> > (1) The child's resistance to the targeted parent is based on the child's own experience of abuse, neglect, or other conduct by the targeted parent that constitutes a genuine safety concern, as substantiated through competent evidence; or
> >
> > (2) Modification of custody would pose a specific, documented risk to the child's physical safety.
>
> (c) The presumption shall not be rebutted solely by:
>
> > (1) The child's stated preference to avoid contact with the targeted parent, unless supported by evidence of legitimate safety concerns;
> >
> > (2) The passage of time since the child last had contact with the targeted parent; or
> >
> > (3) Unsubstantiated allegations of abuse or neglect.

### Commentary

This provision creates a presumption that protects the child's relationship with the targeted parent while preserving robust safeguards. The "clear and convincing evidence" standard for rebuttal ensures that only substantiated safety concerns can override the presumption. The provision explicitly prevents the use of unsubstantiated allegations or the child's expressed preference (which may itself be a product of alienation) as the sole basis for rebuttal.

## Model Provision 5: Reunification Services

### Recommended Language

> **Section [___]. Evidence-Based Reunification Services.**
>
> (a) Upon a finding of parental alienation as defined in Section [reference], the court shall order evidence-based reunification services when the child's relationship with the targeted parent has been substantially impaired or severed.
>
> (b) Reunification services shall:
>
> > (1) Be provided by qualified mental health professionals with specialized training in parental alienation and family reunification;
> >
> > (2) Utilize evidence-based methods and protocols recognized in the peer-reviewed clinical literature;
> >
> > (3) Include safety screening to confirm that the targeted parent does not pose a substantiated risk to the child;
> >
> > (4) Be structured to restore the child's relationship with the targeted parent in a manner that is developmentally appropriate and child-centered;
> >
> > (5) Address the alienating dynamics, including any ongoing alienating behaviors by the alienating parent; and
> >
> > (6) Include a treatment plan with measurable goals, timelines, and reporting requirements to the court.
>
> (c) The court shall appoint a reunification coordinator or program that meets the qualifications established in subsection (b). The [state agency / Administrative Office of the Courts] shall maintain a registry of approved reunification providers and programs.
>
> (d) Costs of reunification services shall be allocated by the court based on the parties' respective financial circumstances, with consideration of which party's conduct necessitated the services.
>
> (e) The court shall retain jurisdiction to monitor the progress of reunification services and to modify orders as necessary to protect the child's best interests.
>
> (f) Nothing in this section shall be construed to require reunification with a parent against whom there are substantiated findings of abuse or neglect that constitute a current risk to the child's safety.

### Commentary

This provision ensures that reunification services are evidence-based, professionally administered, and include appropriate safety screening. The requirement for qualified professionals and recognized protocols protects against unstructured or coercive approaches. The safety carve-out in subsection (f) ensures that reunification is never ordered when there are substantiated safety concerns.

## Implementation Notes for Legislators

1. **Coordinate with existing statutes.** These model provisions should be integrated with each jurisdiction's existing family law framework, including best-interest-of-the-child standards, custody statutes, and domestic violence protections.

2. **Appropriate committee referral.** These provisions are typically best referred to judiciary or family law committees, with input from child welfare and mental health advisory bodies.

3. **Fiscal considerations.** Provisions requiring training and reunification services may have fiscal impacts. Consider phased implementation, grant funding, or existing judicial education budget allocations.

4. **Stakeholder engagement.** Engage family court judges, mental health professionals, family law attorneys, and child advocacy organizations in the drafting and review process.

5. **Sunset and review clauses.** Consider including a review period (e.g., 3-5 years) to assess the effectiveness of new provisions and make data-driven adjustments.
