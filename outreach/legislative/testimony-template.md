## Instructions

This template provides a structured framework for delivering effective legislative testimony on parental alienation and child-centered safety. Adapt the content to your jurisdiction, the specific bill or policy under consideration, and your own expertise. All testimony should remain evidence-based, professional, and focused on children's well-being.

### 4. Impact on Children

> The research is clear: children who are subjected to parental alienation suffer significant psychological harm. Published studies document:
>
> - Elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and low self-esteem (Verrocchio et al., 2018)
> - Disrupted attachment and loss of a secure parental relationship
> - Higher rates of substance abuse in adolescence and adulthood (Baker, 2005)
> - Difficulty forming healthy relationships throughout life
> - Intergenerational transmission of alienating dynamics
>
> These are not theoretical harms. They are documented outcomes that affect real children in our state every day.

## Tips for Delivering Testimony Effectively

### Before the Hearing

- **Know the bill.** Read the full text of the legislation and understand what it does and does not do.
- **Time your testimony.** Most committees allow 3-5 minutes. Practice delivering your testimony within the allotted time.
- **Prepare a written version.** Submit a written copy of your testimony to the committee clerk in advance or at the hearing.
- **Bring supporting materials.** Have copies of key research studies, your one-page briefing packet, or other PACCS materials available for committee members.

### During the Hearing

- **Be respectful and professional.** Address committee members formally. Do not use emotional or adversarial language.
- **Lead with data.** Legislators respond to evidence, not anecdotes alone. Ground every claim in research.
- **Be concise.** Make your key points clearly and directly. Do not ramble.
- **Anticipate opposition.** Be prepared for questions that challenge the concept of parental alienation. Respond with evidence, not defensiveness.
- **Stay child-centered.** Frame every point in terms of the impact on children, not the grievances of adults.

### After the Hearing

- **Follow up.** Send a thank-you email to the committee chair and members. Offer to be a resource for further questions.
- **Provide additional research.** If a committee member asked a question you could not fully answer, follow up with a written response and supporting citations.
- **Share the outcome.** Report back to PACCS and your network on the hearing's outcome and next steps.
