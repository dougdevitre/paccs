# Audience-Specific Advocacy Asks

**PACCS — Professional Alliance for Child Centered Safety**
**Audience:** Legislators, Courts, and the General Public
**Format:** Structured Advocacy Requests with Rationale

---

## Section 1: Asks for Legislators

### 1. Define Parental Alienation in State Statute

Adopt a clear, evidence-based statutory definition of parental alienation that identifies specific alienating behaviors and focuses on their impact on the child. Without a definition, courts lack a consistent framework and children fall through the gaps.

### 2. Mandate Alienation Training for Family Court Professionals

Require all family court judges, custody evaluators, guardians ad litem, and court-appointed mediators to complete evidence-based training on alienation dynamics, including how to distinguish alienation from legitimate estrangement. Untrained professionals cannot identify what they do not understand, and misidentification harms children.

### 3. Establish Graduated Consequences for Custodial Interference

Create a statutory framework of proportional consequences — from behavioral orders to custody modification — for parents who engage in sustained alienating behaviors. Currently, many courts lack effective tools to hold alienating parents accountable, and children bear the cost.

### 4. Fund Evidence-Based Reunification Programs

Allocate state funding for structured, evidence-based reunification programs administered by qualified professionals with safety screening protocols. Children who have lost a relationship with a parent due to alienation need professional support to restore that relationship safely.

### 5. Create a Rebuttable Presumption Favoring the Targeted Parent

When alienation is established through professional evaluation, establish a rebuttable presumption that the current custody arrangement should be modified to protect the child's relationship with the targeted parent, subject to rebuttal by clear and convincing evidence of a substantiated safety concern.

### 6. Require Timely Adjudication of Alienation Cases

Establish expedited timelines for cases involving allegations of alienation, recognizing that delay compounds harm to children and makes reunification progressively more difficult. Alienation is a time-sensitive harm.

### 7. Mandate Data Collection and Reporting

Require family courts to collect and report data on alienation findings, interventions ordered, and outcomes. Without data, policymakers cannot assess the scope of the problem or the effectiveness of legislative solutions.

## Section 2: Asks for Courts

### 1. Appoint Qualified Evaluators

Appoint custody evaluators and guardians ad litem who have demonstrated training and competency in identifying parental alienation and distinguishing it from legitimate estrangement. The quality of the evaluation determines the quality of the outcome for the child.

### 2. Order Interim Contact Protections

When alienation is alleged and preliminarily supported, issue interim orders that preserve the child's contact with the targeted parent pending full evaluation. Allowing contact to lapse during litigation rewards alienating behavior and makes reunification more difficult.

### 3. Enforce Existing Custody Orders

Consistently enforce existing custody and visitation orders, with meaningful consequences for noncompliance. When courts fail to enforce their own orders, alienating parents learn that court orders are unenforceable suggestions.

### 4. Consider Alienation as a Best-Interest Factor

Explicitly weigh a parent's alienating behavior as a factor in best-interest determinations. A parent who undermines the child's relationship with the other parent is not acting in the child's best interest.

### 5. Order Evidence-Based Interventions

When alienation is found, order evidence-based interventions rather than traditional therapy alone. Standard therapy is not designed to address alienation dynamics and may inadvertently reinforce them.

### 6. Restrict the Use of the Child's Stated Preference in Alienation Cases

Recognize that in cases involving alienation, the child's stated preference may be a product of the alienating parent's influence rather than an authentic, independent choice. Evaluate the child's stated preference in context rather than accepting it at face value.

### 7. Monitor Compliance Through Parenting Coordinators

Appoint parenting coordinators to monitor compliance with court orders and report ongoing alienating behaviors. Ongoing oversight provides accountability and protects the child between court hearings.

## Section 3: Asks for the Public

### 1. Learn the Warning Signs of Parental Alienation

Educate yourself about the behaviors and dynamics of parental alienation. When friends, family members, teachers, or neighbors can recognize alienation, children are more likely to receive early intervention and support.

### 2. Support Legislation That Protects Children

Contact your state legislators and urge them to support legislation that defines parental alienation, mandates training for family court professionals, and funds reunification services. Your voice matters in the legislative process.

### 3. Challenge Misinformation

When you encounter claims that parental alienation has been "debunked" or is merely a "custody tactic," share evidence-based information from credible sources. Misinformation harms children by delaying identification and intervention.

### 4. Support Targeted Parents and Their Children

If you know a parent who is being alienated from their child, offer emotional support and practical assistance. Alienated parents often experience isolation, depression, and grief. Your support can make a meaningful difference.

### 5. Advocate for Professional Training

If you are a teacher, counselor, pediatrician, clergy member, or other professional who works with children and families, seek training on parental alienation and share what you learn with colleagues. Early identification by community professionals can change outcomes for children.

### 6. Share PACCS Resources

Distribute PACCS educational materials — myth-vs-fact sheets, video links, and briefing packets — through your networks. Awareness is the first step toward systemic change.

### 7. Vote for Child-Centered Candidates

When evaluating candidates for judicial and legislative office, ask about their positions on family court reform, child psychological safety, and parental alienation. Elect leaders who prioritize children's well-being.

## The Five Pillars of Child-Centered Safety

All advocacy asks are grounded in the PACCS Five Pillars:

1. **Emotional Safety** — Protecting stable, secure attachment
2. **Psychological Health** — Preventing fear, rejection, role-reversal
3. **Truth & Evidence** — Grounding decisions in validated research
4. **Protection from Abuse of Systems** — Recognizing false allegations and litigation abuse as harm
5. **Family Preservation & Healing** — Supporting ethical, evidence-based interventions
