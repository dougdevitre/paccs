## What Is Parental Alienation?

Parental alienation occurs when a child's relationship with a parent is damaged through the actions of the other parent (or another person), resulting in the child's unjustified rejection of the targeted parent. It involves a pattern of behaviors — including denigration, interference with contact, and psychological manipulation — that undermine the child's relationship with a fit parent without legitimate justification.

Parental alienation is recognized by mental health professionals worldwide and is referenced in the World Health Organization's ICD-11 under "Caregiver-child relationship problem" (QE52.0).

---

## Scope of the Problem

- Research estimates that parental alienation affects approximately 1% of children and adolescents in the general population and a substantially higher percentage in separated and divorced families (Harman et al., 2022).
- Studies suggest that 13.4% of parents report having experienced alienating behaviors from a co-parent (Harman, Leder-Elder, & Biringen, 2019).
- Parental alienation has been identified in family courts across all 50 states and in more than 40 countries internationally.
- Both mothers and fathers can be alienating parents and targeted parents; research does not consistently find a gender-based pattern.

---

## Impact on Children

Peer-reviewed research documents that children subjected to alienation experience:

- **Anxiety and depression** — elevated rates of internalizing disorders
- **Low self-esteem** — resulting from loyalty conflicts and identity confusion
- **Disrupted attachment** — loss of a secure relationship with a loving parent
- **Substance abuse** — higher risk in adolescence and adulthood
- **Relationship difficulties** — impaired ability to form and maintain healthy relationships
- **Intergenerational effects** — alienated children may replicate alienating dynamics in their own families

(Baker, 2005; Bernet et al., 2010; Verrocchio et al., 2018)

## Current Legal Gaps

| Gap | Consequence |
|-----|-------------|
| No statutory definition of parental alienation in most states | Courts lack a consistent framework for identification |
| Insufficient training for judges and custody evaluators | Alienation cases are frequently misidentified or ignored |
| No consequences for sustained custodial interference | Alienating behavior continues without accountability |
| Lack of mandated reunification services | Children lose relationships permanently |
| Inconsistent evidentiary standards | Alienation claims are treated differently across jurisdictions |

---

## What PACCS Recommends

1. **Define parental alienation in statute** — Adopt clear, evidence-based definitions so courts have a consistent framework for identifying alienating behaviors.

2. **Mandate training for family court professionals** — Require judges, custody evaluators, and guardians ad litem to complete evidence-based training on alienation dynamics and child psychological safety.

3. **Establish meaningful consequences for custodial interference** — Create graduated sanctions for parents who engage in sustained alienating behaviors, including modification of custody.

4. **Fund and require evidence-based reunification services** — Ensure that children who have been alienated have access to structured, professionally supervised reunification programs.

5. **Create rebuttable presumptions** — When alienation is established by a qualified professional, create a rebuttable presumption in favor of custody modification to protect the child's well-being.

---

## The Five Pillars of Child-Centered Safety

PACCS recommendations are grounded in five evidence-based principles:

1. **Emotional Safety** — Protecting stable, secure attachment
2. **Psychological Health** — Preventing fear, rejection, role-reversal
3. **Truth & Evidence** — Grounding decisions in validated research
4. **Protection from Abuse of Systems** — Recognizing false allegations and litigation abuse as harm
5. **Family Preservation & Healing** — Supporting ethical, evidence-based interventions
