# GAL and Custody Evaluator Meeting Scripts

> **Audience:** Targeted parents preparing for meetings with a Guardian ad Litem (GAL) or custody evaluator
> **Situation:** When a GAL or custody evaluator has been appointed in your case and you need to present your situation effectively without appearing adversarial, coached, or obsessive
> **Tone:** Cooperative, child-focused, factual, emotionally grounded
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-legal-advisory

---

## Before You Begin
- **Understand the role.** A GAL represents the child's best interests. A custody evaluator assesses both parents and the family dynamics. Neither is your advocate, but both can become powerful allies if they see the truth.
- **Prepare, but do not over-prepare.** Evaluators are trained to detect rehearsed or coached responses. Know your facts and speak from genuine experience, not from a script memorized word-for-word.
- **Organize your documentation.** Bring a concise binder or packet with a timeline, key communications, and evidence organized chronologically. Offer it at an appropriate time — do not dump it on the evaluator at the start.
- **Be yourself, but your best self.** Dress neatly. Be on time. Make eye contact. Speak calmly. Show genuine emotion when appropriate, but do not perform.
- **Consult your attorney beforehand.** Ask your attorney what this specific GAL or evaluator tends to focus on and any known preferences for how information is presented.

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: How to Present Your Case
**Context:** The evaluator asks you to describe the situation or your concerns about the custody arrangement.

**Opening statement (keep to 3-5 minutes):**
"Thank you for taking the time to meet with me. I want to share what's been happening with my family and with my children, and I want to start by saying that I believe [Child/children] deserve a strong, healthy relationship with both parents. My primary concern is that over the past [timeframe], my relationship with [Child] has been significantly disrupted, and I believe the children are caught in the middle of adult conflict in ways that are harming them."

**Then present the pattern — not individual incidents in isolation:**
"What I've observed is a pattern that includes [list 3-5 observable behaviors]:
- Repeated denial or interference with court-ordered parenting time
- My child expressing statements that mirror adult language and concepts they would not develop independently
- Exclusion from medical decisions, school events, and extracurricular activities
- A shift in my child's behavior that correlates with the timing of the separation/divorce
- Resistance or refusal to participate in recommended family therapy"

**Provide context, not just complaints:**
"Before the separation, my relationship with [Child] was [describe — close, involved, warm, active]. I was involved in [specific examples — bedtime routines, school activities, coaching, meals]. This change did not happen gradually over time. It coincided with [specific event or timeframe]."

**Close with what you want:**
"What I'm asking for is not punishment for the other parent. I'm asking for a structure that protects my children's right to have a relationship with both parents. I believe that with appropriate support — whether that's therapy, a modified schedule, or clear enforcement of the existing order — my children can recover from this dynamic."

**Do Not Say:** "My ex is a narcissist/sociopath." (Diagnostic labels alienate evaluators.) / "They're alienating my children." (Let the evaluator reach that conclusion from the evidence.) / "I'm the better parent." / "The children belong with me." / Anything that positions this as your needs versus the other parent's needs.

### Scenario 2: Questions to Ask the Evaluator
**Context:** You have an opportunity to ask the GAL or evaluator questions. Use them wisely.

**Appropriate questions:**
1. "What is the process for your evaluation? What should I expect in terms of interviews, home visits, and timeline?"
2. "Will you be speaking with third parties such as teachers, therapists, or medical providers? I can provide contact information if helpful."
3. "How should I provide documentation to you? Do you prefer it in a specific format?"
4. "Will you be observing interactions between me and the children? If so, how are those structured?"
5. "Are there any specific tools or assessments you use as part of this evaluation?"
6. "How do you typically address situations where a child expresses a strong preference that may have been influenced by a parent?"
7. "What is the best way to communicate with you if new incidents occur during the evaluation period?"
8. "When can I expect your report, and will I have an opportunity to review it before it goes to the court?"

**Do Not Ask:** "Are you on my side?" / "What do you think so far — am I winning?" / "Has the other parent said anything about me?" / "Have you dealt with alienation before? Do you believe in it?" (This sounds like you are testing them.)

### Scenario 3: How to Discuss Alienation Without Sounding Adversarial
**Context:** You need to convey that alienation dynamics are present without using inflammatory language or appearing obsessed with the other parent.

**Framework — describe behaviors, not diagnoses:**

Instead of: "My ex is alienating my children."
**Say:** "I've observed a pattern of behaviors that I believe are interfering with my children's ability to maintain a healthy relationship with me. I'd like to share some specific examples."

Instead of: "My child has been brainwashed."
**Say:** "My child has begun making statements that use adult language and concepts that are inconsistent with their developmental stage. For example, my 7-year-old recently said, '[exact quote].' This language mirrors phrasing that the other parent has used in text messages to me."

Instead of: "The other parent is coaching the child."
**Say:** "I've noticed that after extended time with the other parent, my child's behavior and statements change significantly. Before visits, they are warm and engaged. After returning from the other parent's home, they are [withdrawn / hostile / repeating specific phrases]. I have documentation of this pattern."

Instead of: "My ex is a pathological liar."
**Say:** "There have been several instances where information I received from the other parent did not align with independently verifiable facts. For example, [specific factual discrepancy]. I have documentation."

Instead of: "They're trying to erase me from my child's life."
**Say:** "I have been excluded from [school communications, medical appointments, extracurricular decisions] despite having legal custody rights. I have made repeated written requests to be included, which I can share with you."

### Scenario 4: Red Flags to Raise About the Other Parent's Behavior
**Context:** You need to communicate concerning behaviors without appearing to be on a smear campaign.

**Present each concern as an observation with supporting evidence:**

**Gatekeeping:** "I have been unable to attend [number] of my child's school events because I was not informed about them. I have emails to the school requesting to be copied on all communications, and I have the other parent's messages where they stated I 'don't need to be there.'"

**Schedule interference:** "In the past [timeframe], [number] of my scheduled parenting time exchanges did not occur. I have parenting app records and timestamped photos showing I arrived at the exchange location each time."

**Weaponizing professionals:** "My child's therapist has informed me that they were told by the other parent not to share information with me. I have [documentation]. I contacted the therapist with a copy of my custody order, and they confirmed they were not aware of my custodial rights."

**Undermining the child's attachment:** "My child recently returned a gift I gave them, saying, 'Mom/Dad says I shouldn't keep things from your house.' I have a photo of the returned items and can provide the text exchange where the other parent acknowledged this."

**Using the child as a messenger:** "Rather than communicating with me directly, the other parent frequently sends messages through the children. For example, my child told me, 'Mom/Dad says you need to pay for...' I have documented [number] instances of this."

**Do Not Say:** Any characterization of the other parent's personality or mental health. / "They do this because they're [diagnosis]." / Anything you cannot substantiate with documentation.

### Scenario 5: How to Talk About Your Child's Rejection
**Context:** Your child has expressed that they do not want to see you, and the evaluator asks about it.

**Say:** "This is the most painful part of this situation. My child has told me they don't want to see me. I take their feelings seriously, and I would never want to force a relationship that causes them distress. At the same time, I have significant concerns about how they arrived at these feelings."

"Before [specific timeframe], my relationship with [Child] was [describe — close, warm, involved]. The rejection began [when — after separation, after a specific event, after the other parent made specific statements]. It did not develop gradually in a way that would be consistent with a child independently forming these feelings."

"What concerns me most is:
- The language my child uses mirrors the other parent's language, not a child's natural expression.
- The rejection is absolute and lacks the ambivalence that children typically feel in these situations. They express no positive memories, no mixed feelings — only rejection.
- The rejection extends to my extended family, who had a close and loving relationship with the children.
- The reasons my child gives for rejecting me are either factually inaccurate, trivial, or things that happened long ago and were previously resolved."

"Despite the rejection, I have continued to [send letters, leave voicemails, attend events when allowed, request parenting time]. I have documentation of every attempt. I am not giving up on my child."

**Do Not Say:** "My child doesn't really feel that way." (Dismisses the child's expressed feelings.) / "If you just make them come, they'll be fine." / "The other parent programmed them." / Anything that suggests the child is lying — frame it as the child being put in an impossible position.

**Follow Up:** Provide the evaluator with documentation of your ongoing outreach to the child, your history of involvement before the rejection began, and any evidence of the other parent's role in the dynamic.

## Key Principles
- **Lead with the child, not with your grievance.** Every statement should center on the child's wellbeing and their right to both parents. Evaluators respond to child-focused parents.
- **Show, do not tell.** Instead of labeling the other parent, present documented behaviors and let the evaluator draw conclusions. Your job is to provide the data; their job is to interpret it.
- **Demonstrate emotional regulation.** Evaluators assess your emotional stability. Showing genuine sadness is appropriate. Showing rage, bitterness, or obsession is damaging.
- **Be honest about your own imperfections.** No parent is perfect. Acknowledging your own mistakes (without excessive self-blame) builds credibility. An evaluator who sees you as self-aware will trust your other observations more.
- **Trust the process.** A good evaluator will see through manipulation. Give them the information and the space to do their job. Pushing too hard or contacting them too frequently can backfire.
