# Medical and Therapy Provider Communication Scripts

> **Audience:** Targeted parents who need to assert their rights with medical providers, therapists, and mental health professionals involved in their child's care
> **Situation:** When you have been excluded from medical decisions, blocked from accessing records, or when a provider appears biased or uninformed about your custodial rights
> **Tone:** Professional, cooperative, rights-aware, child-centered
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-advocate

---

## Before You Begin
- **Know your legal rights.** Under HIPAA, both parents with legal custody generally have the right to access their minor child's medical records unless a court order specifically restricts this right. State laws may vary — consult your attorney for jurisdiction-specific guidance.
- **Obtain multiple certified copies of your custody order.** You will need to provide one to every medical provider, therapist, and specialist involved in your child's care.
- **Approach providers professionally.** Medical and mental health providers are typically not trying to exclude you. They may have been given incomplete or inaccurate information by the other parent.
- **Separate your feelings from your strategy.** Being excluded from your child's medical care is painful. Channel that pain into organized, professional action.
- **Document every interaction.** Note the date, time, who you spoke with, what was said, and any follow-up actions.

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: Asserting Your Right to Medical Information
**Context:** You have discovered that your child is seeing a doctor, specialist, or therapist and you were not informed. Or you have been told you cannot access your child's records.

**Say (calling the provider's office):** "Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm calling regarding my child, [Child's Full Name], date of birth [DOB]. I am [Child]'s [mother/father] and I have joint legal custody. I'd like to ensure that I'm listed as an authorized parent in your system and that I have access to [Child]'s medical records. I have a copy of our custody order that I can provide. Who should I send that to?"

**If they say they need the other parent's permission:** "I appreciate your caution, but under HIPAA and our state's custody laws, both parents with legal custody have independent access to their minor child's medical records. My right to access does not depend on the other parent's consent. I can provide a copy of the custody order that confirms my legal custody status. May I email or fax that to you today?"

**If they say they were told not to share information with you:** "I understand that someone may have instructed you not to share information with me. However, unless you have a court order on file that specifically restricts my access, both custodial parents have equal rights to medical information under federal and state law. I'd like to request a copy of any document in your file that restricts my access. If no such court order exists, I'd like to be added to the contact records and receive copies of all records, visit summaries, and treatment plans."

**Do Not Say:** "My ex told you to block me, didn't they?" / "This is illegal — I'll report you." / Any hostile or threatening language.

**Follow Up:** Send a written request (email or certified letter) for records access. Include a copy of your custody order. Request written confirmation of your status. If the provider refuses to comply, consult your attorney about filing a formal complaint.

### Scenario 2: Requesting Both-Parent Notification of Appointments
**Context:** You want to be informed of all scheduled appointments and have the ability to attend.

**Say:** "I'd like to set up a system where both parents are notified of all scheduled appointments for [Child]. I understand the other parent may be the one who initially scheduled this care, and I'm not asking to interfere with that. I simply want to be informed so I can be involved in my child's healthcare. Can you add my contact information as a second parent contact and send appointment confirmations and summaries to me as well?"

**If they say they can only have one primary contact:** "I understand your system may have limitations. Could I be added as an emergency contact or secondary parent contact? At minimum, I'd like to receive a summary of each visit — diagnosis, treatment plan, prescriptions, and follow-up recommendations. Can I arrange to receive that after each appointment?"

**If they suggest you coordinate with the other parent:** "I appreciate the suggestion, and I would prefer to handle this cooperatively. Unfortunately, that coordination has not been reliable in our situation. Having direct communication from your office ensures I stay informed regardless of the co-parenting dynamic. It's really about making sure both parents can support [Child]'s health consistently."

**Do Not Say:** "The other parent deliberately hides appointments from me." / "They're using the doctor as a weapon." / Any characterization of the co-parent.

**Follow Up:** Provide written authorization for the provider to communicate with you. Ask for the patient portal login if one exists. Request retroactive records (within your legal rights) to catch up on any care you have missed.

### Scenario 3: When a Therapist Appears Biased Toward One Parent
**Context:** Your child's therapist seems to have formed opinions based primarily on the other parent's narrative, makes statements that reflect one-sided information, or has made recommendations without consulting you.

**Say (requesting a meeting with the therapist):** "I'd like to schedule a session or phone call to introduce myself and share my perspective on [Child]'s situation. I understand you may have already received information from the other parent, and I want to make sure you have a complete picture. I'm not asking you to take sides — I'm asking for a chance to provide context that may be relevant to [Child]'s treatment."

**During the meeting:** "I appreciate you taking the time to speak with me. I want to share a few things that may be helpful for your work with [Child]:

First, here is a brief history of my relationship with [Child] before the current conflict began. [Provide 2-3 specific, positive examples.]

Second, I have concerns that [Child] may be receiving messages at home that are influencing how they present in therapy. For example, [Child] has begun using phrases like '[exact quote],' which is language I've also seen in communications from the other parent. I have documentation if that would be helpful.

Third, I want to support the therapeutic process. What can I do on my end to help [Child] feel safe and supported? Are there family sessions you would recommend that include me?"

**If the therapist makes a statement that concerns you:** "I want to make sure I understand. You mentioned [what they said]. Can you help me understand how you arrived at that observation? Was it based on what [Child] reported, what the other parent reported, or your own clinical assessment?"

**Do Not Say:** "You've been manipulated by the other parent." / "You're only hearing one side." / "You're not qualified to assess this situation." / Anything that questions the therapist's competence or motives directly.

**Follow Up:** Document the meeting. If bias persists after you have provided your perspective and documentation, consult your attorney about requesting a change of therapist or an independent evaluation. A therapist who has formed a strong alliance with one parent may not be effective for the child's treatment.

### Scenario 4: When a Provider Has Been Told Not to Share Information with You
**Context:** You contact a provider and they state that they have been instructed (by the other parent) not to share information with you or to refuse you access.

**Say:** "I understand that someone has asked you not to share information with me. I want to approach this respectfully. Could you tell me whether you have a court order on file that restricts my access to my child's records? If not, I'd like to provide you with a copy of our custody order, which confirms I have joint legal custody and the right to access [Child]'s medical information."

**If they say they have a court order on file:** "Could I see a copy of that order? I want to verify it with my attorney. If the order on file does not specifically restrict my access, I'd like to resolve this today."

**If they say they were verbally told by the other parent:** "I appreciate that the other parent may have communicated that, but a verbal request from one parent does not override a court order or federal law. Both parents with legal custody have independent rights under HIPAA. I have the custody order here. Can we review it together and update your records accordingly?"

**If they refuse to engage:** "I understand this may be an uncomfortable situation for your office. I'm going to put my request in writing and provide a copy of the custody order. I'd appreciate a written response within [reasonable timeframe — 5-10 business days]. If we can't resolve this directly, my attorney may need to get involved, but I'd strongly prefer to handle it cooperatively."

**Do Not Say:** "The other parent is lying to you." / "I'm going to report your practice." / Any ultimatums on the first interaction.

**Follow Up:** Send a formal written request via certified mail. Include the custody order, your identification, and a specific request for records and ongoing access. If the provider does not respond or continues to refuse access, consult your attorney about legal remedies, including subpoenas.

### Scenario 5: How to Provide Custody Orders to Providers
**Context:** Establishing your rights with a new or existing provider by providing the custody order proactively.

**Say (in person):** "I'd like to provide a copy of our custody order for [Child]'s file. This confirms that I have joint legal custody and the right to access medical records, be notified of appointments, and participate in medical decisions. I'd like to be listed as a parent contact in your system. Here is my identification and the certified copy. Could you make a copy for the file and return the original to me?"

**Say (via letter/email):**

"Dear [Provider Name / Practice Name],

I am writing to provide you with a copy of the custody order for my child, [Child's Full Name], date of birth [DOB], who is a patient at your practice.

I am [Child]'s [mother/father] and I have joint legal custody, as reflected in the enclosed court order dated [date], issued by [Court Name and Jurisdiction].

I respectfully request the following:

1. That this custody order be placed in [Child]'s file.
2. That I be listed as an authorized parent contact with the following information: [phone, email, address].
3. That I receive notification of all scheduled appointments.
4. That I be provided with copies of all medical records, visit summaries, treatment plans, and prescriptions.
5. That I be consulted on any significant medical decisions regarding [Child]'s care.

Please note that there is no court order restricting my access to [Child]'s medical information or my right to participate in medical decisions.

I look forward to working cooperatively with your practice to ensure [Child] receives the best possible care. Please confirm receipt of this letter and the actions taken.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Contact Information]

Enclosure: Certified copy of custody order"

**Do Not Say:** Any reference to the co-parent conflict. / "The other parent may try to remove me from the records." / Anything that puts the provider in the middle of a dispute.

**Follow Up:** Confirm receipt within one week. If you do not receive confirmation, follow up with a phone call. Keep a record of which providers have the custody order on file and the date you provided it.

## Key Principles
- **Providers are gatekeepers to your child's health — treat them as allies.** Your goal is to establish a cooperative relationship, not to win an argument. The provider who sees you as a reasonable, involved parent will naturally support your access.
- **The custody order is your key.** Every interaction should include or reference the custody order. It is the legal document that establishes your rights. Without it, providers may default to the other parent's instructions.
- **Written requests create accountability.** Verbal requests can be denied, forgotten, or misrepresented. Always follow up in writing.
- **Bias in providers is a case issue, not a provider issue.** If a therapist or doctor has been given one-sided information, your first response should be to provide your side — not to attack their competence. If bias persists after correction, it becomes relevant to your case.
- **Never use the child as a conduit.** Do not ask your child about their medical appointments, what the doctor said, or what medications they are taking. Get that information directly from the provider.
