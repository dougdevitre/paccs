# BIFF Response Method Scripts

> **Audience:** Targeted parents communicating with a co-parent in high-conflict custody situations
> **Situation:** When you need to respond to hostile, manipulative, or provocative messages from your co-parent via text, email, or parenting app
> **Tone:** Brief, Informative, Friendly, Firm (BIFF)
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-advocate

---

## Before You Begin
- **Understand BIFF:** BIFF stands for Brief, Informative, Friendly, Firm. It was developed by Bill Eddy, LCSW, Esq. for communicating with high-conflict personalities. The goal is to end the hostile conversation, not win it.
- **Why BIFF works:** High-conflict individuals seek emotional reactions. BIFF responses remove the fuel. Courts review your messages too — BIFF keeps you looking reasonable and child-focused.
- **Wait before responding:** Never reply in the first 30 minutes. Draft your response, then review it against the BIFF checklist before sending.
- **Use a parenting app when possible:** OurFamilyWizard, TalkingParents, or AppClose create timestamped, court-admissible records automatically.
- **Every message is a court exhibit:** Write as though a judge will read it aloud in open court — because they might.

---

## The BIFF Checklist

Before sending any response, verify all four elements:

| Element | Question to Ask Yourself |
|---------|--------------------------|
| **Brief** | Is this the shortest version that addresses the issue? (2-5 sentences max) |
| **Informative** | Does this provide only necessary factual information? |
| **Friendly** | Does this open or close with a brief, neutral-to-warm tone? |
| **Firm** | Does this end the back-and-forth? Does it avoid inviting further argument? |

If a message fails any element, revise before sending.

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: Denied Visitation
**Context:** Your co-parent refuses to make the child available for your court-ordered parenting time.

**They Say:** "The kids don't want to come. I'm not forcing them. You can't make me send them if they don't want to go."

**You Say:** "Thank you for letting me know how the children are feeling. Per our court order, my parenting time begins today at 5:00 PM. I will be at the designated exchange location at that time and look forward to seeing the children. If there is a concern about the children's wellbeing that prevents the exchange, please let me know in writing so I can share it with my attorney."

**Do Not Say:** "You're alienating my kids!" / "I'll call the police!" / "You always do this!" / Any accusation of manipulation.

**Why This Works:** You acknowledged the children's feelings (friendly), stated the court order (informative/firm), confirmed your intent to appear (creates documentation of your compliance), and invited a written explanation (creates a record if they fabricate a reason). A judge reading this sees a calm, cooperative parent.

**Follow Up:** Go to the exchange location. Document your arrival with a timestamped photo. Wait the full duration. File a contemporaneous log entry noting the denial.

### Scenario 2: Late Pickup or Drop-off
**Context:** Your co-parent is chronically late for exchanges, or sends a last-minute message about being late.

**They Say:** "Running late. Be there in an hour maybe. Deal with it."

**You Say:** "Thank you for the update. Our exchange time per the parenting plan is 6:00 PM. I will wait until 6:30 PM. If you are unable to arrive by then, please let me know so we can discuss an alternative arrangement for today. I want to make sure the transition goes smoothly for the children."

**Do Not Say:** "You're always late!" / "This is so disrespectful!" / "Fine, I guess the kids don't matter to you."

**Why This Works:** You set a clear, reasonable boundary (firm), acknowledged the message (friendly), and offered a practical solution window (informative). You documented that you were willing to wait but not indefinitely. Courts view chronic lateness as a pattern, and your calm responses build that record.

**Follow Up:** Log the late arrival with exact times. If they do not arrive by your stated deadline, send one follow-up: "It is now 6:30 PM and the exchange has not occurred. I am leaving the exchange location. Please contact me to reschedule." Save all messages.

### Scenario 3: Child Reports Badmouthing
**Context:** Your child tells you that the co-parent said negative things about you. You need to address this with the co-parent.

**They Say (via the child):** "Mom/Dad said you don't really love us" or "Mom/Dad says you're the reason we don't have money."

**You Say (to the co-parent):** "I want to make you aware that [Child] mentioned some concerns after their last visit that seem to relate to adult topics. I believe we both want the children to feel safe and loved by both parents. Can we agree to keep adult matters between us and speak positively about each other to the children? I appreciate your partnership in this."

**Do Not Say:** "Stop badmouthing me to the kids!" / "You're poisoning them against me!" / "The kids told me everything you said."

**Why This Works:** You did not accuse or quote the child directly (protecting the child from being caught in the middle). You framed it as a shared goal (friendly). You stated a clear expectation (firm) without escalating. If the badmouthing continues, this message shows the court that you attempted to address it cooperatively.

**Follow Up:** Document what the child said (date, time, exact words if possible) in your incident log. Do not interrogate the child. If the pattern continues, raise it with your attorney.

### Scenario 4: Unilateral Schedule Change Demands
**Context:** Your co-parent demands a schedule change without following the proper process outlined in your court order.

**They Say:** "I'm taking the kids next weekend instead. My family is visiting and they need to see them. This isn't up for discussion."

**You Say:** "I appreciate you wanting the children to spend time with extended family. Next weekend is my parenting time per our current order. I am happy to discuss a schedule swap if we can agree on a make-up time that works for both of us and the children. Would you like to propose a specific alternative weekend?"

**Do Not Say:** "No way, it's my weekend!" / "You can't just change the schedule whenever you feel like it!" / "Your family isn't my problem."

**Why This Works:** You validated the underlying desire (friendly), stated the existing order (informative), showed willingness to cooperate (friendly/firm), and put the ball in their court to propose a fair trade (firm). A judge sees flexibility and reasonableness.

**Follow Up:** If they refuse to propose a swap and simply take the children, document the violation. Do not engage in a back-and-forth. Contact your attorney.

### Scenario 5: Holiday Conflicts
**Context:** A dispute arises over holiday parenting time, which is often the most emotionally charged scheduling issue.

**They Say:** "The kids are spending Christmas with me this year. I already bought tickets to see my parents and I'm not changing it."

**You Say:** "I understand the holidays are important to both of us. Per our parenting plan, this year's Christmas allocation is [state what the order says]. I am committed to following the schedule as ordered. If you would like to discuss a modification for future years, I am open to that conversation at an appropriate time. I hope we can make this holiday season positive for the children."

**Do Not Say:** "You always try to steal the holidays!" / "I don't care about your plans!" / "The kids would rather be with me anyway."

**Why This Works:** You referenced the specific court order provision (informative), expressed willingness to plan ahead (friendly), and did not engage with the emotional provocation (firm). Holiday disputes are common in alienation cases; judges look for which parent follows the order.

**Follow Up:** If the co-parent violates the holiday schedule, document it calmly. Consider whether filing a motion for contempt is appropriate — discuss with your attorney.

### Scenario 6: Extracurricular Activity Decisions
**Context:** Your co-parent enrolled the child in an activity without consulting you, or objects to an activity you proposed.

**They Say:** "I signed [Child] up for travel soccer. Practices are on your weekends. You need to take them."

**You Say:** "Thank you for letting me know about [Child]'s interest in soccer. Our parenting plan requires that we consult each other before enrolling the children in extracurricular activities that affect both households' schedules. I would like to discuss this together so we can consider [Child]'s interests, the schedule impact, and the costs. Can we set up a time to talk about it this week?"

**Do Not Say:** "You had no right to sign them up without asking me!" / "I'm not driving them anywhere on my time!" / "You just did this to interfere with my parenting time."

**Why This Works:** You acknowledged the child's interest (friendly/child-centered), cited the court order requirement (informative), proposed a collaborative discussion (friendly/firm), and did not refuse outright or accuse. Courts favor parents who co-parent on decisions.

**Follow Up:** If the co-parent refuses to discuss, document the unilateral decision and consult your attorney about whether it constitutes a violation of joint decision-making provisions.

### Scenario 7: Medical Decisions
**Context:** Your co-parent made a medical decision for the child without consulting you, or is blocking your access to medical information.

**They Say:** "I took [Child] to the doctor and they're starting a new medication. You don't need to be involved."

**You Say:** "Thank you for letting me know about [Child]'s medical appointment. As a parent with legal custody, I have a right to be informed about and participate in medical decisions for our child. Could you please share the doctor's name, the diagnosis, and the medication details? I want to make sure I can support [Child]'s health consistently in both homes. Going forward, I would appreciate advance notice of medical appointments so I can attend or provide input."

**Do Not Say:** "You can't make medical decisions without me!" / "I'm going to call the doctor and overrule you!" / "You're not a medical expert — you don't know what's best."

**Why This Works:** You requested specific information (informative), asserted your legal rights without being aggressive (firm), framed everything around the child's health (child-centered), and set an expectation for future communication (firm). Medical gatekeeping is a recognized alienation behavior — documenting your requests builds your case.

**Follow Up:** Contact the medical provider directly with a copy of your custody order. Request to be added to the child's records as an authorized contact. Log the interaction.

### Scenario 8: Financial Disputes
**Context:** Your co-parent demands money outside of what the court order requires, or accuses you of not paying enough.

**They Say:** "You need to pay for [Child]'s new laptop for school. You owe me $800 by Friday or I'm going to tell the kids you don't care about their education."

**You Say:** "I want [Child] to have what they need for school. Per our court order, my child support obligations are current, and extraordinary expenses require mutual agreement. If you believe this is a shared expense, could you please send me the details in writing — what it is, the cost, and why it is needed — so I can review it? I am happy to discuss a fair arrangement."

**Do Not Say:** "I'm not your ATM!" / "Go ahead and tell the kids whatever you want!" / "You waste all the child support anyway."

**Why This Works:** You affirmed your support for the child (friendly), stated your compliance with the order (informative), requested documentation (firm), and did not take the bait on the emotional threat. Financial manipulation is common in high-conflict cases — responding with process protects you.

**Follow Up:** Keep all financial communication in writing. Maintain records of all payments. If the co-parent threatens to disparage you to the children over finances, note it in your log.

### Scenario 9: Child Refuses Contact — Messaging the Co-Parent
**Context:** Your child is refusing to speak to you or come to your parenting time, and you need to communicate with the co-parent about it.

**They Say (or you initiate):** The child has been refusing phone calls or visits for several weeks.

**You Say:** "I am concerned that [Child] has not been willing to speak with me or attend our scheduled parenting time for the past [timeframe]. I love [Child] and I am committed to maintaining our relationship. I would like to work together to understand what is happening and find a solution. Would you be open to involving a family therapist to help [Child] feel comfortable with both of us? I am happy to discuss options."

**Do Not Say:** "You're turning my child against me!" / "I know you're behind this!" / "If [Child] won't talk to me, I'm going to court."

**Why This Works:** You documented the pattern with a specific timeframe (informative), expressed your love and commitment (friendly), proposed a professional solution (firm/constructive), and did not accuse. If this message goes unanswered or is dismissed, it becomes powerful evidence that you sought cooperative solutions.

**Follow Up:** Continue sending age-appropriate, loving messages to your child regardless of whether they respond. Document each attempt. Discuss reunification therapy options with your attorney.

### Scenario 10: New Partner Introductions
**Context:** Either you or your co-parent is introducing a new romantic partner to the children, which often triggers conflict.

**They Say:** "I don't want [your partner's name] around my children. Keep them away or I'll go to the judge."

**You Say:** "I understand that new relationships can be an adjustment for everyone, especially the children. I am thoughtful about who I introduce to [Child] and how. Unless our court order specifies restrictions on introducing new partners, I will continue to use good judgment about what is in the children's best interest. If you have specific concerns about [Child]'s safety or wellbeing, I am willing to listen."

**Do Not Say:** "You can't tell me who to have around the kids!" / "You're just jealous!" / "Your new partner is way worse." / Any personal attacks on the co-parent's relationship status.

**Why This Works:** You validated that transitions affect children (friendly), asserted your rights (firm), referenced the court order (informative), and left the door open for legitimate safety concerns (reasonable). Courts generally do not restrict new partner introductions unless there is a documented safety concern.

**Follow Up:** Introduce new partners gradually, with the child's comfort as the priority. Do not involve a new partner in co-parenting communications. Document any threats the co-parent makes.

## Key Principles
- **The goal is to end the conflict, not win the argument.** BIFF responses are not about being right. They are about being effective.
- **Every message is evidence.** Assume a judge, a GAL, and a custody evaluator will read everything you write. Make them see a calm, child-focused, cooperative parent.
- **Do not JADE (Justify, Argue, Defend, Explain).** High-conflict individuals use your explanations as ammunition. State facts and move on.
- **Respond to the request, not the tone.** Strip away the insults and emotional language. Find the actual issue. Address only that.
- **When in doubt, do not send.** If you are unsure whether your response passes the BIFF test, wait. Ask a trusted friend, therapist, or attorney to review it first.
