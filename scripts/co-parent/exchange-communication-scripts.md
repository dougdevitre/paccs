# Custody Exchange Communication Scripts

> **Audience:** Targeted parents managing custody exchanges in high-conflict co-parenting situations
> **Situation:** Before, during, and after custody exchanges — including normal transitions, disrupted exchanges, and emotionally charged situations
> **Tone:** Calm, child-centered, neutral, factual
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-advocate

---

## Before You Begin
- **Arrive on time, every time.** Courts track patterns. Your consistency is your evidence.
- **Use a public exchange location when possible.** Police station lobbies, fire stations, and busy parking lots are ideal. Some jurisdictions have designated safe exchange sites.
- **Bring only what is needed.** Have the child's belongings packed and ready. Do not bring a new partner, family members, or anyone who might escalate tensions unless safety requires a witness.
- **Record if legally permitted.** Know your state's recording consent laws. In one-party consent states, consider audio or video recording exchanges. In all states, you can take timestamped photos of your arrival and departure.
- **Have your phone ready to document.** If an exchange is disrupted, immediately send a text or parenting app message summarizing what happened while details are fresh.

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: Normal Exchange
**Context:** A routine, uneventful custody exchange. Even when things go well, your words matter — children absorb everything.

**Say to the child (drop-off):** "I had a really great time with you this weekend. I love you. You're going to have a great time with Mom/Dad. I'll see you on [next scheduled day]."

**Say to the child (pickup):** "Hey, I'm so happy to see you! I've been looking forward to this. Let's get going — [mention a low-key plan like dinner or a favorite activity]."

**Say to the co-parent:** "Hi. [Child] is all set. Their backpack has [relevant items]. Have a good week." (Keep it short and neutral.)

**Do Not Say to the child:** "I'm going to miss you so much" (induces guilt) / "Tell me everything that happens at Mom/Dad's house" (makes them a spy) / "I wish you didn't have to go" (undermines the other parent).

**Do Not Say to the co-parent:** Anything beyond brief logistics. Do not discuss court matters, finances, or personal topics at exchanges.

**Follow Up:** Log the exchange as completed on time with no issues. Even positive exchanges should be documented to show your pattern of cooperation.

---

### Scenario 2: Late Exchange
**Context:** The co-parent is late for the exchange and you are waiting with or for the child.

**Say to the child (if waiting for co-parent to pick up):** "Mom/Dad is running a little behind today. That's okay — it happens sometimes. We'll hang out together until they get here. Want to [suggest a brief activity]?"

**Say to the child (if waiting for co-parent to drop off):** "I know you were supposed to be here by now. Sometimes things take a little longer than planned. I'm right here and I'm not going anywhere."

**Say to the co-parent (via text at the scheduled time):** "Hi, I'm at the exchange location. It's [time]. Just checking on your ETA so I can plan accordingly."

**Say to the co-parent (if 15+ minutes late with no communication):** "It is now [time] and the exchange has not occurred. I am at [location]. Please update me on your expected arrival so we can complete the transition for [Child]."

**Do Not Say to the child:** "Your Mom/Dad is late again" / "This always happens" / Any statement that assigns blame.

**Do Not Say to the co-parent:** "You're always late!" / "This is contempt of court!" / Anything emotional or accusatory.

**Follow Up:** Document the exact arrival times. Take a timestamped photo at the exchange location. If the co-parent is chronically late (3+ times), discuss filing a motion with your attorney.

### Scenario 3: Child Is Distressed at Exchange
**Context:** The child is crying, clinging, or expressing fear or reluctance during the transition.

**Say to the child (if the child is leaving you):** "I can see you're having some big feelings right now, and that's okay. I love you no matter what. You're going to be safe, and I will see you on [next scheduled day]. You can always think about me, and I'll be thinking about you too."

**Say to the child (if the child is arriving to you distressed):** "I'm here. You're safe. You don't have to talk about anything right now. Let's just [suggest a calming activity — get a snack, listen to music, take a walk]. Whenever you're ready to talk, I'm here to listen."

**Say to the co-parent:** "I can see [Child] is having a hard time with the transition today. Let's keep things calm and give them a moment. Is there anything I should know about how their day has been?"

**Do Not Say to the child:** "Why are you crying?" (interrogation) / "Did someone say something to upset you?" (leading) / "You don't have to go if you don't want to" (undermines the order and the other parent).

**Do Not Say to the co-parent:** "What did you do to them?" / "This always happens when they come from your house" / "You need to stop upsetting the children."

**Follow Up:** Document the child's behavior in your log (what you observed, not your interpretation). If distress at exchanges is a recurring pattern, discuss it with the child's therapist and your attorney. Do not question the child about what happened at the other home.

---

### Scenario 4: Co-Parent Is Confrontational at Exchange
**Context:** The co-parent begins arguing, yelling, making accusations, or behaving aggressively at the exchange.

**Say to the co-parent:** "I'm not going to discuss this here in front of the children. If you have a concern, please put it in writing and I'll review it. Let's complete the exchange so the children can move on with their day."

**If they continue:** "I've asked to discuss this another time. I'm going to complete the exchange now." (Then do so calmly and leave.)

**If you feel unsafe:** "I'm not comfortable continuing this exchange under these conditions. I am going to step back to my car. I will contact you to reschedule." (Then leave. Call 911 if threatened.)

**Say to the child (afterward):** "Sometimes grown-ups have disagreements, and that can feel uncomfortable. That wasn't your fault. Both Mom/Dad and I love you. How are you feeling?"

**Do Not Say to the co-parent:** Anything that engages with the content of their argument. Do not defend yourself, explain yourself, or match their energy.

**Do Not Say to the child:** "Your Mom/Dad was being crazy" / "I'm sorry they acted that way" / Any statement that positions you and the child against the co-parent.

**Follow Up:** Immediately document the incident: date, time, location, what was said, who was present, and the child's reaction. If you have video or audio, preserve it. If there was a threat or physical aggression, contact law enforcement and your attorney.

### Scenario 5: Child Refuses to Go
**Context:** The child states they do not want to go with the other parent at the exchange.

**Say to the child:** "I hear you, and your feelings matter. But right now, it's time to go with Mom/Dad, and I need you to do that. I love you, and I'll see you on [next day]. If something is bothering you, we can talk about it when we're together next time, or you can talk to [therapist name] about it."

**Say to the co-parent:** "I've encouraged [Child] to go with you. They seem to be having a hard time with the transition today. I want to support the exchange happening. Can you give them a minute?"

**If the child physically refuses and cannot be transitioned safely:** (To the co-parent) "I want this exchange to happen. [Child] is refusing to transition right now and I don't want to physically force them in a way that could be harmful. Can we give them 10-15 minutes to settle? If they still refuse, I think we should involve [therapist/parenting coordinator] to help."

**Do Not Say to the child:** "You don't have to go" / "I'll protect you" (implies the other parent is a threat) / "I'll talk to the judge about this" / "Just tell Mom/Dad why you don't want to go."

**Do Not Say to the co-parent:** "They don't want to go with you" (stated triumphantly) / "This is your fault" / "What are you doing to make them not want to come?"

**Follow Up:** Document exactly what the child said and did, what you said, and the outcome. This is a critical pattern in alienation cases. Your response must show the court that you actively encouraged the child to go, not that you supported their refusal.

### Scenario 6: Forgotten Items
**Context:** The child left belongings (medication, school supplies, comfort items, clothing) at the other parent's home, or the co-parent claims you did not send required items.

**Say to the co-parent (requesting items):** "Hi, [Child] mentioned they left their [item] at your home. Could you bring it to the next exchange or drop it off at [agreed location]? If it's [Child]'s medication, I'd appreciate it as soon as possible. Thank you."

**Say to the co-parent (if they accuse you of not sending items):** "I packed [Child]'s bag with [list of items]. If something is missing, I'm happy to replace it or send it at the next exchange. Please let me know what's needed."

**Say to the child:** "I know you left your [item] at Mom/Dad's. We'll get it back. In the meantime, we can [offer an alternative]. No big deal."

**Do Not Say to the co-parent:** "You should have checked the bag!" / "You always keep their stuff!" / "Buy your own — I'm not responsible for things at your house."

**Do Not Say to the child:** "Your Mom/Dad should have packed it" / "They always forget your stuff."

**Follow Up:** For essential items like medication, follow up in writing if the item is not returned promptly. Keep duplicates of critical items at both homes when possible (medications, school supplies, comfort objects).

---

### Scenario 7: Exchange Location Changes
**Context:** The co-parent requests or demands a change to the established exchange location, or circumstances require an adjustment.

**Say to the co-parent (if they request a change):** "I received your request to change the exchange location. Our current order designates [location] as the exchange point. I'm open to a temporary change if we can agree on a public location that works for both of us. What about [suggest a specific public place]? Please confirm by [time] so I can plan."

**Say to the co-parent (if you need to request a change):** "Due to [brief, factual reason — road closure, weather, car trouble], I'd like to request we use [specific alternative public location] for today's exchange. I apologize for the inconvenience and want to make sure the transition still happens on time."

**Say to the co-parent (if they demand a change and you do not agree):** "I understand you'd prefer a different location. Since our order specifies [location], I plan to be there at the scheduled time. If you'd like to request a permanent change, we can discuss it through our attorneys or parenting coordinator."

**Do Not Say:** "I'm not going out of my way for you!" / "Fine, wherever, I don't care" / Any agreement to meet at a private residence or isolated location if safety is a concern.

**Follow Up:** Any agreed location change should be confirmed in writing before the exchange. If you feel unsafe at any exchange location, discuss modifying the order with your attorney.

## Key Principles
- **The child is watching and listening.** Everything you say and do at an exchange shapes their sense of security. Be the calm parent.
- **Exchanges are not negotiation sessions.** Keep all substantive discussions out of the exchange. The only words spoken at an exchange should be brief logistics and warm words to the child.
- **Document everything, react to nothing.** Your documentation after the exchange is more powerful than any reaction during it.
- **Your job is to make the exchange happen.** Courts evaluate which parent facilitates the child's relationship with the other parent. Be the one who tries.
- **Safety is the one exception.** If you are in physical danger, you may leave. Document immediately and contact law enforcement. The child's safety and your safety come first.
