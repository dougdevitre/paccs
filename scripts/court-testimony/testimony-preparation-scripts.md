# Court Testimony Preparation Scripts

> **Audience:** Targeted parents preparing to testify in family court hearings, custody trials, or related proceedings
> **Situation:** When you will be taking the witness stand for direct examination, cross-examination, or presenting evidence of parental alienation to a judge
> **Tone:** Factual, composed, child-centered, credible
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-legal-advisory

---

## Before You Begin
- **Consult your attorney thoroughly.** Never testify without preparation with your attorney. These scripts supplement, not replace, that preparation.
- **Know the goal of your testimony.** You are not there to prove the other parent is a bad person. You are there to demonstrate that a pattern of behavior is harming your child's relationship with you, and that a specific remedy is in the child's best interest.
- **Dress for credibility.** Business professional or business casual. Conservative colors. No flashy jewelry. Clean and pressed. The judge should focus on your words, not your appearance.
- **Arrive early.** Be at the courthouse at least 30 minutes before your hearing. Bring water, identification, and a notepad. Do not bring your phone into the courtroom unless permitted.
- **Practice, but do not memorize.** Rehearse the structure of your answers with your attorney. Do not memorize exact phrases — judges and opposing counsel can tell when testimony is scripted.

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: How to Present Yourself — Demeanor and Tone

**General demeanor guidelines:**
- Sit up straight. Hands visible, resting on the witness stand or in your lap.
- Speak clearly and at a moderate pace. Do not rush.
- Address the judge as "Your Honor." Address attorneys by name or "Counsel."
- Look at the person asking the question when they ask it. Look at the judge when answering. (Confirm this practice with your attorney, as some jurisdictions differ.)
- Pause before answering every question. This gives your attorney time to object and gives you time to think.
- If you do not understand a question, say: "Could you please rephrase that?" Never guess at what is being asked.
- If you do not know the answer, say: "I don't recall" or "I don't have that information." Never fabricate.

**Emotional composure:**
- It is acceptable to show genuine emotion. Judges are human. A parent who tears up when discussing their child's rejection is credible.
- What is not acceptable: anger, sarcasm, eye-rolling, sighing, arguing with the attorney, or addressing the other parent directly.
- If you feel overwhelmed, take a breath and say: "I apologize, Your Honor. May I have a moment?" Then take a slow breath and continue.

### Scenario 2: Opening Statement Framing — What Judges Need to Hear

**Context:** Your attorney asks you to describe the situation. This is your opportunity to set the frame.

**Model opening (direct examination):**

Attorney: "Can you describe your relationship with [Child] and the concerns that bring you before the court today?"

**You Say:** "Your Honor, I am here because I am deeply concerned about my child's wellbeing. Before the separation, I was an involved, hands-on parent. I [describe 2-3 specific examples — coached their soccer team, put them to bed every night, helped with homework daily, attended every doctor's appointment]. My relationship with [Child] was close, warm, and consistent."

"Over the past [timeframe], that relationship has been significantly disrupted. My parenting time has been denied or interfered with on [number] occasions. My child has gone from a loving, affectionate child to one who refuses contact with me — and the reasons they give are not consistent with their age or our actual history. I have documented this pattern, and I am asking the court for [specific remedy — enforcement of the existing order, modification of custody, reunification therapy, appointment of a GAL, etc.] to protect [Child]'s right to a relationship with both parents."

**Key framing elements:**
- Start with the child, not with your grievance.
- Establish the baseline — what the relationship looked like before.
- Describe the change with specifics and timeline.
- State what you are asking for and why it serves the child.

**Do Not Say:** "The other parent is alienating my child." (Let the evidence show it.) / "I'm the better parent." / "The children should be with me." / "My ex is [any diagnosis]."

### Scenario 3: How to Answer Direct Examination Questions

**Context:** Your own attorney is asking you questions to present your case.

**Model Q&A:**

Q: "How many times has the court-ordered parenting time been denied in the past six months?"
A: "Based on my documentation, my parenting time was denied or significantly interfered with on 14 occasions between September and February."

Q: "Can you give a specific example?"
A: "On October 12, my parenting time was scheduled to begin at 5:00 PM. I arrived at the exchange location at 4:55 PM. At 5:10, I received a text from the other parent saying, 'The kids don't want to come.' I responded asking to speak with the children, and the other parent did not reply. I waited at the exchange location until 6:00 PM. The children were not brought to the exchange. I have the text messages and a timestamped photograph of my arrival."

Q: "How has this affected your child?"
A: "Before this pattern began, [Child] would run to greet me at exchanges. They would call me every day. They drew pictures for me and talked about things we would do together. Now, [Child] avoids eye contact, repeats phrases like '[exact adult phrase],' and told me on [date] that they 'don't need two parents.' This is language that a [age]-year-old would not typically use independently."

**Guidelines for direct examination:**
- Answer the question that was asked — nothing more, nothing less.
- Use dates, numbers, and specifics whenever possible.
- Reference documentation: "I have records that show..." / "The parenting app shows..."
- Describe behaviors and statements, not your interpretation of them.
- When discussing the child, center their experience, not your pain.

### Scenario 4: How to Handle Cross-Examination

**Context:** The opposing attorney is asking you questions designed to undermine your credibility, provoke an emotional response, or create contradictions.

**Core rules:**
1. Listen to the entire question before answering.
2. Pause. Breathe. Then answer.
3. Answer only what was asked. Do not volunteer information.
4. If the question is a yes-or-no question, answer yes or no. If you need to explain, say: "Yes, but I'd like to provide context if I may."
5. If the question contains a false premise, address the premise: "I don't agree with the way that question is framed. What actually happened is..."
6. Never argue with the attorney. They want you to lose your temper.

**Common cross-examination tactics and responses:**

**Tactic: Leading with a false premise.**
Q: "Isn't it true that you have anger management issues?"
A: "No, that is not true. I have never been diagnosed with anger management issues, and there are no incidents of anger-related behavior in my record."

**Tactic: Twisting a partial truth.**
Q: "You were late to an exchange on June 5, correct?"
A: "I arrived at 5:12 PM for a 5:00 PM exchange. I had communicated in advance that I would be approximately 10 minutes late due to traffic. The other parent was informed and acknowledged the message."

**Tactic: "Isn't it possible" framing.**
Q: "Isn't it possible that your child simply prefers to be with their mother/father?"
A: "I believe every child benefits from a relationship with both parents. My concern is not about preference but about the documented pattern of interference that has disrupted my child's ability to maintain our relationship. Before this pattern began, [Child] expressed strong attachment to me."

**Tactic: Provoking emotion.**
Q: "If you really cared about your children, why did you [leave / file for divorce / move / etc.]?"
A: "I have always cared about my children, and every decision I have made has been with their wellbeing in mind. [If appropriate: Briefly state the factual reason for the action.] My commitment to my children is demonstrated by my consistent efforts to maintain my parenting time, my involvement in their education and healthcare, and my presence here today."

**Tactic: Rapid-fire questions.**
A: "I'd like to take a moment to make sure I answer accurately." (Slow the pace. You control the speed of your answers.)

### Scenario 5: How to Present Evidence of Alienation

**Context:** You need to present a pattern of alienating behavior in a way the court finds credible and compelling.

**Framework — the four elements judges need to see:**

1. **Baseline relationship:** "Before [date/event], my relationship with [Child] was [describe with specifics]. I was involved in [list parenting activities]. I have [photos, school records, medical records, testimony from others] that demonstrate this."

2. **Pattern of interference:** "Since [date/event], I have documented [number] instances of interference with my parenting time. These include [summarize categories — denied exchanges, blocked communication, exclusion from school/medical, disparaging statements]. Each instance is documented with [type of evidence]."

3. **Child's behavioral change:** "[Child]'s behavior has changed in ways that are inconsistent with normal developmental stages. Specifically: [list observable changes — rejection without ambivalence, use of adult language, rejection extending to extended family, parroting specific phrases, absence of positive memories]. The timing of these changes correlates with [specific event or pattern]."

4. **Remedy requested:** "I am asking the court to [specific request]. This is in [Child]'s best interest because [explanation focused on the child's right to both parents, emotional development, and the research on outcomes for children who lose a parental relationship]."

**Do Not Say:** "This is textbook alienation." / "Any expert would agree." / "The research proves it." (Let your attorney and any experts present the clinical framework. Your job is to present the facts.)

### Scenario 6: Common Questions and Model Answers

**Q: "What kind of parent are you?"**
A: "I am an involved, loving parent. Before the current situation, I was responsible for [specific daily parenting tasks]. I attended every school event, every doctor's appointment, and every extracurricular activity. I continue to make every effort to be present in my child's life, despite the obstacles I've faced."

**Q: "Why do you think your child doesn't want to see you?"**
A: "I believe my child is caught in a loyalty conflict that no child should have to experience. The statements my child makes and the language they use suggest they are being influenced by adult perspectives they would not develop independently. Before the separation, there was no indication of any problem in our relationship."

**Q: "Have you done anything that might have contributed to the relationship problems?"**
A: "No parent is perfect, and I have reflected seriously on my own behavior. I have made mistakes — [acknowledge 1-2 minor, genuine shortcomings without self-incrimination]. But none of those things explain the complete rejection I'm experiencing from a child who previously had a loving, secure attachment to me. This change was sudden and correlates with specific events in the custody conflict."

**Q: "What would you do if the court gave you more parenting time?"**
A: "I would focus on rebuilding my child's sense of safety and security in our relationship. I would follow any therapeutic recommendations. I would not speak negatively about the other parent to my child. I would maintain a stable, predictable routine. And I would continue to cooperate with the court and any professionals involved."

**Q: "How do you co-parent with the other parent?"**
A: "I use a parenting app for all communication. I respond to messages within 24 hours. I follow the court-ordered schedule. When changes are requested, I try to accommodate them when I can. I have made repeated efforts to co-parent cooperatively, and I have the documentation to show that."

### Scenario 7: What NOT to Say in Court

**Never say:**
- "They're a narcissist / sociopath / abuser." (Diagnostic labels without expert testimony hurt your credibility.)
- "I know they're lying." (The judge decides credibility, not you.)
- "Everyone knows they're the problem." (Unsubstantiated generalizations.)
- "My child told me [hearsay]." (Unless your attorney has established a hearsay exception, the judge may strike it.)
- "I want to destroy them / make them pay / get revenge." (Immediately disqualifies you as the reasonable parent.)
- "This judge / this court / the system is unfair." (Alienates the decision-maker.)
- "I refuse to answer that." (Unless your attorney instructs you to invoke a privilege, answer the question.)
- Anything about the other parent's personal life that is not directly relevant to the child's welfare.
- Any statement beginning with "I feel like..." when the question asks for facts. (Separate feelings from facts in your testimony.)

### Scenario 8: How to Maintain Composure When Hearing Lies

**Context:** The other parent or their witnesses testify with statements you know to be false.

**What to do:**
- **Remain physically still.** Do not shake your head, mouth words, make faces, or react visibly. The judge is watching you as much as the witness.
- **Take notes.** Write down the false statement, the time, and a brief note about the evidence that contradicts it. Pass the note to your attorney if needed.
- **Breathe.** Slow, deep breaths through the nose. Focus on keeping your hands still and your expression neutral.
- **Trust your attorney.** Your attorney will have the opportunity to cross-examine and to call rebuttal witnesses. The lies will be addressed through the process.
- **Remind yourself of the goal.** You are not here to react to lies. You are here to present the truth. Let the contrast between your composure and the other parent's behavior speak for itself.

**If you are on the stand and the opposing attorney references something false:**
A: "That is not accurate. What actually occurred is [brief factual correction]. I have documentation that supports this."

**If you feel yourself about to lose composure:**
A: "Your Honor, I apologize. This is a deeply personal matter, and I want to make sure I answer accurately. May I take a moment?" (Then breathe, collect yourself, and continue.)

**Do Not:** Interrupt the other parent's testimony. Roll your eyes. Whisper loudly to your attorney. Storm out. Break down in a way that prevents you from continuing. These reactions are natural, but they are damaging in court.

## Key Principles
- **Credibility is everything.** In family court, there is often limited physical evidence. The judge is making decisions based on who they believe. Every word, every reaction, every piece of body language affects your credibility.
- **Facts beat emotions.** Judges hear emotions every day. What persuades them is organized, specific, documented facts presented by a calm, credible witness.
- **The child is the center of every answer.** Frame every response around the child's wellbeing, the child's rights, and the child's best interest. The parent who speaks about the child wins. The parent who speaks about themselves or the other parent loses.
- **Preparation is the antidote to anxiety.** The more you prepare with your attorney, the less anxious you will feel. Anxiety causes rambling, defensiveness, and emotional reactions — all of which hurt your case.
- **You cannot control the outcome, but you can control your conduct.** Present the truth clearly, behave with dignity, and let the process work. Regardless of the outcome of any single hearing, your conduct on the record is permanent.
