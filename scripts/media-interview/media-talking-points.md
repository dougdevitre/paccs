# Media Appearance Preparation and Talking Points

> **Audience:** PACCS representatives, advocates, and affected parents preparing for media appearances (interviews, podcasts, panel discussions, op-eds)
> **Situation:** When you are speaking publicly about parental alienation, family court reform, or PACCS's mission in any media format
> **Tone:** Authoritative, compassionate, evidence-based, non-partisan
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-policy-education

---

## Before You Begin
- **Know your role.** Are you speaking as a PACCS representative, as a professional, or as a parent sharing lived experience? This determines what you can and should say.
- **Coordinate with PACCS leadership.** If you are representing PACCS, ensure your talking points are aligned with current organizational messaging. Review any media policies or guidelines.
- **Protect your privacy and your case.** If you have an active custody case, consult your attorney before any media appearance. Anything you say publicly can be used in court.
- **Research the outlet and interviewer.** Know the audience, the format (live vs. recorded, length, other guests), and whether the interviewer has covered this topic before. Look for potential adversarial angles.
- **Practice with a trusted person.** Do a mock interview. Have someone ask the hardest possible questions. Refine your answers until they are concise and compelling.

## Core PACCS Messages — Key Talking Points

Use these five messages as the foundation for any media appearance. Return to them whenever a question goes off-track.

### Talking Point 1: Children Need Both Parents
"The research is clear and consistent: children thrive when they have meaningful, ongoing relationships with both parents. When a child is systematically turned against a loving parent, the harm to that child is measurable and long-lasting. This is a child welfare issue."

### Talking Point 2: Parental Alienation Is a Pattern of Behavior, Not a Custody Strategy
"Parental alienation is not a legal tactic or a theoretical concept. It is a documented pattern of behavior in which one parent undermines the child's relationship with the other parent. It includes badmouthing, limiting contact, interfering with communication, and creating conditions where the child feels they must choose sides. It harms the child most of all."

### Talking Point 3: The System Needs Reform
"Our family court system was designed to protect children, but in too many cases, it enables the very harm it was meant to prevent. Alienating behaviors go unrecognized, unaddressed, or even rewarded. We need better training for judges, attorneys, evaluators, and mental health professionals, and we need laws that hold parents accountable for interference with the parent-child relationship."

### Talking Point 4: This Affects All Demographics
"Parental alienation crosses every demographic line — it affects mothers and fathers, all races, all income levels, all family structures. This is not a gender issue. It is a child protection issue. PACCS supports all parents and all children."

### Talking Point 5: Evidence-Based Solutions Exist
"We are not starting from scratch. There is a growing body of research, proven intervention models, and successful legislative frameworks from around the world. What we need is the will to implement them. PACCS is working to bring evidence-based solutions to families, courts, and communities."

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: Bridging Phrases for Difficult Questions
**Context:** The interviewer asks a question that is off-topic, loaded, or designed to provoke. Use bridging phrases to redirect to your message.

**Bridging phrases:**
- "That's an important question, and it connects to a broader point, which is..."
- "I understand why people ask that. What the research actually shows is..."
- "That's one perspective. What we see in the data and in families' lived experiences is..."
- "I want to make sure we address the core issue here, which is..."
- "Let me put that in context..."
- "The real question we should be asking is..."
- "I appreciate you raising that. Here's what we know..."
- "That's a common misconception, and I'm glad we can address it. The reality is..."

---

### Scenario 2: How to Share Personal Experience Safely
**Context:** You are sharing your own story as a targeted parent or advocate.

**Framework for safe personal sharing:**
1. **Set boundaries before the interview.** Tell the producer or interviewer in advance what you will and will not discuss. State clearly: "I will not name my co-parent, my children, or any professionals involved in my case."
2. **Speak in patterns, not specifics.** "I experienced years of denied visitation" is safer than "On March 15, my ex refused to let me see my daughter."
3. **Focus on impact, not incidents.** "The hardest part is the silence — knowing your child is out there and you can't reach them" is more compelling and safer than recounting a specific confrontation.
4. **Use "I" statements.** "I went through..." / "I experienced..." / "What I learned was..." Keep the focus on your experience, not accusations.
5. **Connect your story to the larger issue.** "My story is not unique. Thousands of parents experience exactly what I went through. That's why systemic change matters."

**Say:** "I'm sharing my experience not because my case is special, but because it represents a pattern that is happening to families across this country. When I lost contact with my child, it wasn't because I was a bad parent. It was because the system didn't have the tools to recognize what was happening."

**Do Not Say:** Your child's name or identifying details. / Your co-parent's name. / Specific case details that could identify your family. / Anything that contradicts statements you have made in court. / Anything your attorney has not approved.

### Scenario 3: Statistics and Research to Cite
**Context:** Supporting your statements with evidence-based data.

**Key statistics and sources:**
- "Studies estimate that 10-15% of divorces involving children include elements of parental alienation, and in high-conflict custody disputes, the prevalence is significantly higher." (Source: Baker, A.J.L., research on alienation prevalence)
- "Children who are alienated from a parent are at elevated risk for depression, anxiety, substance abuse, low self-esteem, and difficulty forming healthy relationships in adulthood." (Source: Baker & Ben-Ami, 2011, Adult Recall of Childhood Psychological Maltreatment)
- "The American Professional Society on the Abuse of Children (APSAC) has identified parental alienation as a form of child psychological maltreatment." (Source: APSAC Practice Guidelines)
- "Parental alienation is recognized in the ICD-11 under 'Caregiver-child relationship problem' (QE52.0), and contact with mental health services in the context of family breakdown is classified under QE50-QE52." (Source: World Health Organization, ICD-11)
- "Research by Dr. Jennifer Harman and colleagues has documented that parental alienating behaviors affect approximately 22 million American parents." (Source: Harman, Kruk, & Hines, 2018, Children and Youth Services Review)
- "Studies on reunification programs show that structured therapeutic interventions can successfully restore parent-child relationships in the majority of cases when implemented with appropriate authority and compliance." (Source: Warshak, R.A., Family Court Review, research on reunification camps and programs)
- "Multiple countries — including Brazil, Mexico, Italy, and others — have enacted specific legislation addressing parental alienation, reflecting growing international recognition of the issue." (Source: Comparative family law research)

**When citing research:** "According to peer-reviewed research published in [journal]..." is more credible than "Studies show..." Always offer to provide citations to the interviewer or outlet after the interview.

### Scenario 4: Do-Not-Say List
**Context:** Words, phrases, and framings that should be avoided in any media appearance.

**Never say:**
- "Parental alienation syndrome" (the word "syndrome" is contested and gives critics an easy target; say "parental alienation" or "alienating behaviors")
- "My ex is a narcissist / sociopath / abuser" (diagnostic labels without clinical authority undermine credibility)
- "Fathers' rights" or "mothers' rights" exclusively (frame as children's rights and both-parents' rights)
- "Family court is corrupt" (say "the system needs reform" or "there are structural gaps")
- "Judges don't care" (say "judges often lack the specialized training needed to recognize these dynamics")
- "All allegations are false" (never make blanket statements about allegations; say "false allegations do occur and we need better tools to distinguish them from genuine safety concerns")
- "We need to punish alienating parents" (say "we need accountability measures that prioritize the child's wellbeing")
- Specific case details, names of parties, or identifying information about any family
- Anything that could be construed as minimizing genuine abuse or domestic violence
- Political party affiliations or framing the issue as partisan

### Scenario 5: Sample Interview Q&A — 10 Common Questions with Model Answers

**Q1: "What is parental alienation?"**
A: "Parental alienation is a pattern of behavior in which one parent systematically undermines a child's relationship with the other parent. It can include badmouthing, limiting contact, creating loyalty conflicts, and making the child feel they have to choose one parent over the other. The result is a child who rejects a parent they previously loved — not because of anything that parent did, but because of the dynamic they've been placed in."

**Q2: "Is parental alienation real? Some people say it's not scientifically recognized."**
A: "Parental alienation is recognized in the ICD-11, which is the World Health Organization's diagnostic classification system. It has been the subject of decades of peer-reviewed research. Multiple professional organizations, including the American Professional Society on the Abuse of Children, have addressed it in practice guidelines. The question is no longer whether it exists — it's whether our systems are equipped to respond to it."

**Q3: "Isn't this just about parents who are angry about divorce?"**
A: "Divorce is painful for everyone, but what we're talking about goes far beyond normal post-separation adjustment. We're talking about children who completely reject a loving parent, who lose contact with entire branches of their family, and who suffer measurable psychological harm. This isn't about adult feelings. It's about children being caught in a dynamic that damages their development and their ability to form healthy relationships."

**Q4: "Doesn't the concept of parental alienation get used to silence abuse victims?"**
A: "This is a critically important concern, and it's one PACCS takes seriously. Genuine abuse must always be investigated, taken seriously, and prosecuted. Parental alienation and abuse are not mutually exclusive — they can and do co-occur. What we advocate for is thorough, evidence-based assessment that can distinguish between a child who is genuinely afraid of a parent because of abuse and a child who has been conditioned to reject a parent through alienating behaviors. Both situations require intervention, and getting the assessment wrong harms the child either way."

**Q5: "What does PACCS do?"**
A: "PACCS is a multidisciplinary organization focused on protecting children's emotional and psychological safety. We provide evidence-based resources for families, training for legal and mental health professionals, support for legislative reform, and advocacy for parents and children affected by alienation. Our approach is child-centered, research-driven, and inclusive of all family structures."

**Q6: "What should courts be doing differently?"**
A: "Three things. First, every family court judge and attorney should receive specialized training on high-conflict custody dynamics, including alienation. Second, courts need better assessment tools — forensic evaluations that are designed to identify alienating patterns, not just surface-level interviews. Third, there need to be meaningful consequences for parents who violate court orders and interfere with their child's relationship with the other parent. Right now, in too many jurisdictions, the worst that happens is another court date."

**Q7: "What would you say to a parent who is going through this right now?"**
A: "First, you are not alone. What you're experiencing is happening to millions of parents. Second, document everything — your parenting attempts, your communication, the patterns you observe. Third, get professional support — a family law attorney with alienation experience, a therapist who understands high-conflict dynamics, and a community that gets it. And fourth, do not give up on your child. Even when they push you away, they need to know that you never stopped trying."

**Q8: "How does this affect children long-term?"**
A: "The research is sobering. Children who are alienated from a parent are at elevated risk for depression, anxiety, substance abuse, relationship difficulties, and identity problems in adulthood. Many alienated children, as adults, eventually recognize what happened and seek to reconnect with the rejected parent — but the lost years cannot be recovered. The earlier we intervene, the better the outcomes for the child."

**Q9: "Is this a gender issue? Is it mostly mothers or fathers who alienate?"**
A: "The research shows that alienating behaviors are perpetrated by both mothers and fathers. This is not a gender issue — it is a child welfare issue. PACCS advocates for children's right to both parents, regardless of the gender of the alienating or targeted parent. Framing this as a men's issue or a women's issue divides people who should be working together to protect children."

**Q10: "What can the public do to help?"**
A: "Educate yourselves and your communities. If you know a parent going through this, listen without judgment. Support legislative reform in your state — contact your representatives and ask them what they are doing to protect children from parental alienation. And if you are a professional — a teacher, therapist, doctor, judge, attorney — learn to recognize the signs. You may be the first person to see what is happening. Your awareness can change a child's life."

## Key Principles
- **Stay on message.** Media appearances are not conversations — they are opportunities to deliver your key points. Use every question as a doorway to your talking points.
- **Humanize the issue without exploiting it.** Personal stories are powerful, but they must be shared with dignity, privacy, and purpose. Never use a child's story for shock value.
- **Anticipate the counterargument.** Every talking point should have a response to the strongest objection. Practice these in advance.
- **Credibility is cumulative.** One thoughtful, well-prepared media appearance is worth more than ten emotional, off-the-cuff interviews. Be the voice of reason and evidence.
- **Every appearance is permanent.** Assume that everything you say will be recorded, quoted, and searchable forever. Speak accordingly.
