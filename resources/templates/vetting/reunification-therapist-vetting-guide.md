# Reunification Therapist Vetting Guide

> **Audience:** Targeted parents, attorneys, guardians ad litem
> **Purpose:** Help families evaluate whether a reunification therapist or program is qualified, ethical, and capable of facilitating genuine parent-child reunification after alienation.
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-reunification

---

## Why Vetting Matters

Not every therapist who offers "reunification therapy" is qualified to provide it. Parental alienation cases require specialized knowledge that most graduate programs do not teach. An unqualified or biased therapist can delay reunification, reinforce alienation dynamics, or cause further harm to the child and targeted parent.

This guide helps you identify competent professionals and avoid harmful ones.

---

## 12 Vetting Questions to Ask

Ask these questions during an initial consultation. Pay attention to both the content of the answer and the therapist's demeanor when answering.

### 1. "What is your training and experience specifically with parental alienation cases?"

**Good Answer:** Names specific training programs, workshops, or certifications in alienation dynamics. Can cite a number of alienation cases they have handled. References recognized researchers or frameworks (e.g., Gardner's criteria, Childress's model, Warshak's research, Baker's studies, Bernet's work).

**Bad Answer:** Vague references to "high-conflict families" without distinguishing alienation from general conflict. Claims alienation "is not a real diagnosis." Cannot name specific training or cases.

### 2. "Do you recognize parental alienation as a legitimate dynamic?"

**Good Answer:** Yes, unequivocally. May reference the ICD-11 inclusion (caregiver-child relationship problem), the body of peer-reviewed research, or professional consensus that alienation is a well-documented pattern of behavior.

**Bad Answer:** Dismisses it as "controversial," "debunked," or "just a custody tactic." States that children always have valid reasons for rejecting a parent. Says they "do not take sides on that debate."

### 3. "What reunification model or approach do you use?"

**Good Answer:** Can describe a structured, phased approach with specific components (assessment, graduated contact, therapist-facilitated sessions, monitoring). May reference established programs or models. Describes a clear methodology, not just "talk therapy."

**Bad Answer:** Cannot articulate a model. Describes general family therapy without alienation-specific adaptations. Says "every case is different" without being able to describe any structure.

### 4. "How do you handle a child's expressed resistance to contact?"

**Good Answer:** Differentiates between alienation-driven resistance and genuine safety concerns. Explains that resistance in alienation cases is expected and that canceling contact reinforces the alienation. Describes strategies for working through resistance while supporting the child.

**Bad Answer:** Says they "follow the child's lead." States the child should not be "forced" into contact. Treats the child's stated preference as decisive without assessment. Equates a child's resistance with a therapeutic contraindication.

### 5. "Will both parents participate? What is each parent's role?"

**Good Answer:** Describes distinct roles: the targeted parent participates in sessions with the child; the alienating parent has specific obligations to support the process, cease alienating behaviors, and attend their own therapeutic work. Both parents are held accountable, but accountability is not symmetrical because the problem is not symmetrical.

**Bad Answer:** Treats both parents identically as though the conflict is mutual. Requires the targeted parent to "take responsibility" for the child's rejection. Does not address the alienating parent's behavior at all.

### 6. "How do you prevent the alienating dynamic from continuing during therapy?"

**Good Answer:** Describes concrete measures: structuring sessions to prevent coaching or interference; monitoring communication between the alienating parent and child; coordinating with the court to enforce orders; addressing alienating behaviors directly when they occur; limiting the alienating parent's ability to sabotage the process.

**Bad Answer:** Has no plan. Relies entirely on the alienating parent's "good faith." Avoids labeling any behavior as alienating. Says they "work with both parents equally."

### 7. "What does your typical reunification timeline look like?"

**Good Answer:** Provides a general framework (e.g., "most cases involve 4-6 months of graduated contact, with the goal of normalized parenting time"). Explains that faster timelines are generally better because delay reinforces alienation. Adjusts based on severity but has benchmarks.

**Bad Answer:** No timeline. Says "it takes as long as it takes" without benchmarks. Describes an open-ended process with no milestones. Suggests years of therapy before meaningful contact.

### 8. "How do you measure progress?"

**Good Answer:** Describes specific, observable indicators: child's comfort level during visits, spontaneous positive interactions, reduction in scripted language, willingness to engage in activities, eventual independent contact. Uses structured assessments or progress tools.

**Bad Answer:** Cannot articulate progress indicators. Relies on subjective impressions. Measures progress solely by the child's stated feelings about the targeted parent (which in alienation cases will be negative for an extended period).

### 9. "Will you provide reports to the court?"

**Good Answer:** Yes. Provides regular written progress reports. Willing to testify if needed. Understands that court accountability is essential to the process. Describes what reports include (attendance, progress, compliance, concerns).

**Bad Answer:** Refuses to communicate with the court. Cites "confidentiality" as a reason to avoid all reporting (note: court-ordered treatment typically includes reporting provisions). Is unwilling to document non-compliance by either parent.

### 10. "What are your policies on communication with attorneys and GALs?"

**Good Answer:** Has a defined protocol for communication with all professionals involved. Provides updates to attorneys and GALs as appropriate. Understands their role within the larger legal and professional framework.

**Bad Answer:** Will only communicate with one parent's attorney. Refuses to coordinate with other professionals. Has no system for keeping the team informed.

### 11. "What happens if the alienating parent does not comply with the process?"

**Good Answer:** Has a non-compliance protocol: documents the behavior, reports to the court, recommends consequences, and does not allow non-compliance to derail the process. Understands that reunification cannot succeed without the alienating parent being held accountable.

**Bad Answer:** Shrugs it off. Says they "cannot control what happens outside of sessions." Has no plan. Reduces or cancels the targeted parent's contact as a response to the alienating parent's sabotage.

### 12. "Have you ever recommended a custody change to protect a child from ongoing alienation?"

**Good Answer:** Has experience recommending — or at minimum, is willing to recommend — custody modifications when alienation is severe and the alienating parent will not stop. Understands that in severe cases, removing the child from the alienating environment may be the only effective intervention.

**Bad Answer:** Has never recommended it and is unwilling to consider it. Believes custody should never change regardless of the severity of alienation. Views such recommendations as "extreme" in all cases.

## Red Flags in Reunification Programs

If you observe any of the following, the program or therapist may be harmful to your case.

### 1. The therapist allows the child to "choose" whether to have a relationship.

This is child-led severance, not reunification. In alienation cases, the child's expressed preference is a symptom of the problem, not a treatment goal.

### 2. The program has no defined timeline or milestones.

Open-ended therapy without structure benefits the alienating parent by creating indefinite delay. Time works against reunification.

### 3. The alienating parent controls the pace or terms of reunification.

If the alienating parent can slow down, pause, or terminate the process, the reunification is effectively under their control — and it will fail.

### 4. The therapist has not assessed for alienation dynamics.

Any competent reunification professional must begin with an assessment that specifically evaluates for alienation. Without this, treatment is not targeted and may address the wrong problem.

### 5. There is no accountability structure for the alienating parent.

If the alienating parent has no obligations, no consequences for non-compliance, and no behavioral expectations, the process is not reunification — it is theater.

### 6. The therapist blames the targeted parent for the child's rejection.

Statements like "you need to understand why your child does not want to see you" or "what did you do to cause this" place the burden on the victim. This is a fundamental misunderstanding of alienation dynamics.

### 7. The program relies solely on the child's stated preferences without context.

A child who has been subjected to alienation will express a preference to reject the targeted parent. Treating this preference as an independent, informed decision is clinically negligent.

### 8. There is no coordination with the court or other professionals.

Reunification exists within a legal framework. A therapist who operates in isolation from the court, the GAL, and other professionals cannot be effective and may actively undermine the case.

### 9. The therapist requires the targeted parent to "earn" the child's trust.

This framework places the burden of proof on the victim and implies the targeted parent is responsible for the alienation. In a healthy model, trust is rebuilt through structured contact, not through the targeted parent performing for the child.

### 10. Sessions are consistently canceled or rescheduled without explanation.

Repeated cancellations — especially when initiated by the alienating parent or the child — are a sign that the alienating dynamic is controlling the process.

## Green Flags: What to Look For

These indicate a competent, ethical reunification professional:

- **Specific alienation training.** Can name courses, certifications, or mentors.
- **Structured approach.** Has a clear phased model with timelines and milestones.
- **Court integration.** Works within the legal framework and provides reports.
- **Balanced but not neutral.** Holds both parents accountable, but recognizes that alienation is not a symmetrical problem.
- **Child-centered but not child-led.** Prioritizes the child's long-term wellbeing over the child's short-term stated preference.
- **Comfortable confronting alienating behavior.** Does not avoid the core issue to maintain a therapeutic alliance with the alienating parent.
- **Experience with resistance.** Has worked through a child's initial rejection and knows it is expected and manageable.
- **Willingness to recommend difficult interventions.** Including custody modification if the alienation is severe.
- **Clear communication.** Provides regular updates to all parties and the court.
- **Understands urgency.** Recognizes that delay harms the child and acts accordingly.

## Questions to Ask About Their Approach

These deeper questions help you understand the therapist's philosophy and methodology.

**On Philosophy:**
- "Do you believe a child has the right to reject a safe parent?"
- "How do you define 'child-centered' in the context of alienation?"
- "What role do you believe the court should play in reunification?"

**On Methodology:**
- "Walk me through what the first four sessions would look like."
- "How do you handle it when the child is coached before a session?"
- "What activities do you use in sessions and why?"

**On Accountability:**
- "How do you document non-compliance?"
- "What do you recommend when the alienating parent sabotages the process?"
- "Have you ever terminated a reunification because the alienating parent would not comply? What happened?"

**On Outcomes:**
- "What percentage of your reunification cases have resulted in restored contact?"
- "What does success look like to you?"
- "What does failure look like, and what causes it?"

## How to Report Concerns About Unethical Practitioners

If you believe a reunification therapist is behaving unethically, you have several options.

### 1. Document Everything

Before reporting, document the concerning behavior:
- Dates and details of problematic sessions or statements
- Communications (emails, letters, reports) that demonstrate the issue
- Impact on your case and your child

### 2. Report to Their Licensing Board

Every licensed therapist is regulated by a state licensing board:
- **LCSW (Social Workers):** State Board of Social Work Examiners
- **LPC / LMFT (Counselors / Marriage and Family Therapists):** State Board of Professional Counselors or equivalent
- **Psychologists (PhD, PsyD):** State Board of Psychology
- **Psychiatrists (MD):** State Medical Board

Search "[your state] + [license type] + licensing board + complaint" to find the correct filing process. Complaints are typically free and can be filed online.

### 3. Notify the Court

If the therapist was court-ordered, file a motion or request a hearing to address your concerns. Provide specific, documented examples. Ask your attorney to present the issues.

### 4. Notify the GAL

If a guardian ad litem is assigned, report your concerns to them with documentation. The GAL can investigate and make recommendations to the court.

### 5. Contact Professional Organizations

File complaints with relevant professional organizations:
- American Psychological Association (APA)
- National Association of Social Workers (NASW)
- American Association for Marriage and Family Therapy (AAMFT)

### 6. Seek a Second Opinion

Request that the court appoint a new reunification therapist or allow you to retain an independent expert to evaluate the current therapist's approach.
