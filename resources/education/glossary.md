# PACCS Master Concept Glossary

> **Audience:** All — parents, professionals, legislators, advocates, educators
> **Purpose:** Standardized definitions of key terms used across PACCS materials
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-research-integrity

---

## A

### Access Denial
**Definition:** Blocking, limiting, or interfering with a parent's court-ordered or legal right to spend time with their child.
**Context:** Includes "forgetting" exchanges, scheduling conflicts during parenting time, leaving town without notice, and refusing to make the child available.
**Related Terms:** Contact denial, Custodial interference, Alienating behaviors

### Alienating Behaviors
**Definition:** A pattern of actions by one parent that damages or destroys the child's relationship with the other parent.
**Context:** Includes denigration, access denial, emotional manipulation, false allegations, institutional manipulation, enmeshment, and other tactics. Identified across 10 categories in PACCS documentation.
**Related Terms:** Parental alienation, Denigration, Enmeshment

### Alienating Parent
**Definition:** The parent who engages in alienating behaviors that undermine the child's relationship with the other parent.
**Context:** Also referred to as the "favored parent" or "aligned parent" in clinical literature. Behavior may be conscious or unconscious.
**Related Terms:** Targeted parent, Parental alienation

### Ambiguous Grief
**Definition:** Grief experienced when a loved one is physically alive but psychologically absent — or present but emotionally unavailable.
**Context:** Targeted parents experience ambiguous grief because their child is alive but inaccessible. This type of grief is particularly difficult because there is no closure or culturally recognized mourning process.
**Related Terms:** Targeted parent, Reunification

---

## B

### BIFF Method
**Definition:** A communication framework for high-conflict situations: Brief, Informative, Friendly, Firm.
**Context:** Used in co-parent communication to keep exchanges documented, professional, and non-escalatory. Widely recommended by family law professionals.
**Related Terms:** Co-parent communication, Documentation

### Borrowed Scenarios
**Definition:** When a child recounts events they could not have personally witnessed or experienced, using language or details that originate from the alienating parent.
**Context:** One of the 8 behavioral indicators of alienation in children. Example: a 6-year-old describing financial disputes in adult legal terminology.
**Related Terms:** Independent thinker phenomenon, Alienating behaviors

---

## C

### Child-Centered Safety
**Definition:** A framework that prioritizes the child's emotional, psychological, and physical wellbeing above all other considerations in custody and family law decisions.
**Context:** The foundational principle of PACCS, organized around Five Pillars: Emotional Safety, Psychological Health, Truth & Evidence, Protection from System Abuse, and Family Preservation & Healing.
**Related Terms:** Five Pillars, Best interests of the child

### Coercive Control
**Definition:** A pattern of behavior that seeks to take away the victim's liberty or freedom through intimidation, degradation, isolation, and control.
**Context:** In custody contexts, coercive control manifests as controlling the other parent's access to the child, financial abuse through litigation, isolation from support networks, and weaponizing institutions.
**Related Terms:** Litigation abuse, Institutional manipulation

### Contact Denial
**Definition:** Preventing or obstructing a parent's communication or physical contact with their child.
**Context:** Includes blocking phone calls, not passing messages, intercepting mail or gifts, refusing video calls, and withholding the child from scheduled parenting time.
**Related Terms:** Access denial, Alienating behaviors

### Custody Evaluation
**Definition:** A formal assessment conducted by a qualified mental health professional to evaluate family dynamics, parenting capacity, and the best interests of the child.
**Context:** In alienation cases, evaluations must assess for alienating behaviors, not just parenting capacity. Evaluators should have specific alienation training.
**Related Terms:** Forensic evaluation, Guardian ad Litem

### Custodial Interference
**Definition:** A criminal offense in most states that occurs when a person takes, detains, conceals, or entices a child in violation of a custody order.
**Context:** Ranges from misdemeanor (first offense, child withheld) to felony (fleeing jurisdiction, international abduction). Federal law applies to international cases (18 U.S.C. § 1204).
**Related Terms:** Access denial, Contact denial

---

## D

### Denigration
**Definition:** Badmouthing, belittling, or speaking negatively about the targeted parent to the child or in the child's presence.
**Context:** One of the 10 alienating behavior categories. May be direct ("Your father doesn't love you") or indirect (eye-rolling, dismissive tone when the other parent is mentioned).
**Related Terms:** Campaign of denigration, Alienating behaviors

---

## E

### Enmeshment
**Definition:** An unhealthy emotional boundary pattern where the child is drawn into the alienating parent's emotional world, mirroring their views, grievances, and loyalty expectations.
**Context:** The child loses their own autonomous perspective and becomes psychologically fused with the alienating parent. The child may act as a confidant, emotional support, or proxy in the parental conflict.
**Related Terms:** Parentification, Role reversal

### Estrangement
**Definition:** A child's rejection of a parent that is proportionate to and caused by that parent's own harmful behavior (abuse, neglect, harsh parenting).
**Context:** Distinguished from alienation by the presence of substantiated harmful behavior by the rejected parent, age-appropriate descriptions of negative experiences, and absence of a campaign by the favored parent.
**Related Terms:** Parental alienation, Differential diagnosis

---

## F

### False Allegations
**Definition:** Fabricated or exaggerated claims of abuse, neglect, or safety concerns made against a parent, typically in the context of custody disputes.
**Context:** Used as an alienating tactic to gain custody advantage, restrict the targeted parent's access, and damage their credibility. A false allegation is itself evidence of alienation.
**Related Terms:** Alienating behaviors, Coercive control

### Family Preservation
**Definition:** The principle that children have a right to safe relationships with both parents, and that interventions should aim to preserve and repair family bonds rather than sever them.
**Context:** The fifth of the PACCS Five Pillars. Supports ethical, evidence-based reunification and non-coercive therapeutic approaches.
**Related Terms:** Reunification, Five Pillars

### Five Pillars (PACCS)
**Definition:** The PACCS framework for child-centered safety: (1) Emotional Safety, (2) Psychological Health, (3) Truth & Evidence, (4) Protection from System Abuse, (5) Family Preservation & Healing.
**Context:** All PACCS materials, recommendations, and advocacy positions align with these five pillars.
**Related Terms:** Child-centered safety

### Forensic Evaluation
**Definition:** A specialized psychological evaluation conducted for legal proceedings, applying clinical methodology to questions before the court.
**Context:** In alienation cases, forensic evaluators assess both parents, the child, collateral contacts, and documentary evidence to determine the presence and severity of alienation.
**Related Terms:** Custody evaluation, Guardian ad Litem

---

## G

### Guardian ad Litem (GAL)
**Definition:** A court-appointed advocate for the child's best interests in custody proceedings.
**Context:** GALs investigate family dynamics, interview parties, and make recommendations to the court. In alienation cases, it is critical that the GAL has alienation-specific training and does not default to the child's stated preference without assessing for manipulation.
**Related Terms:** Custody evaluation, Best interests of the child

---

## I

### Independent Thinker Phenomenon
**Definition:** When an alienated child insists that their rejection of a parent is entirely their own decision, made independently and without influence.
**Context:** One of the 8 behavioral indicators of alienation. The child may say "Nobody told me to feel this way" while displaying all other hallmarks of programmed rejection. This presentation is itself a symptom of successful alienation.
**Related Terms:** Borrowed scenarios, Alienating behaviors

### Institutional Manipulation
**Definition:** Using schools, doctors, therapists, or other institutions to exclude a parent, reinforce the alienation narrative, or gain tactical advantage.
**Context:** Includes enrolling in a new school without the other parent's knowledge, selecting a therapist who only hears one side, or instructing providers not to share information.
**Related Terms:** Alienating behaviors, Coercive control

---

## L

### Legal Abuse / Litigation Abuse
**Definition:** Weaponizing the court system through excessive, frivolous, or strategic filings designed to exhaust, bankrupt, or punish the other parent.
**Context:** Includes filing repeated motions, seeking unnecessary emergency orders, refusing to comply with discovery, delaying proceedings, and using court appearances as opportunities for further alienation.
**Related Terms:** Coercive control, Alienating behaviors

---

## P

### Parental Alienation
**Definition:** A dynamic in which one parent systematically undermines and damages the child's relationship with the other parent through a pattern of alienating behaviors, resulting in the child's unwarranted rejection of the targeted parent.
**Context:** Recognized in the DSM-5 (V61.20, V61.29), ICD-11 (QE52.0), and extensive clinical literature. Constitutes a form of psychological child abuse.
**Related Terms:** Alienating behaviors, Targeted parent, Alienating parent

### Parentification
**Definition:** Inappropriately assigning adult roles or responsibilities to a child, such as making them a confidant, messenger, emotional support, or decision-maker in parental conflicts.
**Context:** A form of role reversal that is psychologically harmful to the child. In alienation cases, the child may be burdened with the alienating parent's grievances, financial worries, or legal strategy.
**Related Terms:** Role reversal, Enmeshment

### Psychological Abuse
**Definition:** A pattern of behavior that harms a child's emotional development, sense of self, and psychological wellbeing.
**Context:** Parental alienation constitutes psychological abuse under the DSM-5 (V995.51 / T74.32 — Child Psychological Abuse, Confirmed). The child is weaponized against their own parent, causing lasting harm to attachment, identity, and trust.
**Related Terms:** Parental alienation, Child-centered safety

---

## R

### Reunification
**Definition:** The process of restoring and rebuilding a parent-child relationship after a period of alienation, separation, or contact denial.
**Context:** Ethical reunification is court-ordered, professionally guided, evidence-based, and graduated. It is the child's right, not a punishment. Reunification is a process, not a single event.
**Related Terms:** Family preservation, Targeted parent

### Role Reversal
**Definition:** When the normal parent-child dynamic is inverted, with the child taking on the role of caretaker, protector, or emotional support for the alienating parent.
**Context:** The child may monitor the targeted parent's behavior, report back to the alienating parent, or feel responsible for the alienating parent's emotional wellbeing.
**Related Terms:** Parentification, Enmeshment

---

## S

### Safety Plan
**Definition:** A structured document outlining steps a parent will take in various crisis scenarios related to alienation, including emergency contacts, documentation procedures, and self-care strategies.
**Context:** Every targeted parent should have a safety plan covering contact denial, false allegations, child in danger, and institutional manipulation scenarios.
**Related Terms:** Crisis response, Documentation

---

## T

### Targeted Parent
**Definition:** The parent who is the target of alienating behaviors — the parent the child is being turned against.
**Context:** Also referred to as the "rejected parent" or "alienated parent." The targeted parent is a victim of the alienation, along with the child.
**Related Terms:** Alienating parent, Parental alienation

### Trauma-Informed
**Definition:** An approach that recognizes the presence and impact of trauma, avoids re-traumatization, and prioritizes safety, trust, and empowerment.
**Context:** All PACCS materials and interactions are trauma-informed. When working with families affected by alienation, practitioners must handle stories with care, respect boundaries, and create psychologically safe environments.
**Related Terms:** Child-centered safety, Psychological abuse
