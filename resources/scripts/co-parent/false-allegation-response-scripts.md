# False Allegation Response Scripts

> **Audience:** Targeted parents who have been falsely accused of abuse, neglect, or other misconduct in the context of a custody dispute
> **Situation:** When false allegations have been made against you and you need to communicate with attorneys, investigators, CPS, family, and the accusing parent
> **Tone:** Factual, measured, cooperative, evidence-based
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-crisis-safety

---

## Before You Begin
- **Do not panic.** False allegations in custody disputes are a documented tactic. Your response strategy matters more than the allegation itself.
- **Contact your attorney immediately.** Do not speak to investigators, CPS, or law enforcement without legal counsel. This is not optional.
- **Do not contact the accusing parent about the allegation.** Anything you say will be used against you. Communicate only through your attorney.
- **Preserve all evidence.** Do not delete any texts, emails, social media posts, photos, or videos. Begin organizing your documentation immediately.
- **Do not discuss the allegation on social media.** Not a single post, comment, or vague reference. Silence on social media is your strongest position.

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: Talking to Your Attorney — First Call
**Context:** You have just learned about the false allegation and need to inform your attorney or retain one immediately.

**Say:** "I have been accused of [specific allegation — state it factually]. I believe this allegation is false. I need your guidance on how to respond. Here is what I know so far: [who made the allegation, when you learned about it, what agency or authority is involved, any documents you have received]. I have not spoken to investigators or the other parent about this. What are my immediate next steps?"

**Key Questions to Ask Your Attorney:**
- "Should I proactively contact CPS or law enforcement, or wait for them to contact me?"
- "What documentation should I gather immediately?"
- "Are there any temporary orders or emergency motions I should anticipate?"
- "Should I request a polygraph, psychological evaluation, or forensic interview for my child?"
- "What are the risks if I cooperate fully versus assert my Fifth Amendment rights?"
- "Who should I designate as character witnesses?"

**Do Not Say:** "This is obviously made up — no one will believe it." (Never assume.) / "I want to sue them for defamation." (Not the priority right now.) / "I have nothing to hide." (True, but your attorney needs to guide the process.)

**Follow Up:** Provide your attorney with all relevant documentation within 24 hours. Create a timeline of events leading up to the allegation.

---

### Scenario 2: Speaking with Investigators (with Attorney Present)
**Context:** Law enforcement or a detective contacts you about the allegation. Your attorney has advised you to cooperate.

**Say:** "Thank you for taking the time to investigate this thoroughly. I want to cooperate fully, and I have my attorney present to ensure the process goes smoothly. I welcome a complete investigation because I am confident the evidence will demonstrate that this allegation is not supported by facts. I am prepared to answer your questions."

**When answering questions:**
- Answer only what is asked. Do not volunteer additional information.
- "That is correct." / "That is not correct." / "I don't recall the specific date, but I can check my records and provide it."
- "I have documentation that addresses that point. My attorney can provide it to you."
- "I would like to clarify — [brief, factual clarification]."

**Say if you feel a question is misleading or unfair:** "I want to make sure I answer accurately. Could you rephrase that?" or "My attorney and I would like a moment to discuss before I answer that question."

**Do Not Say:** "She/he is lying!" / "This is all about the custody case." / "I would never do that — I'm a great parent." / Anything speculative. / Anything about the other parent's character.

**Follow Up:** Request a copy of any written statement you provide. Document the interview (date, time, who was present, topics covered) immediately afterward with your attorney.

### Scenario 3: Communicating with CPS / Child Protective Services
**Context:** A CPS investigator contacts you for an investigation or home visit.

**Say (initial contact):** "Thank you for reaching out. I want to cooperate with your investigation. I'd like to schedule a time that works for both of us, and I would like my attorney to be present or available by phone. When would be a good time?"

**Say (during the visit/interview):** "I appreciate you being thorough. I have nothing to hide, and I want to give you whatever you need to complete your assessment. Here is my home — you're welcome to look around. My child's room is [here]. I have documentation of my parenting time, school involvement, and medical care if you'd like to see it."

**If CPS asks about the co-parent or the custody dispute:** "There is an active custody case. I'd prefer to let the evidence speak for itself rather than characterize the other parent. I have documentation that may be relevant, and my attorney can provide context about the custody proceedings."

**If CPS asks to interview your child:** "I want to be cooperative. Please coordinate with my attorney regarding the time and setting for a child interview so it can be done in a way that's least stressful for my child."

**Do Not Say:** "My ex made this up to win custody." (Even if true, let the evidence show it.) / "CPS is wasting taxpayer money." / "I know my rights — you can't come in without a warrant." (Technically true in some states, but resistance creates a negative impression.) / Anything negative about the other parent unless directly asked a specific factual question.

**Follow Up:** Request the CPS caseworker's name, contact information, and case number. Ask about the investigation timeline. Provide any requested documentation promptly through your attorney.

### Scenario 4: Talking to Friends and Family
**Context:** People in your life have heard about the allegation, and you need to address it without compromising your case.

**Say:** "I know you may have heard something about an allegation made against me in connection with the custody case. I want you to know that the allegation is false, and I am working with my attorney to address it through the proper legal channels. I can't discuss the details right now because there is an active investigation, and I need to protect the process and my child's privacy. I appreciate your support and understanding."

**If they press for details:** "I know it's frustrating that I can't share more. My attorney has advised me not to discuss specifics while the investigation is active. What I can tell you is that I am cooperating fully and I am confident the truth will come out. Your support means everything to me right now."

**If they offer to help:** "Thank you. The most helpful thing you can do right now is [be available as a character witness if my attorney contacts you / help with childcare logistics / just be someone I can call when I'm having a hard day]. Please don't discuss the situation on social media or with people outside our close circle."

**Do Not Say:** Specific details of the allegation. / "My ex is a sociopath/narcissist." / Anything that could be repeated and distorted. / Anything about your legal strategy.

**Follow Up:** Identify 3-5 people who can serve as character witnesses (people who have observed your parenting firsthand). Inform your attorney of who you have spoken with and what you said.

---

### Scenario 5: What NOT to Say to Anyone
**Context:** Universal guidance on statements that can damage your case regardless of who hears them.

**Never say any of the following:**
- "I'll destroy them for this." (Interpreted as a threat.)
- "They'll pay for what they've done." (Interpreted as retaliation.)
- "Nobody will believe them." (Arrogance undermines credibility.)
- "I can't believe CPS is even investigating this." (Dismissiveness suggests lack of concern for the child.)
- "My kid knows the truth." (Implies you have coached the child or expect them to testify for you.)
- "This is all about money/custody/control." (Even if true, it sounds like you are minimizing a child welfare concern.)
- "I want to take a lie detector test." (Discuss with your attorney first — results are generally inadmissible and can backfire.)
- Any detailed account of the allegation shared casually, in texts, on social media, or to people who are not your attorney.
- Anything negative about the accusing parent to your child. Absolutely nothing. Ever.

---

### Scenario 6: Responding to the Accusing Parent
**Context:** The co-parent contacts you about the allegation directly, often to gauge your reaction or provoke a response they can use against you.

**They Say:** "How could you do that to our child?" / "CPS is going to take the kids away from you." / "Everyone knows what you did."

**You Say:** Nothing about the allegation. If you must respond (because the message also contains a logistics question), address only the logistics.

**Example:** They send: "After what you did, I'm not sending the kids this weekend. CPS agrees with me." You respond: "Per our court order, my parenting time begins Saturday at 10:00 AM. I will be at the exchange location at that time. Any concerns about the children's safety should be directed to the appropriate authorities. Please direct all other communications to my attorney, [attorney name], at [contact info]."

**Do Not Say:** "You know this is a lie." / "I'm going to prove you made this up." / "You're going to regret this." / Any emotional response whatsoever.

**Follow Up:** Screenshot and save every message. Do not delete anything, even if it is painful to read. Forward provocative messages to your attorney.

---

### Scenario 7: Social Media Guidance
**Context:** Managing your online presence during a false allegation investigation.

**What to post:** Nothing about the case. Nothing about the investigation. Nothing about your co-parent. Nothing about custody, family court, CPS, or parenting struggles. Nothing vague that could be interpreted as referencing the situation.

**What is safe to post:** Normal life content that shows stability — meals you cooked, activities you did (without children visible unless your attorney approves), professional accomplishments, community involvement.

**What to do with existing posts:** Do not delete anything. Deletion can be interpreted as destruction of evidence. If your attorney advises taking your accounts private, follow their guidance.

**If someone posts about the allegation publicly:** Do not respond. Do not comment. Do not engage. Contact your attorney. If the post constitutes defamation, your attorney can advise on appropriate action.

**If a supportive friend or family member posts something about your case:** Contact them privately and ask them to take it down. Explain that any public discussion, even supportive, can compromise your case.

**Script for asking someone to remove a post:** "I really appreciate your support, and it means the world to me. However, my attorney has advised that any public discussion of my case — even positive — could be used against me. Would you mind taking that post down? I know it comes from a good place, and I'm grateful."

## Key Principles
- **Silence is your strongest weapon.** In the face of false allegations, every unnecessary word is a risk. Let your evidence and your attorney speak for you.
- **Cooperation demonstrates confidence.** Parents who cooperate with investigations signal they have nothing to hide. Parents who obstruct signal the opposite.
- **The investigation is the path to vindication.** A thorough, fair investigation is your ally. Do everything you can to support it, not impede it.
- **Protect your child above all.** Your child did not choose this. Never discuss the allegation with them, never ask them to take sides, and never make them feel responsible for what is happening.
- **Document the pattern.** False allegations rarely exist in isolation. They are part of a broader pattern of alienating behavior. Your documentation of that pattern is critical to your defense.
