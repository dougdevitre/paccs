# Attorney Meeting Preparation Scripts

> **Audience:** Targeted parents preparing for meetings with their family law attorney
> **Situation:** First consultations, ongoing case updates, pre-hearing preparation, and difficult conversations about cost and strategy
> **Tone:** Organized, concise, professional, goal-oriented
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-legal-advisory

---

## Before You Begin
- **Prepare a written summary before every meeting.** Attorneys bill by the hour. Every minute you spend rambling is money spent on information they could have read in advance.
- **Organize documents chronologically.** Bring a timeline, not a pile. Label exhibits. Use tabs or folders.
- **Write down your questions in advance.** Prioritize them. You may run out of time — make sure the most important questions come first.
- **Bring a notebook.** Take notes during the meeting. You will not remember everything your attorney says.
- **Be honest about everything.** Your attorney cannot help you if you withhold information. Tell them the worst things your co-parent might say about you. No surprises in court.

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: First Consultation
**Context:** You are meeting an attorney for the first time to discuss your custody case. This may be your current attorney or one you are evaluating.

**Opening Statement (keep to 2-3 minutes):**
"Thank you for meeting with me. I'm going through a high-conflict custody situation involving [brief summary — e.g., 'my co-parent is systematically interfering with my relationship with my children']. I have [number] children, ages [ages]. We have been [separated/divorced] since [date]. Our current custody arrangement is [describe briefly]. The primary issues I need help with are [list 2-3 key issues — e.g., 'denied visitation, false allegations, and parental alienation']. I have a timeline and documentation with me."

**Questions to Ask:**
1. "What is your experience with high-conflict custody cases, specifically involving parental alienation or contact denial?"
2. "What is your approach to presenting alienation claims in court? How do you frame them for judges who may be skeptical?"
3. "In your experience, what evidence is most persuasive to judges in these types of cases?"
4. "What is the typical timeline for a case like mine from filing to resolution?"
5. "Do you recommend involving a Guardian ad Litem, custody evaluator, or parenting coordinator? Why or why not?"
6. "What are your fees, billing structure, and retainer requirements? Do you offer payment plans?"
7. "How do you prefer to communicate — email, phone, portal? What is your typical response time?"
8. "Who else in your office will work on my case? Will I always deal with you directly?"
9. "What is the best-case and worst-case realistic outcome for my situation?"
10. "Is there anything I should be doing right now to strengthen my position?"

**Do Not Say:** "I just want to destroy my ex." / "Money is no object." / "I've already figured out the legal strategy — I just need you to file it." / "My last three attorneys were all terrible."

**Follow Up:** After the consultation, write down your impressions while they are fresh. Compare if you are meeting multiple attorneys. Choose based on experience with alienation cases, communication style, and realistic expectations — not on who tells you what you want to hear.

### Scenario 2: Ongoing Case Update Meeting
**Context:** You have new incidents to report and need to update your attorney efficiently.

**Opening:** "Since our last meeting on [date], there have been [number] significant developments I need to update you on. I've organized them in writing with supporting documentation. Here's a summary."

**Structure for presenting each incident:**
1. **Date and time:** "On [date] at [time]..."
2. **What happened:** One to two factual sentences.
3. **Evidence:** "I have [text messages / photos / witness / parenting app records] documenting this."
4. **Impact on the child:** "The effect on [Child] was [describe observable behavior]."
5. **Your response:** "I responded by [what you did — keeping it BIFF-aligned]."
6. **Question:** "Should we include this in our filing? Does this change our strategy?"

**Example:** "On March 15 at 5:00 PM, my co-parent did not appear at the exchange location for my scheduled parenting time. I waited 45 minutes and sent two text messages, which went unanswered. I have timestamped photos of my arrival and the text messages. This is the fourth denied exchange in six weeks. My child later told me they were told I 'didn't feel like coming.' Should we file a contempt motion, or are we building toward a modification?"

**Do Not Say:** Long, emotional narratives about how the incident made you feel. / "They always do this" without specifics. / Unorganized complaints without documentation.

**Follow Up:** Email a copy of your written summary and documentation to your attorney after the meeting for their file.

### Scenario 3: Pre-Hearing Preparation
**Context:** You have a court hearing coming up and need to prepare with your attorney.

**Say:** "I want to make sure I'm fully prepared for the hearing on [date]. Can we walk through the following?"

**Questions to cover:**
1. "What is the specific issue before the court at this hearing?"
2. "What is our goal for this hearing — what outcome are we asking the judge for?"
3. "What evidence will we be presenting? In what order?"
4. "Will I be testifying? If so, what questions will you ask me, and what should I expect on cross-examination?"
5. "What is the other side likely to argue, and how do we counter it?"
6. "Are there any witnesses being called — by us or by them?"
7. "What should I wear and how should I present myself?"
8. "Where should I look when answering questions — at you, at the judge, at the other attorney?"
9. "What are the judge's tendencies in cases like ours? Is there anything I should know about this specific judge?"
10. "What is the worst thing the other side could bring up, and how should I handle it?"
11. "If the ruling does not go our way, what are our next steps?"

**Practical preparation:**
- "Can we do a brief practice run of my direct examination?"
- "Are there phrases or topics I should absolutely avoid?"
- "Should I bring any documents or exhibits to court, or will you handle all of that?"

**Do Not Say:** "I'm sure we'll win." / "The judge will see right through them." / "I'll just speak from the heart." (Preparation wins hearings, not spontaneity.)

**Follow Up:** Review your testimony preparation notes the night before. Get a full night of sleep. Arrive at the courthouse 30 minutes early. Bring water, a notepad, and your composure.

### Scenario 4: "I Can't Afford This Anymore"
**Context:** Legal fees are overwhelming and you need to have an honest conversation about costs.

**Say:** "I need to be transparent with you about my financial situation. I've spent [amount] so far on this case, and my resources are [limited/running low/exhausted]. I am fully committed to my children and to this case, but I need to discuss how we can proceed in a way that is financially sustainable. Can we talk about options?"

**Questions to ask:**
1. "Can we prioritize the most critical motions and defer less urgent ones?"
2. "Are there any tasks I can do myself to reduce billable hours — such as organizing documents, drafting initial timelines, or communicating with providers?"
3. "Is a payment plan available for the remaining retainer or upcoming costs?"
4. "Are there any legal aid organizations, pro bono resources, or reduced-fee programs for parents in my situation?"
5. "Would you recommend limited-scope representation — where you handle court appearances but I handle some paperwork?"
6. "What happens to my case if I need to take a pause in active litigation? How do I protect my position in the meantime?"
7. "Is it worth exploring mediation or a parenting coordinator to resolve some issues outside of court?"

**Do Not Say:** "This is a waste of money." / "You're charging too much." / "If you really cared about kids, you'd do this for free." / "I'll just represent myself." (without careful consideration)

**Follow Up:** If your attorney offers a modified approach, get the agreement in writing. Research local legal aid resources, law school clinics, and organizations that support alienated parents. Some nonprofit organizations offer financial assistance for custody cases.

### Scenario 5: Questions About Strategy and Timeline
**Context:** You feel the case is moving too slowly, or you disagree with your attorney's approach.

**Say:** "I want to understand our overall strategy and timeline better. I have some questions about the direction we're heading."

**Questions to ask:**
1. "Can you walk me through our strategy from now through resolution? What are the major milestones?"
2. "What is the realistic timeline for [specific goal — e.g., restoring parenting time, completing the evaluation, getting a final order]?"
3. "I've read about [specific legal strategy — e.g., filing for contempt, requesting a change of custody, seeking a forensic evaluation]. Is that appropriate for my case? Why or why not?"
4. "I feel like the other side is escalating faster than we are responding. Is there a reason for the pace we're maintaining?"
5. "Are there any motions we should be filing now that we're not? What are the pros and cons?"
6. "I've been documenting [specific pattern]. When does it become strong enough to present to the court?"
7. "If this case went to trial today, how would you assess our position?"
8. "Is there a point at which we should consider settling versus continuing to litigate? What would that look like?"

**Do Not Say:** "Why aren't you doing anything?" / "I read online that we should file [motion]." / "My friend's attorney did [X] and it worked." / "I don't think you're fighting hard enough."

**Follow Up:** Take notes on your attorney's reasoning. If you still disagree after hearing their rationale, you have the right to seek a second opinion from another attorney. A good attorney will not be offended by this.

## Key Principles
- **Your attorney is your partner, not your therapist.** Save emotional processing for your support system. Use legal meetings for legal strategy.
- **Preparation saves money.** The more organized you are, the less time your attorney spends sorting through information — and the lower your bill.
- **Trust the process, but stay engaged.** You should understand every filing, every motion, and every decision. Ask questions until you do.
- **Honesty is non-negotiable.** Your attorney needs to know the truth — all of it, including the parts that make you look bad. They cannot prepare for what they do not know.
- **Documentation is currency.** The more organized, timestamped, and factual your records are, the stronger your attorney's hand in court.
