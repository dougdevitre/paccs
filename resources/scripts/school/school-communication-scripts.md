# School Communication Scripts

> **Audience:** Targeted parents navigating school interactions when a co-parent has excluded them or the school is unaware of their custodial rights
> **Situation:** Asserting parental rights at school, requesting both-parent communication, addressing exclusion, and responding to school-reported concerns about a child
> **Tone:** Professional, cooperative, assertive, solution-oriented
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-policy-education

---

## Before You Begin
- **Know your legal rights.** Under the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), both parents with legal custody have the right to access their child's educational records, regardless of the custody arrangement, unless a court order specifically restricts access.
- **Obtain certified copies of your custody order.** Bring them to every school meeting and provide copies to the front office, the principal, and your child's teacher(s).
- **Approach the school as a partner, not an adversary.** Schools are often caught in the middle. Most staff want to do the right thing — they may simply not know the full picture.
- **Put everything in writing.** Follow up every verbal conversation with an email summarizing what was discussed and agreed upon. This creates a paper trail.
- **Do not involve the child.** Never ask your child to carry messages to school staff, hand over documents, or advocate for your involvement. That is your responsibility as the adult.

## Scripts

### Scenario 1: Asserting Parental Rights — Enrollment and Records Access
**Context:** You discover your child has been enrolled in a school without your knowledge, or you have been denied access to records.

**Say (to the school administrator):** "Good morning. My name is [Name] and I am the [mother/father] of [Child], who is enrolled at this school. I have joint legal custody, and I'd like to ensure that I'm listed as a parent in your system and have full access to my child's educational records. I have a certified copy of our custody order here for your records. I'd also like to receive copies of all communications that go to the other parent — report cards, progress reports, newsletters, event invitations, and any disciplinary or special education notices."

**If the school says they were told you should not have access:** "I understand someone may have communicated that, but I want to clarify my legal rights. Under FERPA, both parents with legal custody have equal access to educational records unless a court order specifically restricts that access. My custody order does not contain such a restriction, as you can see here. I'd like to be added to all distribution lists and contact records immediately. Can you help me with that today?"

**If they ask you to take it up with the other parent:** "I appreciate the suggestion, but my legal rights to access school records are independent of the other parent's cooperation. The school's obligation under FERPA is to both parents. I'm not asking for anything unusual — just the same access and communication that any custodial parent receives."

**Do Not Say:** "My ex has been hiding the kids' school from me!" / "They're alienating me from the school too!" / Anything that puts school staff in the middle of the parental conflict.

**Follow Up:** Send a follow-up email to the principal confirming what was discussed, attaching a copy of your custody order, and requesting written confirmation that you have been added to all contact and distribution lists.

### Scenario 2: Requesting Both-Parent Communication
**Context:** The school has been sending information only to the other parent, and you need to establish direct communication.

**Say (to the teacher):** "Thank you for meeting with me. I want to be actively involved in [Child]'s education. I'd like to receive all communications directly — not through the other parent. This includes grades, assignments, teacher notes, behavioral reports, and any concerns you may have about [Child]. Could I provide you with my email and phone number to be added as a primary contact?"

**Say (to the front office):** "I'd like to set up my parent portal account for [Child]'s records. I'm [Child]'s [mother/father] with joint legal custody. If the system allows two parent accounts, I'd like to activate mine. Here is my custody order and my identification."

**If the school says they only communicate with one parent:** "I understand that may be your current practice, but both parents with legal custody are entitled to receive information directly from the school. Routing communication through only one parent creates an opportunity for important information to not reach me, which could affect [Child]'s educational support. I'm asking for my own direct line of communication — it doesn't affect the other parent's access at all."

**Do Not Say:** "The other parent never tells me anything." / "They're deliberately keeping me out of the loop." / Any request for the school to stop communicating with the other parent.

**Follow Up:** Test the communication channels. If you do not receive the next scheduled communication (report card, newsletter, etc.), follow up promptly in writing.

### Scenario 3: Addressing Exclusion from Events and Conferences
**Context:** You were not informed about a parent-teacher conference, school play, sports event, or other school activity that the other parent attended.

**Say (to the teacher or administrator):** "I recently learned that there was a [parent-teacher conference / school event] on [date] that I was not informed about. I'd like to make sure I'm included in all future event notifications. I'm also happy to attend separate conferences if that is more comfortable for the school or if it would be better for [Child]. Can we set up a system to ensure I'm notified of everything going forward?"

**If the school says the other parent was supposed to tell you:** "I appreciate that, but I can't rely on the other parent to relay information to me. I need direct notification from the school. Would it be possible to add me to the email list, parent portal, and any event notification systems? I want to be present for [Child] — that's all I'm asking."

**Requesting a separate conference:** "If a joint conference is difficult given the family situation, I'm happy to schedule a separate meeting with the teacher. I'd like to discuss [Child]'s academic progress, social development, and any concerns you may have. I want to be an informed and involved parent."

**Do Not Say:** "They deliberately excluded me!" / "I demand to know why I wasn't invited!" / Any statement that suggests the school is conspiring with the other parent.

**Follow Up:** Request that the school put you on all automated notification systems. Ask for a specific contact person you can reach out to if you notice a gap in communication.

### Scenario 4: Responding to Teacher Concerns About the Child
**Context:** A teacher or school counselor has raised concerns about your child's behavior, emotional state, or academic performance.

**Say:** "Thank you for bringing this to my attention. I take [Child]'s wellbeing very seriously. Can you help me understand what you've been observing — specifically when the behavior started, how often it occurs, and whether you've noticed any patterns in timing?"

**If the behavior correlates with custody transitions:** "I want to share some relevant context without going into too much detail about the family situation. [Child] is going through a significant transition at home. I've noticed that their behavior tends to change around custody exchanges. Is there anything the school can do to provide additional support during these transitions — such as access to the school counselor or a safe space to decompress?"

**If the school reports concerning statements by the child:** "Thank you for telling me. Can you share what [Child] said as exactly as you can remember? I want to make sure the appropriate people are aware. I'd like to discuss this with [Child]'s therapist and potentially with my attorney. Could you also document what was said, including the date and context?"

**If the school implies one parent is the problem:** "I appreciate your concern. I'd prefer to keep the focus on how we can support [Child] at school rather than assigning blame. Both parents love [Child], and I'm committed to working through the family situation with appropriate professionals. What can we do together to help [Child] feel safe and successful here?"

**Do Not Say:** "This is because of what's happening at the other parent's house." / "My ex is causing all of this." / Any request for the school to take sides or intervene in the custody dispute.

**Follow Up:** Document the school's concerns in writing. Share them with your child's therapist and your attorney. Ask the school to continue monitoring and provide updates.

### Scenario 5: When the School Takes Sides
**Context:** School staff appear to be aligned with the other parent, treating you with suspicion, or limiting your involvement.

**Say (to the principal):** "I want to address a concern I have about my experience with the school. I've felt that my involvement has been limited in ways that are not consistent with my custodial rights. I've experienced [specific examples — not being notified of events, staff seeming uncomfortable when I visit, being told I need the other parent's permission to access records]. I'm not here to create conflict. I'm here to be an involved parent. Can we discuss how to ensure that both parents are treated equally by the school?"

**If the school has received negative information about you from the co-parent:** "I understand that you may have received information from the other parent that has shaped your perception of me. I'd ask that you evaluate me based on your own interactions with me and my involvement with [Child]'s education. I am happy to provide my custody order, which confirms my rights. I'd also welcome the opportunity to volunteer, attend events, and be present — so you can see my parenting firsthand."

**If the school refuses to cooperate:** "I respect your position, but I want to make sure we're clear on the legal requirements. Under FERPA, the school is required to provide both custodial parents with access to educational records. I'd prefer to resolve this collaboratively, but if necessary, I will escalate this concern to the school district's FERPA compliance officer. I hope that won't be necessary — I'd rather we work together for [Child]'s benefit."

**Do Not Say:** "I'll sue the school!" / "You're participating in alienation!" / "I'm going to report you to the school board." (These threats destroy the relationship you need to build.)

**Follow Up:** Document every interaction in writing. If the school does not comply with FERPA, file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Education's Family Policy Compliance Office. Discuss with your attorney whether the school's behavior is relevant to your custody case.

### Scenario 6: Template Letter to School Administrators

**Context:** A formal written request to establish your parental rights at the school. Send via email and certified mail.

**[Your Address]**
**[Your Phone Number]**
**[Your Email]**
**[Date]**

[Principal Name]
[School Name]
[School Address]

**Re: Parental Rights and Communication Request — [Child's Full Name], [Grade/Class]**

Dear [Principal Name],

I am writing to formally establish my rights as a custodial parent of [Child's Full Name], who is currently enrolled in [grade/class] at [School Name]. I have joint legal custody of [Child], as reflected in the attached court order dated [date].

Pursuant to the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), 20 U.S.C. Section 1232g, I am entitled to access all educational records pertaining to my child and to receive all communications provided to parents. I respectfully request the following:

1. That I be added as a parent contact in [Child]'s school records with my current contact information (provided below).
2. That I receive direct copies of all school communications, including but not limited to: report cards, progress reports, newsletters, event invitations, disciplinary notices, Individualized Education Program (IEP) documents, and any other correspondence sent to parents.
3. That I be provided with independent access to the parent portal or any online communication systems used by the school.
4. That I be notified of all parent-teacher conferences and given the opportunity to attend — either jointly or, if preferred, in a separate session.
5. That I be permitted to attend all school events, performances, and activities that are open to parents.

I have attached a certified copy of the custody order for your records. Please note that there is no court order restricting my access to [Child]'s educational information or my right to participate in school activities.

I am committed to being an active, supportive parent in [Child]'s education, and I look forward to working cooperatively with the school. Please confirm receipt of this letter and the actions taken in response. I am happy to meet in person to discuss any questions you may have.

Thank you for your time and your commitment to [Child]'s success.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]

## Key Principles
- **Schools are not your adversary — treat them as partners.** Most school staff want to help. Approach every interaction with the assumption that they are acting in good faith until proven otherwise.
- **Lead with the child's needs, not your grievance.** Frame every request in terms of the child's educational benefit and wellbeing. Schools respond to child-centered parents.
- **Put it in writing.** Verbal agreements are easily forgotten or denied. Follow up every conversation with an email. Keep copies of everything.
- **Know the law, but use it gently.** Citing FERPA is appropriate. Threatening lawsuits is not. Use legal authority as a last resort, not an opening move.
- **Be visible and consistent.** Show up. Volunteer. Attend events. When school staff see you as a present, engaged parent, they are less likely to accept a narrative that you are absent or uninvolved.
