# Alienation Identification Checklist

> **Audience:** Parents, Advocates, Evaluators, Attorneys
> **Purpose:** Provide a printable checklist of the 10 recognized alienating behavior categories with space to document when each was first observed, how frequently it occurs, specific examples, and available evidence.
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-advocate

---

## How to Use This Template

- Review each of the 10 alienating behavior categories below and check those that apply to your situation.
- For each checked category, fill in the documentation columns with factual, date-stamped information.
- This checklist is a starting point for identifying patterns. Use it alongside your incident log, behavior pattern tracker, and master timeline.
- Share this completed checklist with your attorney, GAL, or evaluator to help them understand the scope of the situation.
- Do not self-diagnose alienation. Use this tool to document observable behaviors for professionals to evaluate.

## Tips

- This checklist helps identify patterns, not diagnose alienation. Leave the diagnosis to qualified evaluators. Your job is to document what you observe.
- The more categories that are checked, and the more examples and evidence you can provide, the stronger the picture becomes for professionals.
- Focus on documenting the most clear-cut, well-evidenced examples first. Quality matters more than quantity.
- Print this checklist and fill it out by hand, then store it securely. A handwritten, dated document can serve as contemporaneous evidence.
- Revisit and update this checklist monthly. New behaviors may emerge over time, and existing behaviors may escalate.
