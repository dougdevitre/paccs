# False Allegations in Custody Disputes: Fact Sheet

> **Audience:** Judges, attorneys, guardians ad litem, custody evaluators, targeted parents
> **Purpose:** Provide research-backed information on the prevalence, patterns, and impact of false allegations of abuse in custody disputes, and guidance for distinguishing false from genuine allegations.
> **Related Skill:** paccs-tech-legal-advisory, paccs-tech-research-integrity

---

## The Problem

False allegations of abuse — including child abuse, domestic violence, and sexual abuse — are a recognized tool in high-conflict custody disputes. When used as a tactic to gain advantage in litigation or to sever a child's relationship with the other parent, false allegations cause profound harm to targeted parents, to the children involved, and to the integrity of the systems designed to protect genuine victims.

This fact sheet presents the research, not opinions. Every claim below is sourced from peer-reviewed literature or official data.

---

## Prevalence Data and Statistics

### What the Research Shows

- **False allegations occur at elevated rates in custody disputes.** Trocme and Bala (2005) analyzed Canadian child welfare data and found that intentionally false allegations accounted for approximately 12% of all maltreatment allegations in custody and access disputes, compared to 4% in non-custody contexts.

- **Unsubstantiated rates are high.** Research consistently shows that 50-70% of abuse allegations made during custody disputes are not substantiated by child protective services investigations (Trocme & Bala, 2005; Brown et al., 2000).

- **Sexual abuse allegations in custody disputes have particularly high rates of non-substantiation.** Studies report that sexual abuse allegations arising in the context of custody disputes are unsubstantiated at rates of 50-75%, compared to lower rates in non-custody contexts (Faller, 2005; Bala et al., 2007).

- **The accusing parent is the most common source.** In custody-related false allegations, the allegation most frequently originates from one parent against the other, rather than from the child independently (Bala et al., 2007).

- **False allegations often escalate during critical litigation moments.** Research documents a pattern of allegations emerging or intensifying around custody evaluations, mediation failures, or when one parent seeks increased parenting time (Johnston et al., 2005).

### Important Context

- **Most abuse allegations outside of custody disputes are true.** The elevated false allegation rate in custody disputes does not mean that all abuse allegations are false. It means that the custody context introduces a motive and opportunity for fabrication that must be accounted for in assessment.

- **"Unsubstantiated" does not mean "false."** An unsubstantiated finding means the evidence was insufficient to confirm the allegation. Some unsubstantiated cases involve genuine abuse that could not be proven. However, a pattern of multiple unsubstantiated allegations by the same accuser is a significant data point.

- **False allegations and genuine abuse are not mutually exclusive dynamics.** In some cases, a parent who has genuinely experienced domestic violence may also make false allegations about child abuse. Each allegation must be evaluated on its own evidence.

## How to Distinguish False From Genuine Allegations

No single factor is determinative. Professionals must evaluate the totality of the evidence.

### Indicators That an Allegation May Be False

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Timing correlates with litigation events** | Allegation emerges shortly before or after a custody filing, evaluation, mediation, or hearing |
| **Pattern of multiple unsubstantiated allegations** | The accusing parent has made prior allegations that were investigated and not confirmed |
| **Allegation content uses adult language or legal concepts** | The child describes events using words, framing, or concepts beyond their developmental capacity |
| **Child's account changes based on audience** | Child describes abuse to the accusing parent's network but not to neutral investigators, or account shifts significantly between tellings |
| **No corroborating evidence despite extensive investigation** | Medical exams, forensic interviews, and CPS investigations yield no supporting evidence |
| **Child's affect is inconsistent with the allegation** | Child describes serious abuse without corresponding emotional distress, or shows rehearsed emotional responses |
| **Accusing parent over-coaches the child** | Accusing parent is present during disclosures, prompts the child, or has documented conversations where they suggest details to the child |
| **Allegation is vague and shifts over time** | Initial allegation is vague, then becomes more detailed and specific as the accusing parent adds elements |
| **Accused parent has no prior history** | No history of violence, abuse, criminal behavior, or concerning parenting prior to the custody dispute |
| **Accusing parent has a pattern of alienating behavior** | False allegations are one component of a broader pattern including denigration, access denial, and institutional manipulation |

### Indicators That an Allegation May Be Genuine

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Child's disclosure is spontaneous** | Child discloses without prompting, in a natural context, to a neutral party (teacher, counselor) |
| **Child's account is age-appropriate and consistent** | Details are described in the child's own language, are developmentally consistent, and remain stable across multiple tellings |
| **Corroborating evidence exists** | Medical findings, physical evidence, eyewitness accounts, or documented behavioral changes consistent with the reported abuse |
| **Accused parent has a relevant history** | Prior reports, criminal history, documented anger issues, substance abuse, or prior CPS involvement |
| **Child shows trauma-consistent symptoms** | Behavioral changes, nightmares, regression, anxiety, or avoidance behaviors that align with the type of abuse alleged |
| **Child retains some positive feelings toward the accused parent** | Genuine victims often have ambivalent feelings, not pure hatred (which is more characteristic of alienation) |
| **No pattern of false reporting by the accusing parent** | The allegation is the first, and the accusing parent has not used allegations as a litigation strategy |

## Impact of False Allegations

### On Children

- **Psychological harm from unnecessary investigations.** Children subjected to forensic interviews, medical examinations, and CPS investigations experience stress, confusion, and fear — regardless of whether the allegation is true (Poole & Lamb, 1998).
- **Disruption of the parental relationship.** False allegations typically result in reduced or suspended contact with the accused parent during investigation, deepening alienation (Bernet, 2010).
- **Internalized false narrative.** Children who are repeatedly told a parent harmed them may develop false memories or genuine fear based on the implanted narrative, even when no abuse occurred (Ceci & Bruck, 1995).
- **Loss of trust in protective systems.** When a child is used as a tool in false allegations, they learn that the systems meant to protect them can be manipulated — undermining their trust in institutions.
- **Long-term psychological effects.** Baker (2005) documented that adults who were alienated as children — often through campaigns that included false allegations — reported depression, low self-esteem, substance abuse, difficulty with trust, and damaged relationships in adulthood.

### On Targeted Parents

- **Presumption of guilt.** Even when allegations are unsubstantiated, the targeted parent often faces lasting stigma. The accusation becomes part of the court record and may influence future proceedings.
- **Loss of parenting time.** Courts often suspend or restrict contact during investigations. In alienation cases, this plays directly into the alienating parent's strategy.
- **Financial devastation.** Defending against false allegations requires attorneys, evaluators, and expert witnesses, often costing tens of thousands of dollars.
- **Psychological trauma.** Being falsely accused of harming your own child is profoundly damaging. Targeted parents report PTSD symptoms, depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation (Harman et al., 2018).
- **Professional and social consequences.** False allegations can damage careers, reputations, and relationships, even when disproven.

### On the Justice System

- **Resource diversion.** False allegations consume investigative, judicial, and therapeutic resources that should be directed at genuine cases of child abuse.
- **Erosion of credibility.** When false allegations are common in custody disputes, genuine allegations may be treated with inappropriate skepticism, potentially leaving real victims unprotected.
- **Delayed resolution.** Each false allegation adds months or years to custody proceedings, prolonging instability for the child.

## Common Patterns in False Allegation Cases

### The Escalation Pattern

False allegations in alienation cases frequently follow a predictable escalation:

1. **Denigration.** The alienating parent makes negative comments about the targeted parent to the child and to third parties.
2. **Access denial.** Parenting time is interfered with through scheduling "conflicts," illness claims, or the child's stated refusal.
3. **Minor allegations.** Initial allegations are relatively minor: "the child came home hungry," "the house was dirty," "the child was unsupervised."
4. **Institutional involvement.** The alienating parent contacts CPS, schools, or the child's therapist with concerns, creating a paper trail.
5. **Serious allegations.** When minor tactics do not achieve the desired custody outcome, allegations escalate to physical abuse, sexual abuse, or domestic violence.
6. **Multiple investigations.** Each allegation triggers a new investigation, further disrupting the targeted parent's contact and creating the appearance of a pattern of reported concerns.

### The "Concerned Parent" Presentation

The alienating parent often presents as a protective, concerned parent who is reluctantly reporting out of fear for the child. This presentation can be convincing to professionals who do not look at the broader pattern. Key features:

- Claims to have tried to resolve things privately before reporting
- Expresses reluctance about making the allegation ("I did not want to believe it")
- Frames the allegation as the child's disclosure, not their own suspicion
- Appears cooperative and reasonable in initial interviews
- The mask slips when the allegation is not substantiated and new allegations emerge

### The Documentation Weaponization Pattern

- The alienating parent keeps meticulous records of the targeted parent's perceived failures while omitting context.
- Normal parenting moments (a raised voice, a child returning with a minor scrape, a bedtime disagreement) are reframed as evidence of harm.
- The alienating parent presents this "documentation" to therapists, evaluators, and the court as evidence of a pattern.
- Without the targeted parent's perspective, the one-sided record can appear concerning.

## What Courts Should Know

### 1. Every allegation deserves investigation, but not every allegation deserves deference.

Investigate thoroughly. Protect children. But also recognize that false allegations are a documented litigation tactic. Evaluate the evidence, the context, and the pattern — not just the accusation.

### 2. The pattern is the evidence.

A single allegation is a data point. Multiple unsubstantiated allegations by the same accuser, particularly when they escalate at key litigation moments, constitute a pattern that is itself evidence of alienation and litigation abuse.

### 3. Suspending contact during investigation plays into the alienating parent's strategy.

When contact is automatically suspended upon an allegation, the accusing parent has learned that allegations produce the result they want. Consider supervised contact rather than no contact during investigations.

### 4. Professionals must be qualified to assess for both abuse and alienation.

An evaluator who can assess for abuse but not alienation (or vice versa) is insufficient. The differential diagnosis requires competence in both dynamics.

### 5. Children can be coached.

Research on children's suggestibility (Ceci & Bruck, 1995) demonstrates that children — particularly young children — can be influenced to report events that did not occur, especially when a trusted authority figure (parent) repeatedly suggests or implies those events.

### 6. False allegations should have consequences.

When an allegation is determined to be fabricated (not merely unsubstantiated, but intentionally false), the court should consider this as evidence of alienation and as a factor in custody determinations. Without consequences, false allegations remain a cost-free tactic.
